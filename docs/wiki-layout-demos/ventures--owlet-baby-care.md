# Owlet Baby Care

**Status:** Draft
**Confidence:** High
**Focus:** Consumer health, infant monitoring
**Stage:** Public (NYSE: OWLT)
**Location:** Lehi, UT
**Updated:** 2026-05-09

## Summary

Owlet makes the Smart Sock, a wearable infant pulse-oximeter that streams heart rate and oxygen data to a parent's phone. Founded in 2013 out of BYU, took the company public via SPAC in 2021, then spent two years rebuilding regulatory relationships after the FDA flagged the product as an unauthorized medical device.

## Impact

Owlet is the second Utah consumer-health company to go public in the post-Recursion era. Its FDA stumble is now the canonical Utah case study in consumer-vs-medical-device positioning — important for any founder building a sensor product for parents, athletes, or patients.

## What They Are Building

The Smart Sock 3, a re-cleared FDA-authorized version, plus subscription-software extensions: BabySat (prescription-grade home pulse oximetry), Dream Sock (infant sleep), and Cam (video monitoring with sound alerts). The post-FDA strategy is "two products" — a wellness product and a regulated medical device — sold side by side.

## What They Need Now

Continued regulatory clarity, especially as competitors like Nanit move into adjacent monitoring categories. Distribution muscle in pediatric-clinic channels. A path back to growth that doesn't depend on the SPAC-era valuation expectations.

## Who Could Help

- Pediatric-clinic operators who can advise on BabySat distribution.
- Regulatory consultants experienced with Class II medical-device clearance.
- Senior commercial leaders with experience in SPAC-recovery situations.

## Utah Context

Owlet is BYU-grown — founded by Kurt Workman, Zack Bomsta, Jordan Monroe, and Jacob Colvin out of an entrepreneurship class. The company stayed in Utah even after the SPAC; HQ is in Lehi. The cultural model (consumer-friendly, family-focused, plain-speaking) is recognizably Utah.
