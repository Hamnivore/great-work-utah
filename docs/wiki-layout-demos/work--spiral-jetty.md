## Summary

Robert Smithson's 1,500-foot spiral of basalt and earth, built into the northeast shore of Great Salt Lake at Rozel Point in April 1970. The work is permanent in the way a piece of weather is permanent: visible some years, submerged others, rimmed with salt almost always.

## Impact

A foundational work of postwar Land art and one of the few canonical pieces of 20th-century American art that lives in Utah. Its disappearances and reappearances rewrote what *permanent* means for a public artwork.

## Why It Mattered

Smithson chose Rozel Point because of the failed oil drilling, the pink algae, the salt cycles — a place that already wanted to disappear. The Jetty doesn't fight the lake. It *uses* the lake's indifference as part of the work.

## Utah Context

The site is on state-leased land. The Holt/Smithson Foundation manages the work alongside the Dia Art Foundation. Access is by unmarked gravel road; the drive in is part of the experience.

## People and Institutions

- **Robert Smithson** — artist; died in 1973 surveying a related project in Texas.
- **Nancy Holt** — Smithson's partner and primary documentarian. Photographs and 16mm films are most of what we have of the original.
- **Holt/Smithson Foundation** — current stewards.
- **Dia Art Foundation** — co-stewards since 1999.
