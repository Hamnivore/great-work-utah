# Atmospheric Waves Experiment

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a real NASA science mission with Utah-built flight hardware and USU-led science, but its historical significance depends on what the data ultimately teaches space-weather and upper-atmosphere science
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion / Environment & Earth
**Type:** NASA instrument / space-weather science mission
**Era:** 2023-present
**Location:** North Logan and Logan, UT; International Space Station

## What it is

NASA's Atmospheric Waves Experiment, or AWE, is an ISS-mounted instrument built by Utah State University's Space Dynamics Laboratory to study atmospheric gravity waves through airglow in Earth's upper atmosphere. The mission looks down from the space station at nighttime infrared airglow, using a four-camera Advanced Mesospheric Temperature Mapper to infer temperature structures and wave motion near the mesopause.

The science team is led from Utah State University. Michael Taylor, a USU physicist, originated and led the mission before retiring; Ludger Scherliess, also at USU, became principal investigator as the mission moved into operations.

## Why this might become great work

Terrestrial weather and space weather are usually treated as different worlds. AWE is aimed at the boundary between them: how storms, mountains, convection, and lower-atmosphere dynamics send gravity waves upward, disturbing the ionosphere and affecting satellite communication, navigation, and tracking.

If the dataset materially improves upper-atmosphere models or space-weather forecasting, AWE could become one of the cleanest recent examples of Utah-built space instrumentation changing a scientific field.

## Signals so far

- NASA describes AWE as the first mission of its kind to study how atmospheric gravity waves influence the upper atmosphere from the ISS
- The instrument was built, tested, and operated through USU's Space Dynamics Laboratory in North Logan
- AWE launched to the ISS in November 2023 and achieved first light shortly after installation
- In March 2025, NASA and USU released data from the first 3,000 orbits, including more than five million images and derived temperature/airglow products
- USU's principal investigator described the data as a never-before-captured view of atmospheric gravity waves

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed AWE results that change models of gravity-wave propagation or ionosphere-thermosphere-mesosphere coupling
- Operational use of AWE-derived insights in space-weather forecasting, satellite operations, navigation, or communication-risk models
- Follow-on missions or instruments that adopt the AWE measurement architecture
- Public datasets becoming a standard benchmark for upper-atmosphere dynamics

## Caveats

- The instrument is operating and the data release is real, but scientific impact takes years to assess
- AWE is a NASA mission with many national partners; Utah's role is instrument build, mission operations, and major science leadership, not sole ownership
- This may remain best understood as one notable mission within the broader A-tier Space Dynamics Laboratory story unless the scientific results become especially influential
