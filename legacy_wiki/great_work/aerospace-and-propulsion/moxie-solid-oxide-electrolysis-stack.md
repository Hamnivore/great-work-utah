# MOXIE Solid Oxide Electrolysis Stack

**Tier:** B
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space technology demonstration / in-situ resource utilization subsystem
**Era:** 2010s-2023 Mars operations; Earth-scale follow-through ongoing
**Location:** North Salt Lake, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - OxEon Energy in North Salt Lake designed, developed, and manufactured the solid oxide electrolysis stack at the core of MOXIE's oxygen-production system, while MIT led the instrument and JPL managed the Mars 2020 project.

## What it was

MOXIE, the Mars Oxygen In-Situ Resource Utilization Experiment, was the toaster-sized Perseverance rover payload that made oxygen from the carbon-dioxide atmosphere of Mars. The specific Utah work was the Solid OXide Electrolyzer stack: the high-temperature electrochemical core that split compressed Martian CO2 into oxygen ions and carbon monoxide, then recombined the oxygen ions into molecular oxygen.

OxEon's team brought decades of solid oxide fuel-cell and electrolysis experience to the flight stack, qualifying it for launch vibration, landing shock, pressure, thermal cycling, power, mass, and volume limits. MOXIE first produced oxygen on Mars on April 20, 2021, and completed its final oxygen run in August 2023 after 16 total runs.

## Why it mattered

MOXIE was not about the few grams of oxygen it made for immediate use. It proved that future explorers might manufacture oxygen on Mars rather than launching all of it from Earth. That matters because oxygen is not only breathable air; it is the oxidizer a Mars ascent vehicle would need in tens of tons to get astronauts home.

Without the MOXIE demonstration, Mars in-situ resource utilization would still have been mostly a paper architecture and laboratory argument. The Utah-built stack helped move it into the category of hardware that survived interplanetary flight, operated on Mars, and produced measured oxygen under real Martian atmospheric conditions.

## The hard problem they solved

Solid oxide electrolysis is hot, power-hungry, materials-sensitive work even in a lab. MOXIE had to do it after a launch, cruise, landing, and long surface deployment, using thin, dusty, variable Martian air and a rover power budget.

The stack had to maintain gas seals and electrochemical performance while cycling to high operating temperatures, reject carbon monoxide and unused carbon dioxide, meet oxygen purity requirements, and fit inside a mass- and volume-constrained flight payload. It also had to operate intermittently across Martian seasons and times of day rather than in a controlled industrial plant.

## Lasting impact

NASA reported that MOXIE extracted oxygen 16 times, generated 122 grams total, reached 12 grams per hour at its most efficient, and achieved 98% purity or better. The 2022 Science Advances paper described MOXIE as the first demonstration of in-situ resource utilization on another planet.

The stack's legacy is twofold. In space exploration, it gives mission planners real data for scaled oxygen plants that could support Mars ascent propellant and life support. On Earth, OxEon has used the same solid oxide electrolysis lineage in DOE-backed work on hydrogen, syngas, sustainable fuels, and higher-volume SOEC manufacturing.

## Caveats

MOXIE was an MIT-led instrument on a NASA/JPL rover, not a Utah-led mission. The fair Utah claim is the solid oxide electrolysis stack and associated flight-qualified electrochemical hardware, not the whole Perseverance mission or all MOXIE science operations.

The demonstration was also intentionally tiny. A human Mars mission would need a system roughly hundreds of times larger, operating continuously and autonomously for more than a year. MOXIE proved feasibility and produced engineering data; it did not by itself solve scaled Mars propellant production.
