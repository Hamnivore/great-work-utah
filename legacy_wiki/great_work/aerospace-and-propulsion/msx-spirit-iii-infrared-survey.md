# MSX / SPIRIT III Infrared Survey Instrumentation

**Tier:** B - dual-use missile-defense instrumentation that also produced a durable mid-infrared astronomy dataset, with Utah contributing core SPIRIT III instrument subsystems
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space instrument / defense experiment / infrared astronomy dataset
**Era:** 1990s; launched 1996; main infrared mission 1996-1997
**Location:** North Logan, UT

## What it was

The Midcourse Space Experiment was a Ballistic Missile Defense Organization satellite launched in 1996 to demonstrate multispectral sensing technologies for detecting and tracking ballistic missiles in flight. Its main cryogenic infrared payload, SPIRIT III, also surveyed the mid-infrared sky.

The Utah contribution was in the instrument hardware. Space Dynamics Laboratory at Utah State University built the SPIRIT III multi-color radiometer and Michelson interferometer that were integrated with the cryogenic telescope. NASA/IPAC's Infrared Science Archive now hosts MSX images and catalogs, including point-source catalogs and Galactic Plane survey data.

## Why it mattered

MSX was not just a military technology demonstration. Its infrared observations filled important gaps left by IRAS, especially in the Galactic Plane where confusion and structured emission made earlier mid-infrared surveys harder to use. The MSX Point Source Catalog and images became standard archival resources for astronomers studying star-forming regions, evolved stars, infrared dark clouds, the Large Magellanic Cloud, and the mid-infrared structure of the Milky Way.

The military side mattered too, though in a more morally complicated way. Space-based infrared background characterization is central to missile warning, missile defense, and target discrimination. MSX helped define the backgrounds against which missiles and other objects must be detected.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was making a cryogenic infrared sensing payload useful for both precision defense experiments and astronomy. SPIRIT III needed low-stray-light optics, cooled detectors, multi-band radiometry, interferometric spectral information, calibration, and high-quality scan data from orbit.

For astronomy, the challenge was mapping crowded, structured mid-infrared regions with enough resolution and calibration to produce catalogs rather than only images. For missile-defense work, the challenge was understanding targets and backgrounds in realistic space-viewing conditions.

## Lasting impact

- MSX data remain available through NASA/IPAC IRSA and HEASARC as archival mid-infrared survey products
- The Point Source Catalog includes Galactic Plane sources, IRAS gap coverage, and high-latitude regions such as the Large Magellanic Cloud
- MSX helped bridge the gap between IRAS and later infrared missions such as Spitzer, WISE, and JWST-era archival work
- The program strengthened SDL's experience in space-based infrared sensing, calibration, and defense-related remote sensing

## Key people

- **Space Dynamics Laboratory SPIRIT III teams** - built the multi-color radiometer and Michelson interferometer portions of the infrared payload
- **Andrew Mastandrea, Richard Glasheen, and James Guregian** - documented the SPIRIT III telescope development and its integration with SDL-built subsystems
- **Air Force Research Laboratory / Ballistic Missile Defense Organization teams** - mission sponsorship, data processing, and defense experiment context
- **Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory** - spacecraft and mission integration role

## Caveats

- MSX was primarily a missile-defense experiment, and much of its defense value is classified or hard to assess from public sources.
- SDL did not build the entire satellite or entire SPIRIT III telescope; the defensible Utah claim is for key radiometer/interferometer subsystems and related infrared-instrument expertise.
- The astronomy dataset is valuable, but later missions with greater sensitivity and coverage have surpassed MSX for many uses.
- The entry belongs partly in defense, astronomy, and aerospace; it is placed here because the Utah work was space instrumentation.
