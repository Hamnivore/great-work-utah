# NEO Surveyor Planetary-Defense Telescope

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a major NASA planetary-defense mission with critical instrument integration and testing in Utah, but it has not launched yet
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space telescope / planetary-defense mission / instrument integration
**Era:** 2025-present; planned launch no earlier than 2027
**Location:** Space Dynamics Laboratory, Logan, UT; NASA/JPL-led national mission

## What it is

NEO Surveyor is NASA's first space telescope built specifically to find potentially hazardous near-Earth asteroids and comets. It uses a 50-centimeter infrared telescope to detect objects that are hard for visible-light ground surveys to see: dark asteroids, objects approaching from near the Sun's direction, and bodies that reveal themselves better by heat than by reflected sunlight.

The Utah contribution is the instrument-integration work at Utah State University's Space Dynamics Laboratory in Logan. NASA and JPL report that the spacecraft's telescope and instrument enclosure are undergoing integration and testing at SDL, including the assembly needed to keep the infrared telescope thermally isolated and cold enough to detect faint heat signatures.

## Why this might become great work

Planetary defense starts with detection. Humanity cannot deflect or prepare for an asteroid it does not know exists. Congress directed NASA to find most near-Earth objects larger than 140 meters, but ground surveys miss many of the objects that are dark, sunward, or otherwise optically inconvenient.

If NEO Surveyor launches and performs as designed, it could become a core piece of the global asteroid-warning system. Utah's role would be one more example of SDL doing difficult space-sensing work where instrument build, thermal control, and operational reliability matter as much as the mission idea.

## Signals so far

- NASA describes NEO Surveyor as the first space telescope specifically designed to detect potentially hazardous asteroids and comets
- The mission passed critical design review in 2025
- In 2025, the telescope and instrument enclosure moved to Space Dynamics Laboratory in Logan for integration and testing
- NASA's May 5, 2026 update showed engineers attaching the aluminum telescope to its flight base frame at SDL
- The mission is planned to launch no earlier than September 2027 and perform a multi-year infrared survey from the Sun-Earth L1 region

## What would upgrade this

- Successful launch, deployment, and commissioning
- Survey operations that discover or characterize large numbers of previously missed near-Earth objects
- Measurable progress toward the congressional goal of finding most 140-meter-plus near-Earth objects
- Use of NEO Surveyor data in real planetary-defense decisions, impact-risk reduction, or follow-up missions
- Public recognition that SDL's integration and thermal-control work was essential to mission performance

## Caveats

- NEO Surveyor is a national NASA/JPL mission led by UCLA scientist Amy Mainzer, not a Utah-originated mission
- The spacecraft has not launched; integration success is not mission success
- Planetary-defense impact depends on survey performance, data processing, follow-up observations, and policy action after detection
- This may remain part of the broader Space Dynamics Laboratory story unless the mission becomes especially consequential
