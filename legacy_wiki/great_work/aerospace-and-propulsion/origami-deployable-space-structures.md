# Origami Deployable Space Structures

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a distinctive Utah research program with NASA and Air Force relevance; upgrade depends on flight hardware or broad engineering adoption
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Deployable structures / compliant mechanisms
**Era:** 2010s-present
**Location:** Provo, UT

## What it is

BYU's compliant mechanisms and origami engineering work adapts folding patterns into deployable aerospace structures: solar arrays, antennas, reflectarrays, lidar/telescope apertures, and other systems that need to launch compactly and unfold reliably in space. The key Utah work is not "origami is neat"; it is converting paper-like folding into thick, manufacturable, load-bearing engineering mechanisms with compliant joints, magnets, living hinges, and deployment paths that can survive aerospace constraints.

The program sits at the intersection of mechanism design, materials, space packaging, and manufacturable fold patterns.

## Why this might become great work

Space systems are often constrained by launch volume. A larger antenna, telescope aperture, or solar array can mean better communication, sensing, power generation, or science return, but it has to fit inside a rocket first. Deployable origami-inspired structures offer a way to make large things small during launch and predictable after deployment.

If these patterns become standard in space hardware, BYU's work could be part of the design language for future satellites and space telescopes.

## Signals so far

- BYU collaborated with NASA/JPL on origami-inspired solar array prototypes
- NASA published images of BYU/JPL folding solar-array work in 2014
- BYU's Compliant Mechanisms and Robotics Lab has developed origami-inspired antennas, telescope apertures, magnetic stabilization, living hinges, and thick-origami methods
- The work has received NASA and Air Force Research Laboratory support
- BYU researchers continue publishing new fold families and deployable-structure methods, including recent "bloom pattern" work

## What would upgrade this

- BYU-originated pattern or mechanism used in flight hardware
- Adoption by NASA, DoD, or commercial satellite programs as a standard deployable architecture
- Demonstrated reliability through vibration, thermal, vacuum, and deployment testing at flight-relevant scale
- Clear evidence that the approach enables an aperture, antenna, or array that would otherwise be impractical

## Caveats

- Many deployable structure concepts die between elegant prototype and qualified flight hardware
- NASA/JPL and other collaborators are part of the story; attribution should stay specific to the Utah work
- The impact may be diffuse, as design methods are adopted into many mechanisms rather than one famous mission
