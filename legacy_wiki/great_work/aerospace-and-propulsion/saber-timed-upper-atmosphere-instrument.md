# SABER / TIMED Upper-Atmosphere Instrument

**Tier:** A - a Utah-built infrared limb sounder that turned a planned two-year NASA instrument into a multi-decade reference dataset for the mesosphere and lower thermosphere
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space science instrument / atmospheric remote-sensing dataset
**Era:** 1990s design; launched 2001; operational into the 2020s
**Location:** North Logan, UT

## What it was

SABER, the Sounding of the Atmosphere using Broadband Emission Radiometry instrument, is a 10-channel infrared radiometer flying on NASA's TIMED satellite. It measures limb emissions from Earth's upper atmosphere to retrieve temperature, pressure, density, ozone, water vapor, carbon dioxide, nitric oxide, infrared cooling rates, and related quantities in the stratosphere, mesosphere, and lower thermosphere.

The Utah work was the instrument itself. Space Dynamics Laboratory at Utah State University designed, fabricated, calibrated, and helped sustain SABER in partnership with NASA Langley Research Center, Hampton University, and Global Atmospheric Technologies and Sciences. TIMED launched in December 2001 with a nominal two-year mission. SABER was still operating more than twenty years later.

## Why it mattered

The mesosphere and lower thermosphere are awkward to study: too high for ordinary balloons, too low and diffuse for many spacecraft, and strongly affected by both space weather from above and climate and waves from below. Before long-duration satellite records like TIMED/SABER, this region was much less observed than the troposphere and stratosphere.

SABER made the upper atmosphere measurable at global scale for decades rather than campaigns. NASA's 20-year retrospective says SABER data supported roughly 2,100 journal articles by 2021, and a 2023 instrument paper reports nearly 2,200 peer-reviewed papers using SABER data. The record has been used to study carbon dioxide increases and cooling in the upper atmosphere, El Nino effects reaching near-space altitudes, rocket and shuttle exhaust water vapor, nitric-oxide infrared cooling during space-weather events, ozone and temperature structure, gravity waves, tides, and long-term coupling between the lower atmosphere and geospace.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard engineering problem was sustained, calibrated infrared limb sounding from orbit. SABER had to observe faint emissions through Earth's atmospheric edge across many spectral channels, reject stray light, scan reliably, keep detectors cold, and maintain radiometric stability over years of thermal cycling and space radiation.

The hard scientific problem was continuity. A short mission could answer a few targeted questions; a multi-decade instrument can see solar-cycle behavior, seasonal and interannual variability, volcanic and fire aerosol events, space-weather storms, launch plumes, and climate-scale trends in the same measurement system.

## Lasting impact

- SABER produced one of the major long-duration global datasets for the mesosphere and lower thermosphere
- The instrument helped make upper-atmosphere radiative cooling and carbon dioxide trends observable rather than only modeled
- Its data record became a baseline for studying how terrestrial weather, climate, and space weather interact at the edge of space
- The mission strengthened SDL's reputation for durable calibrated space instruments, feeding the broader Utah State / SDL aerospace ecosystem

## Caveats

- SABER was a partnership; the mission, spacecraft, science leadership, and data interpretation were not solely Utah work.
- The entry is about the SDL-built instrument and its data record, not the entire TIMED mission.
- A large paper count is a proxy for scientific value, not the same thing as direct policy or commercial impact.
- Much of the scientific interpretation came from NASA, Hampton, GATS, and international atmospheric-science teams using the Utah-built instrument.
