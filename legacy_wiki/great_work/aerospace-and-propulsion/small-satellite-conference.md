# Small Satellite Conference

**Tier:** A — the premier global convening for small satellites; an ecosystem builder rather than a single invention
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Conference / technical community
**Era:** 1987–present
**Location:** Logan, UT

## What it is

The Small Satellite Conference began at Utah State University in Logan in 1987, when small satellites were still a fringe idea in serious aerospace circles. Faculty including Frank Redd, Gil Moore, and Rex McGill created a technical meeting where university, government, and industry teams could share mission designs, subsystem lessons, and hard-won experience building spacecraft at smaller scale and lower cost.

What started as a small university gathering became one of the central institutions of the smallsat world. For decades, Logan has been the annual place where CubeSat teams, defense organizations, commercial constellation builders, launch providers, payload developers, and university labs compare notes.

## Why it mattered

Small satellites changed who could participate in space. They lowered the cost of flight experiments, made university spacecraft programs viable, gave startups a path into orbital hardware, and later became the backbone of commercial Earth-observation and communications constellations.

The conference did not invent the CubeSat or build every important smallsat, but it helped make the field legible and cumulative. In emerging engineering communities, the meeting place matters: papers, hallway conversations, student competitions, vendor tables, and mission postmortems all compound into a shared body of practice.

## The hard problem they solved

The technical problem was making useful spacecraft with less mass, less money, less schedule, and smaller teams. The social problem was persuading a conservative aerospace field that small spacecraft were not toys. The Logan conference gave the community a durable forum before the market fully existed.

## Lasting impact

- The conference proceedings form a long-running technical archive for smallsat design and mission lessons
- USU and Space Dynamics Laboratory became permanent nodes in the smallsat ecosystem
- The Frank J. Redd student competition helped train generations of aerospace engineers
- The conference helped normalize small satellites as serious platforms for science, defense, communications, and commercial Earth observation

## Key people

- **Frank J. Redd** — USU engineering professor and founding figure; namesake of the student competition
- **Gil Moore** — USU engineering faculty member involved in creating the conference
- **Rex McGill** — USU engineering faculty member involved in creating the conference
- **Space Dynamics Laboratory / Utah State University** — institutional home and long-running organizer

## Caveats

- This is an ecosystem entry, not a claim that all smallsat breakthroughs happened in Utah
- The conference's impact is diffuse and hard to measure compared with a single spacecraft or invention
- Small satellites also accelerated military surveillance and commercial sensing capabilities with complicated policy implications
