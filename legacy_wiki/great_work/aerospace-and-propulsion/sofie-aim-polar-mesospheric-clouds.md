# SOFIE / AIM Polar Mesospheric Cloud Instrument

**Tier:** B - a Utah-built NASA instrument that helped turn night-shining clouds from rare visual phenomena into a measured upper-atmosphere climate and chemistry record
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space science instrument / atmospheric remote sensing
**Era:** 2000s design; launched 2007; mission ended 2023
**Location:** North Logan, UT

## What it was

SOFIE, the Solar Occultation for Ice Experiment, was one of the primary instruments on NASA's Aeronomy of Ice in the Mesosphere mission. AIM was the first satellite mission dedicated to polar mesospheric clouds, also called noctilucent or night-shining clouds: high, thin ice clouds that form near the edge of space roughly 50 miles above Earth.

Space Dynamics Laboratory at Utah State University designed, built, delivered, tested, and calibrated SOFIE. The instrument used solar occultation, watching sunlight pass through the limb of Earth's atmosphere at sunrise and sunset from orbit. Its 16 spectral bands measured cloud particles, temperature, ozone, water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitric oxide, and related properties of the environment where polar mesospheric clouds form.

## Why it mattered

Polar mesospheric clouds are scientifically useful because they sit in a sensitive part of the atmosphere. Their formation depends on temperature, water vapor, methane oxidation, meteoric dust, gravity waves, atmospheric circulation, and possibly long-term climate change. Before AIM, scientists had observations from ground sightings, sounding rockets, and earlier satellites, but not a dedicated mission built to watch the clouds and their environment globally over many seasons.

SOFIE was one of the instruments that made that possible. NASA's AIM retrospectives credit the mission with the first global-scale, full-season views of these clouds, better understanding of cloud formation and variability, observations of atmospheric waves and radar echoes, and evidence that rocket launches can create night-shining clouds away from polar regions.

## The hard problem they solved

The instrument problem was precision solar occultation from a small spacecraft. SOFIE needed to keep a stable line of sight on the sun through the thin atmospheric limb, measure small differential absorption signals across ultraviolet through infrared bands, and retrieve vertical profiles accurately enough to distinguish gases, ice particles, and temperature structure.

The mission problem was context. Cloud images alone show where the clouds are; SOFIE helped measure the recipe around them: the gases, particles, and thermal conditions that allow ice clouds to appear in an extremely cold, dry, high-altitude region.

## Lasting impact

- SOFIE contributed to the AIM mission's long record of polar mesospheric cloud seasons from 2007 to 2023
- Its measurements supported studies of cloud particle composition, ozone, water vapor, temperature, nitric oxide, meteoric smoke, and upper-atmosphere chemistry
- AIM data helped show that mesospheric cloud behavior is tied to atmospheric waves and broader geospace coupling, not just local ice microphysics
- The instrument strengthened SDL's portfolio of atmospheric remote-sensing hardware alongside SABER, AWE, and other Utah-built space instruments

## Caveats

- SOFIE was one instrument on a broader NASA mission; the satellite, mission management, other instruments, and much of the science analysis were led outside Utah.
- AIM's cloud imaging instrument CIPS also produced many of the mission's most visually distinctive and widely cited cloud products.
- The work is scientifically important but more specialized than broad infrastructure such as SDL itself or the WISE all-sky survey payload.
