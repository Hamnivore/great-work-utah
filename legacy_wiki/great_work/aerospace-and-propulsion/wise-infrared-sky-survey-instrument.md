# WISE Infrared Sky Survey Instrument

**Tier:** A
**Domain:** Aerospace & Propulsion
**Type:** Space science instrument / infrared telescope payload
**Era:** 2004-2011; extended NEOWISE use through 2024
**Location:** Logan / North Logan, UT

## What it was

NASA's Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer, or WISE, was an all-sky infrared survey mission launched in 2009. The Utah-built part was the science instrument: a cryogenically cooled infrared telescope and imaging payload designed, fabricated, tested, and assembled by Space Dynamics Laboratory in Logan / North Logan.

The instrument used a 40-centimeter cryogenic telescope, four infrared detector arrays, a scan mirror that stabilized the field during exposures, and a solid-hydrogen cryostat that cooled the telescope and detectors to very low temperatures. It produced a sensitive infrared map of the entire sky, then continued as NEOWISE for asteroid and near-Earth-object work after the cryogen was exhausted.

## Why it mattered

Visible-light surveys miss cold, dusty, faint, or obscured objects. WISE gave astronomy a uniform infrared map of the sky, revealing brown dwarfs, dusty galaxies, star-forming regions, asteroids, comets, and near-Earth objects. It became one of those infrastructure datasets that scientists keep reusing long after the original mission.

Utah's contribution mattered because the survey depended on a space-qualified infrared payload that could keep detectors cold, scan continuously, hold image quality, and survive launch and operations. The science leadership and data processing were elsewhere, but the enabling instrument was Utah-built.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was building a cryogenic, wide-field infrared survey payload that could image continuously from orbit. The instrument had to cover multiple infrared bands, maintain optical quality and thermal stability, control scan-induced blur, and collect uniform data across the sky during a short cryogenic mission.

SDL's payload design used a cryogenic telescope and reimaging optics, a scan mirror, four large infrared focal-plane arrays, and a two-stage solid-hydrogen cryostat. This was not just a camera bolted to a spacecraft; it was a tightly integrated thermal, optical, detector, electronics, and test problem.

## Lasting impact

WISE produced a full-sky infrared atlas and source catalogs that became standard references for astronomy. NEOWISE extended the mission's impact by repeatedly surveying asteroids and comets, including near-Earth objects relevant to planetary defense.

The project also strengthened SDL's position as a serious builder of NASA infrared science instruments, a lineage visible later in Utah-built or Utah-tested instruments such as AWE and NEO Surveyor.

## Caveats

This should not be claimed as a Utah-led astronomy mission. WISE was a NASA Explorer mission managed by JPL, scientifically led from UCLA, with data processing at IPAC/Caltech and spacecraft work at Ball Aerospace.

The Utah claim is narrower and still important: SDL built the science instrument. The entry belongs here because the instrument was an enabling artifact of a major sky survey, but attribution should stay attached to the payload, not the whole mission.
