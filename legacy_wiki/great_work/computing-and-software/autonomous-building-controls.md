# Autonomous Building Controls

**Speculative tier:** P-B - ambitious physical AI for infrastructure, with strong funding and product signals but limited public proof of broad deployment
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Building automation / digital twin platform
**Era:** 2016-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Autonomous building controls are the attempt to replace hand-coded, reactive building automation with physics-informed digital twins and edge AI. PassiveLogic is the main Utah example: its Hive platform, Quantum digital twins, sensors, and software tools are meant to let buildings model themselves, commission themselves, and make real-time control decisions across HVAC and other systems.

This belongs in computing, not just construction tech, because the hard part is a general software abstraction for physical control. Buildings are messy cyber-physical systems: old equipment, multiple protocols, changing occupancy, weather, comfort constraints, energy pricing, and maintenance drift.

## Why this might become great work

Buildings use a large share of global energy, but their control systems are often brittle and bespoke. If a general autonomous-control platform can be installed at ordinary building scale, the climate and productivity effects could be large: lower energy use, better comfort, faster commissioning, and less dependence on scarce controls specialists.

The interesting possibility is a control stack for the built world that feels more like modern software: model the system, simulate futures, deploy decisions locally, and keep adapting as the building changes.

## Signals so far

- PassiveLogic was founded in Salt Lake City in 2016
- The company received a U.S. Department of Energy contract in 2020 related to digital-twin technology for next-generation automation systems
- PassiveLogic has built a full product stack around Hive controllers, Quantum digital twins, Sense sensors, and mobile/building-authoring workflows
- In 2025, it raised a $74 million Series C, bringing reported total funding to more than $125 million
- Strategic investors include Johnson Controls, Prologis Ventures, Brookfield, and NVentures, which is a useful signal because they touch buildings, infrastructure, or edge compute

## What would upgrade this

- Public, independently measured energy and comfort results across a large building portfolio
- Durable deployments in ordinary retrofits, not only showcase projects
- Adoption by major controls contractors, owners, or facilities teams
- Evidence that the platform reduces commissioning labor and long-term controls drift

## Caveats

- Building automation is full of graveyards where elegant software met messy field reality
- Energy-savings claims can depend heavily on baseline conditions, weather normalization, and occupant behavior
- Public information is still mostly company-provided and investor-facing
- A good platform may still struggle if installation channels, procurement, and facilities workflows do not change
