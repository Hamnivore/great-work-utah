# Biohybrid Neural-Tissue Computing

**Speculative tier:** P-C - strange and potentially important, but still very early and mostly company-reported
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Biocomputation / neuromorphic computing / synthetic biology
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Biohybrid neural-tissue computing is the attempt to use living neurons as part of a computing system. The Utah example is Intactis Bio, a Salt Lake City company working on engineered neural tissue connected to silicon hardware through electrode interfaces. The claim is that living neural networks could become an ultra-low-power substrate for some AI/ML or adaptive-computing workloads.

This sits in the uncanny valley between neuroscience, wetlab automation, neuromorphic computing, and AI infrastructure. It is also exactly the kind of sensational frontier claim that needs a careful file: the headline is wild, but the underlying question is real. AI compute is running into power, heat, and capital constraints, and biology is an existence proof that dense adaptive computation can happen at very low energy.

## Why this might become great work

If living neural tissue can be trained, maintained, interfaced with, and benchmarked in a repeatable way, it could open a new branch of computing rather than merely a faster chip. The world-scale version is not "brains in jars replace GPUs." It is a narrow but useful biological accelerator for tasks where adaptation, pattern separation, or energy efficiency matters more than deterministic digital logic.

Utah already has unusual strengths around Recursion-style high-throughput biology, the University of Utah's biomedical engineering ecosystem, Altitude Lab, and AI infrastructure. A credible biohybrid-compute platform would connect those threads in a way few places could.

## Signals so far

- Intactis Bio lists a Salt Lake City address and says it is a resident of Altitude Lab, a Recursion Pharmaceuticals company
- Utah Business reported that Intactis is technology and research spun out of the University of Utah
- In March 2026, Intactis announced a lab demonstration in which iPSC-derived neurons coupled to custom silicon hardware produced task-distinct activity patterns for arithmetic and language inputs
- The same announcement reported a $250,000 investment from Nucleus Fund, plus support from a SHIFT grant and an Altitude Lab Trail Angel Grant
- The company is advertising a "biocomputation alpha" in which customer AI/ML workflows can be tested against living-neuron biochips, with silicon baselines

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed results showing that neural tissue performs a useful computational task better than a conventional baseline under fair energy, latency, cost, and reliability accounting
- Reproducible operation across many tissues, batches, and time periods
- Clear interfaces for encoding inputs, training the tissue, reading outputs, and keeping the system alive without heroic lab labor
- Paying customers using the platform for a task that is not just a demonstration

## Caveats

- The strongest technical claims are currently company-reported; independent replication is essential
- "Hello, World" and matrix-math demonstrations can be meaningful milestones without proving practical computing utility
- Biological systems are variable, slow to maintain, and hard to manufacture compared with silicon
- Ethical framing matters even when the tissue is not a brain or organism; the work should stay precise about what is being grown and what claims are being made
