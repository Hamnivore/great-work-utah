# Evans & Sutherland

**Tier:** A — first commercial computer graphics company; built the hardware that made real-time 3D possible; flight simulation pioneer
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Company
**Era:** 1968–present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Evans & Sutherland was founded in 1968 by David Evans and Ivan Sutherland — both professors in the University of Utah Computer Science department — operating initially out of an abandoned barracks on the university grounds. It is widely recognized as the first computer graphics company ever formed. E&S built the hardware that the U of U research program needed but that no commercial vendor supplied: vector graphics workstations capable of manipulating 3D geometry in real time.

In the early 1970s the company acquired General Electric's flight simulator division and became the dominant supplier of visual systems for military flight simulators — training pilots on synthetic terrain before they ever flew over real terrain. Their simulators trained pilots for essentially every major U.S. military aircraft program from the 1970s onward.

Key products:
- **LDS-1 (1969)** — Line Drawing System; first commercial real-time 3D display
- **Picture System series (1970s)** — calligraphic color displays capable of rotating large wireframe models in real time
- **Shaded Picture System (1973)** — first commercially available product capable of real-time shaded hidden-surface 3D graphics
- **ESIG (Extended Simulation Image Generator)** — raster-based real-time terrain rendering for flight simulators; defined the visual simulation market

## Why it mattered

E&S built the bridge between the research lab and the commercial world. The techniques developed at the U of U graphics program — Phong shading, texture mapping, hidden-surface removal — would have remained academic curiosities without hardware capable of running them. E&S made real-time 3D compute available to the defense industry first, then to commercial animation and visualization. Many of the people who later built Silicon Graphics, Pixar, and Adobe cut their teeth at E&S.

## Lasting impact

- E&S flight simulators trained a generation of military and commercial pilots; simulator-based training is now universal in aviation, and E&S established the industry standard
- The company seeded the broader 3D graphics industry: Jim Clark (SGI/Netscape), Ed Catmull (Pixar), and John Warnock (Adobe) all worked there
- The rasterization pipeline E&S industrialized in the 1970s–80s is conceptually identical to what runs on modern GPUs
- E&S is still operating in Salt Lake City (now as a digital projector company for planetariums and large-format venues), making it one of the oldest continuously operating companies in the graphics industry

## Key people

- **David Evans** — co-founder; built the U of U CS department; recruited Sutherland
- **Ivan Sutherland** — co-founder; Turing Award winner; authored Sketchpad; brought the research vision
- **Jim Clark** — early employee; later founded Silicon Graphics and Netscape
- **Ed Catmull** — early employee; later co-founded Pixar
- **John Warnock** — early employee; later co-founded Adobe

## Caveats

- E&S's primary revenue was military simulation, which limits how broadly its work is recognized in civilian computing history
- The company missed the transition to raster workstations and PCs in the mid-1980s; Silicon Graphics, which Jim Clark founded after leaving E&S, captured the commercial 3D workstation market that E&S pioneered
- Today E&S is a shadow of its peak — a niche projector company — so the legacy is historical rather than ongoing
