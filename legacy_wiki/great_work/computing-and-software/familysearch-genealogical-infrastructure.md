# FamilySearch Genealogical Infrastructure and GEDCOM

**Tier:** A — one of the world's most important genealogical archives and the source of GEDCOM, the de facto data-exchange format for family history software
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Archive / standards infrastructure / software ecosystem
**Era:** 1894–present; GEDCOM released 1984
**Location:** Salt Lake City and Granite Mountain Records Vault, UT

## What it was

The Genealogical Society of Utah, later FamilySearch, built a global genealogical record-collection and preservation system from Salt Lake City. Its work combined field microfilming, the Family History Library, the Granite Mountain Records Vault, mass indexing, public research centers, and eventually a free online search and family-tree platform.

The computing contribution is unusually concrete: the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints created GEDCOM — Genealogical Data Communication — in 1984 so genealogy programs could exchange structured family-tree data. GEDCOM became the common interchange format across genealogy software and websites, making personal research portable across tools instead of trapped in one database.

## Why it mattered

Genealogy used to be mostly local, paper-bound, and archive-bound. FamilySearch helped turn it into a global information infrastructure: records filmed from churches, courthouses, civil registries, and archives around the world could be preserved in Utah, copied back to custodians, indexed, and searched by ordinary people.

Without FamilySearch, a great deal of family-history work would have remained fragmented across national archives, parish offices, county record rooms, and incompatible desktop programs. Without GEDCOM, the consumer genealogy software ecosystem would have had a harder time becoming interoperable.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was not one algorithm. It was scale, preservation, and schema discipline over more than a century: negotiating with record custodians, filming fragile records, storing physical and digital copies, indexing names and relationships, and defining a format flexible enough for messy family data while simple enough for broad software adoption.

Genealogical data is unusually hard to standardize because names, dates, places, adoptions, disputed parentage, cultural naming systems, sources, and confidence levels do not fit cleanly into ordinary database rows. GEDCOM's longevity reflects a useful compromise: imperfect, but portable enough to become the shared plumbing of the field.

## Lasting impact

- FamilySearch became one of the largest free genealogy resources in the world
- The Granite Mountain Records Vault preserved billions of record images from more than 100 countries
- GEDCOM remains the standard exchange format for moving family-tree data among genealogy tools
- FamilySearch's indexing and online records helped make family history a mainstream digital practice rather than a niche archival specialty
- The project influenced both nonprofit and commercial genealogy ecosystems, including the expectations that records should be searchable, source-linked, and shareable

## Key people

- **Genealogical Society of Utah / FamilySearch** — the institution that built and operated the archive, library, software, indexing, and standards work
- **The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints** — sponsor and funder; religious motivations drove the long time horizon and global record-gathering effort
- **Franklin D. Richards** — first president of the Genealogical Society of Utah

## Caveats

- FamilySearch is religiously motivated, and its collection practices have sometimes raised concerns from communities, record custodians, and other religious institutions
- GEDCOM is a de facto standard, not a perfect data model; genealogists and software developers have long criticized its ambiguities and limitations
- The archive's global scope makes the Utah claim institutional rather than personal: the work was coordinated, stored, and substantially engineered from Utah, while records came from everywhere
