# Iomega Zip Drive

**Tier:** B - a short-lived but highly influential removable-storage product from Roy, Utah that became a 1990s personal-computing standard before being displaced by CD-R, USB, and flash storage
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Company / storage hardware product
**Era:** 1995-2003 peak Zip era; Iomega founded in 1980
**Location:** Roy, UT

## What it was

The Iomega Zip drive was a removable magnetic-disk storage system introduced in 1995 by Iomega, a data-storage company based in Roy, Utah. The original Zip disk stored 100 MB, far more than the 1.44 MB floppy disks still common on personal computers. Later versions stored 250 MB and 750 MB.

The product sat in the awkward but important gap between floppies and the later world of cheap CD burners, USB flash drives, broadband networks, and cloud storage. For designers, students, offices, and home-computer users moving files too large for a floppy, Zip disks became a familiar physical medium.

## Why it mattered

In the mid-1990s, ordinary people suddenly had larger hard drives, desktop publishing files, scanned images, presentations, games, and multimedia projects, but they did not yet have cheap, universal ways to move or back them up. Zip gave the PC and Macintosh world a practical, rewritable, pocketable storage format during that transition.

Iomega later described the Zip years as the period when the company built global sales channels and a strong brand, with Zip sales exceeding $1 billion per year from 1997 through 1999. The product did not define the long-term future of storage, but it mattered in the lived history of personal computing: it made large-file portability feel normal before the technologies that ultimately replaced it were ready.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was packaging high-capacity removable storage at consumer price, consumer size, and consumer ease-of-use. Existing removable hard-disk and cartridge systems served professional niches, but the mainstream PC market needed something cheap enough for retail shelves and simple enough to become a near-floppy replacement.

Zip combined Iomega's removable-media experience with a mass-market enclosure, familiar disk handling, PC and Mac interfaces, and media pricing that made sense for backup and file transfer. Its success was as much product timing and distribution as raw engineering.

## Lasting impact

- Zip became the most visible "superfloppy" product of the late 1990s
- It helped normalize removable high-capacity personal storage before USB flash drives and cheap optical media took over
- Iomega became one of Utah's best-known consumer-computing hardware companies
- The format is now a recognizable artifact in museums, archives, and personal-computing history
- Its rise and fall is a useful case study in transitional standards: dominant enough to matter, but not durable enough to become infrastructure

## Key people

- **Iomega Corporation** - Roy-based company that developed and commercialized the Zip drive
- **David Bailey, David Norton, and Jerome Paul Johnson** - Iomega founders
- **Iomega engineering, manufacturing, marketing, and channel teams** - groups that turned the product from removable-media engineering into a mass-market peripheral

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. Iomega was founded in Utah and was based in Roy during the Zip drive's rise. Contemporary coverage and later company filings identify Iomega as a Roy, Utah company during the period when Zip became a major product.

## Caveats

- Zip was transitional, not foundational infrastructure; CD-R, DVD, USB flash drives, external hard drives, broadband file transfer, and cloud storage overtook it
- Reliability problems, especially the widely reported "click of death," damaged the format's reputation
- It never fully replaced the floppy disk as a universal bundled standard
- The strongest claim is practical 1990s influence, not deep technical originality or long-run dominance
