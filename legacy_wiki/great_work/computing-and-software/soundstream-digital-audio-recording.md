# Soundstream and Commercial Digital Audio Recording

**Tier:** A — the first commercial digital recording company in the United States; helped move recorded music from analog tape into the digital era
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Company / signal-processing system / audio technology
**Era:** 1975–1985
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Soundstream was founded in Salt Lake City in 1975 by Thomas G. Stockham Jr., a University of Utah electrical engineering professor and computer scientist. The company built one of the first practical high-fidelity digital audio recording systems and offered commercial digital recording and computer-based editing services to record labels.

Soundstream's system used 16-bit digital audio and high sampling rates stored on instrumentation tape, with custom electronics and editing tools. It was not just an experiment in a lab; Soundstream recorded and edited real commercial music for labels including Telarc, Delos, RCA, Philips, Vanguard, Warner Brothers, CBS, Decca, and others.

## Why it mattered

The music industry used to depend on analog magnetic tape, with hiss, print-through, degradation, and editing limits. Soundstream showed that digital recording could be musically credible, commercially usable, and editable with computers. That helped prepare the industry for compact discs, digital mastering, digital audio workstations, and the broader world of file-based audio.

Stockham's work also connected audio engineering to signal processing. He had already worked on digital restoration of old acoustic recordings, including Enrico Caruso records, using computational techniques to recover sound from noisy historical media. Soundstream made the same basic claim at commercial scale: sound could be captured, manipulated, restored, and reproduced as data.

## The hard problem they solved

Early digital audio had to overcome limits in converters, storage bandwidth, clock stability, editing interfaces, and skepticism from musicians and engineers. It was not enough to digitize sound; the result had to sound better than analog tape, survive real recording sessions, and fit the habits of studios and record labels.

Soundstream's hard problem was integration. The company had to combine analog front ends, digital conversion, tape transport, error handling, editing software, and mastering workflows into a system that working producers would trust.

## Lasting impact

- Soundstream became the first commercial digital recording company in the United States
- Stockham is widely remembered in audio engineering as a father of digital recording
- Soundstream recordings helped prove digital audio to labels and audiophiles before the compact disc era
- The company's computer-based editing foreshadowed later digital audio workstations
- Stockham received major technical honors, including Emmy, Grammy, and Academy recognition for contributions to digital sound

## Key people

- **Thomas G. Stockham Jr.** — founder of Soundstream; University of Utah professor; pioneer of digital audio recording and restoration
- **Malcolm Low** — early Soundstream collaborator and cofounder
- **Soundstream engineering and recording staff** — built, operated, and refined the system in commercial sessions

## Caveats

- Digital recording was developing internationally; Denon and others were also experimenting with digital audio in the 1970s
- Soundstream did not become the dominant long-term platform; larger electronics companies shaped later standards and mass-market equipment
- The Utah claim is strong for the company and system, but many recordings were made on location outside Utah
