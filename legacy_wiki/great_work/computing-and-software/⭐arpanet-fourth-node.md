# University of Utah — ARPANET Fourth Node ⭐

**Tier:** S — Utah completed the original internet; the four-node ARPANET of December 1969 is the direct ancestor of every network connection on Earth today
**Domain:** Computing & Software
**Type:** Research institution / network node
**Era:** December 5, 1969
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

On December 5, 1969, the University of Utah's Computer Science Department came online as the fourth and final node of the original ARPANET — the U.S. Defense Department's experimental packet-switching network that became the foundation of the modern internet. The original four nodes, all connected by the end of 1969:

1. UCLA (September 2, 1969)
2. Stanford Research Institute (October 1, 1969)
3. UC Santa Barbara (November 1, 1969)
4. **University of Utah** (December 5, 1969)

Utah was the first node outside California and completed the planned original network. The connection used a DEC PDP-10 computer. Ivan Sutherland, who was building the U of U computer science program at the time, was instrumental in securing Utah's inclusion.

## Why it mattered

ARPANET demonstrated that packet-switching networks could work reliably — that data could be broken into packets, routed independently through a network, and reassembled correctly at the destination. This was the conceptual and infrastructural ancestor of TCP/IP and the modern internet. Utah being the fourth node wasn't incidental: it was part of the original experimental design. The four nodes needed to be geographically distributed to test whether the network could route packets across real distances. Utah's inclusion proved the concept worked beyond a single metropolitan area.

## The broader U of U computing context

Utah's selection as the 4th node was not random. By 1969, the U of U CS department — built by David Evans and Ivan Sutherland with ARPA funding — was one of the premier computing research programs in the country. ARPA funded Utah heavily because the graphics and systems research there was exactly what ARPA wanted to develop. The ARPANET connection was the infrastructure layer beneath the same community producing the foundational computer graphics work.

## Lasting impact

- The 4-node ARPANET of 1969 is the direct ancestor of the internet, which now connects ~5 billion people
- Utah's role was recognized by the University with the "Node 4" branding — the U of U IT department is literally called "Node 4"
- The connection established Utah as a participant in the earliest days of networked computing, reinforcing the broader significance of the U of U CS program's era

## Key people

- **Ivan Sutherland** — Utah's CS department head; his ARPA connections and institutional standing were central to Utah being included in the original network
- **David Evans** — built the CS department that gave Utah the standing to be included
- **Larry Roberts** — ARPA program manager who designed and built the ARPANET; made the node selection decisions

## Caveats

- Utah's role as the 4th node is historically significant but passive in one sense — the network was designed by Roberts and ARPA; Utah was a participant, not the architect
- The "birth of the internet" framing is contested; TCP/IP (1983) and the World Wide Web (1991) are also plausible "births"; ARPANET is the infrastructural origin but not the full story
- The significance is partly reflected glory from the broader U of U CS era — Sutherland and Evans would have been notable without the ARPANET connection
