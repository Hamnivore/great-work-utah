# Sun Tunnels

**Tier:** B - a major Land art work and Nancy Holt's signature outdoor installation; influential but more bounded than Spiral Jetty
**Domain:** Culture & Arts
**Type:** Artwork / earthwork / astronomical installation
**Era:** 1973-1976-present
**Location:** Great Basin Desert near Lucin, Box Elder County, UT

## What it was

Nancy Holt's *Sun Tunnels* is a land-art installation in Utah's Great Basin Desert made from four large concrete cylinders arranged in an open cross. Each tunnel is roughly 18 feet long and 9 feet in diameter. The tunnels align with sunrise and sunset on the summer and winter solstices, and their walls are pierced with holes corresponding to the constellations Draco, Perseus, Columba, and Capricorn.

Holt bought the forty-acre site in 1974 and completed the work in 1976. The result is an artwork that turns the desert into an instrument for seeing: the tunnels frame the sun, sky, horizon, and viewer's body at a scale human beings can inhabit.

## Why it mattered

Land art often leaned toward heroic scale and masculine gestures: cuts, displacements, massive marks, and remote interventions. *Sun Tunnels* offered a different grammar. It is still monumental, but its central act is framing perception. It links astronomy, bodily scale, architecture, desert space, and the daily movement of light.

The work helped establish Holt as one of the major artists of the Land art generation and remains one of the clearest examples of a permanent earthwork by a woman from that period. It also expands the Utah land-art story beyond *Spiral Jetty*: Utah was not just the location of one iconic earthwork, but a desert observatory for multiple artists trying to rethink the boundary between art, landscape, and time.

## The hard problem they solved

Holt solved the problem of making vast space intimate without domesticating it. The tunnels work as sculpture, architecture, camera, calendar, and observatory. They need to be precise enough to align with celestial events, durable enough to survive desert exposure, and open enough to change with every hour, season, and weather condition.

## Lasting impact

- It is Holt's most recognized artwork and a key work in the history of Land art
- It remains a public destination for people interested in art, astronomy, desert landscapes, and site-specific practice
- Dia Art Foundation acquired the work in 2018, placing it in a long-term stewardship context alongside other major site-specific works
- It continues to shape scholarship on Land art, especially efforts to recover the role of women artists whose work was historically overshadowed by male peers

## Key people

- **Nancy Holt** - artist; conceived, sited, and built the work
- **Holt/Smithson Foundation** - preserves and advances Holt's and Robert Smithson's artistic legacies
- **Dia Art Foundation** - owner and steward since 2018
- **Utah Museum of Fine Arts** - local partner for interpretation and visitor information

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. Holt selected and purchased land in northwestern Utah because the Great Basin desert gave the work its horizon, isolation, light, and celestial clarity. The solstice alignments, the viewer's experience inside the tunnels, and the journey to the site are all part of the piece.

## Caveats

- Holt was not primarily a Utah-based artist; this is a Utah-sited work by a nationally important artist
- The work's influence is strongest inside contemporary art, landscape, and site-specific installation; it did not reshape mass culture in the way Sundance did
- Remote desert visitation can create safety and conservation issues, and stewardship depends on visitors respecting the artwork and surrounding environment
