# Tabernacle Choir at Temple Square

**Tier:** A — one of the most recorded and broadcast choral institutions in American history; a genuine piece of cultural infrastructure
**Domain:** Culture & Arts
**Type:** Institution / performing ensemble
**Era:** 1847–present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

The Tabernacle Choir was founded on August 22, 1847 — just 29 days after the first Mormon pioneers arrived in the Salt Lake Valley. It is one of the oldest continuously operating choral ensembles in the Western Hemisphere. Today it has approximately 360 volunteer members and performs in the Salt Lake Tabernacle, a 19th-century domed building with one of the finest organ acoustics in the world.

On July 15, 1929, the choir began its weekly radio broadcast, *Music & the Spoken Word* — one of the longest-running continuously broadcast programs in American radio history, now airing on radio, television, and streaming to roughly 12 million listeners in over 50 countries weekly. President Ronald Reagan called it "America's Choir." In 2003 it received the National Medal of Arts from President George W. Bush for "extraordinary contributions to music and the art of choral singing."

The choir has performed at six U.S. presidential inaugurations: Lyndon B. Johnson (1965), Richard Nixon (1969), Ronald Reagan (1981), George H.W. Bush (1989), George W. Bush (2001), and Donald Trump (2017), and has performed for every sitting U.S. president since William Howard Taft in 1911.

## Why it mattered

The choir emerged from a frontier settlement with no permanent buildings and no professional musicians — a community that deliberately built cultural infrastructure alongside physical infrastructure. Its longevity and broadcast reach made it one of the most widely heard choral ensembles in the world, independent of the concert hall system. The weekly broadcast model — free, mass-reach, long-running — was a template for public cultural broadcasting decades before NPR.

## The hard problem they solved

Sustaining a world-class performing ensemble across 175+ years with entirely volunteer membership, no professional performers, and no institutional home other than a single congregation. The Choir has survived frontier conditions, the pre-recording era, the radio era, the television era, and the streaming era — adapting each time while maintaining continuity of identity.

## Lasting impact

- *Music & the Spoken Word* has broadcast every week since 1929, making it one of the longest continuously running broadcast programs of any kind in the world
- The choir has recorded over 200 albums, making it among the most recorded choral ensembles in history
- Its recordings of American hymns and patriotic music are among the most widely distributed versions; for many Americans, the choir's sound is the definitive sound of those works
- The Salt Lake Tabernacle's acoustic design (1867) remains a reference example in architectural acoustics — a whisper from the pulpit is audible 60 feet away

## Key people

- **Brigham Young** — directed that a choir be organized within weeks of the pioneers' arrival; established music as a cultural priority from the founding moment
- **J. Spencer Cornwall** — conductor 1935–1957; presided over the choir's growth into a national broadcast institution
- **Jerold Ottley** — conductor 1974–1999; expanded the repertoire and international profile significantly

## Caveats

- The choir is formally a program of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints; it is not a secular institution, and its repertoire and public appearances reflect church priorities
- Participation in Trump's 2017 inauguration was controversial and drew internal dissent and public criticism
- "America's Choir" is a marketing designation, not a neutral claim; other ensembles (the United States Army Chorus, the Brooklyn Tabernacle Choir) have comparable national reach
