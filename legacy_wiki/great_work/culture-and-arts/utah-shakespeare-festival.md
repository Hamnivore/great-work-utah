# Utah Shakespeare Festival

**Tier:** B - nationally recognized regional theater institution that turned a small Utah college town into a durable Shakespeare and repertory-theater destination
**Domain:** Culture & Arts
**Type:** Theater festival / regional arts institution
**Era:** 1961-present; first season 1962
**Location:** Cedar City, UT; Southern Utah University

## What it was

The Utah Shakespeare Festival is a professional theater festival founded by Fred C. Adams in Cedar City in 1961, with its first season in 1962. Adams was a theater professor at what was then the College of Southern Utah, now Southern Utah University, and built the festival as both an artistic project and a civic bet: give national-park tourists a reason to stay in Cedar City at night, and give southern Utah a serious theater institution of its own.

The festival grew from a tiny, locally underwritten Shakespeare season into a professional repertory company with outdoor and indoor theaters, actor training, seminars, education programs, new-play readings, and a summer/fall season that mixes Shakespeare with classical, modern, and contemporary work. In 2000 it received the Tony Award for Outstanding Regional Theatre.

## Why it mattered

Utah's most internationally visible cultural institution is Sundance, but the Utah Shakespeare Festival shows a different kind of great work: slow institution-building outside the metropolitan center. Cedar City was not an obvious national theater capital. The festival made a rural university town into a recurring destination for professional theater, education, and cultural tourism.

Its impact is partly artistic and partly civic. It created a durable Shakespeare audience in the Intermountain West, gave actors and designers a serious regional platform, and helped anchor Cedar City's identity as "Festival City." For southern Utah, that is not a side effect; it is the work.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was sustaining professional theater in a small, remote market. A festival can have a charming first season and still fail once novelty fades. The Utah Shakespeare Festival had to build audiences, local civic support, donor support, university alignment, production quality, theater infrastructure, and repeat visitor habits across decades.

It also had to avoid becoming only a tourist novelty. The Tony recognition matters because it signaled that the festival had reached a national regional-theater standard, not merely a successful local attraction.

## Lasting impact

- Winner of the 2000 Tony Award for Outstanding Regional Theatre
- A long-running professional Shakespeare and repertory institution on the Southern Utah University campus
- Helped shape Cedar City's civic and tourism identity around festivals, arts, and national-park adjacency
- Built permanent theater infrastructure, including the Adams Shakespearean Theatre era and the later Beverley Taylor Sorenson Center for the Arts
- Created a regional pipeline for theater education, actor training, seminars, backstage tours, and public Shakespeare interpretation

## Key people

- **Fred C. Adams** - founder, actor, director, professor, fundraiser, and longtime executive director
- **Barbara Gaddie Adams** - co-founder and partner in the festival's early creation
- **Angus Bowmer** - Oregon Shakespeare Festival founder whose model influenced Adams's planning
- **Southern Utah University and Cedar City civic leaders** - institutional hosts and local backers who made the festival durable

## Caveats

- This is not S- or A-tier "changed the trajectory of an art form" work in the way Sundance changed independent film.
- The model was influenced by the Oregon Shakespeare Festival rather than invented from scratch.
- The impact is strongest as a regional-theater and civic-institution story, not as a singular artistic breakthrough.
