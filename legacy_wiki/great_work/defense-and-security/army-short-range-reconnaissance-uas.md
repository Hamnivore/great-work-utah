# Army Short-Range Reconnaissance UAS

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a Utah-manufactured small-drone system selected for Army production, but still too recent to judge durability, field performance, or doctrinal impact
**Domain:** Defense & Security
**Type:** Small unmanned aircraft system / battlefield reconnaissance / defense manufacturing
**Era:** 2020-present; Tranche 2 production from 2025
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - Teal Drones is based in Salt Lake City, and Teal says its defense and security drone systems, including Black Widow, are manufactured in Salt Lake City.

## What it is

The Army Short-Range Reconnaissance UAS entry tracks Teal Drones' Black Widow system as a rucksack-portable small unmanned aircraft built for platoon-level reconnaissance. The work is not just a drone model; it is a Utah manufacturing and integration path for a U.S.-made tactical ISR system that can move quickly with small ground units.

The Army's SRR program is meant to give platoons the ability to see beyond the next terrain feature, feed commanders real-time intelligence and target-acquisition data, and connect small UAS output into soldier-facing control and mapping tools. Teal's Black Widow was selected as one of the systems for SRR Tranche 2 production.

## Why this might become great work

Small drones have become a basic battlefield sensor. If every small unit can launch a reliable, secure, portable aircraft for immediate reconnaissance, then situational awareness shifts from a scarce higher-echelon asset to a local tool.

The Utah significance is the domestic industrial-base piece. The U.S. military has struggled with supply-chain trust, fast iteration, electronic-warfare resilience, and scale in small drones. A Salt Lake City-built SRR system could matter if it proves that a domestic manufacturer can deliver battlefield-useful drones fast enough, securely enough, and cheaply enough to become a normal unit-level capability.

## Signals so far

- The Defense Innovation Unit and U.S. Army selected Teal in 2022 to compete in the SRR Tranche 2 program after earlier prototype work
- Red Cat reported that Teal received additional SRR prototype funding in 2023, then was selected as one of two finalists by DIU and the Army in December 2023
- In November 2024, Red Cat announced that Teal's next-generation system had won Army SRR production selection after testing by Army UAS, Maneuver Battle Lab, Army Test and Evaluation Command, and Army Operational Test Center organizations
- In August 2025, an official Army article said the Army had begun SRR Tranche 2 production and selected Teal Drones and Black Widow as one of two manufacturing vendors
- The Army says SRR Tranche 1 fielding began in September 2022 and had reached more than 16 brigades by August 2025, with lessons incorporated into Tranche 2
- The Army frames SRR systems as integrating sensors with Uncrewed Vehicle Control and Android Tactical Assault Kit capabilities for real-time intelligence, reconnaissance, target acquisition, threat detection, and route awareness
- Teal's product materials describe Black Widow as manufactured in Salt Lake City and designed around Blue UAS, secure communications, modular repair, and U.S. production

## What would upgrade this

- Public contract and delivery records showing sustained Army, allied, or other federal procurement over multiple years
- Operational evaluation showing reliability, sensor utility, communication resilience, maintainability, and soldier usability under realistic field conditions
- Evidence that Black Widow or successor systems remain useful in GPS-denied, jammed, low-light, bad-weather, and attrition-heavy environments
- Training, doctrine, or unit feedback showing SRR systems materially improve small-unit reconnaissance and survivability
- Production evidence that the Salt Lake City facility can deliver at scale without quality or supply-chain breakdowns

## Caveats

- This is defense technology with direct battlefield use; tactical value and moral value are not the same question
- The Army source confirms production selection, but public evidence still says little about battlefield effectiveness, loss rates, repair burden, cybersecurity, or electronic-warfare survivability
- The SRR program has multiple vendors and tranches; do not attribute the whole Army small-UAS modernization effort to Teal
- Small-drone technology changes quickly, and today's program-of-record system can become obsolete if counter-drone, jamming, autonomy, or battery constraints move faster
- Red Cat and Teal company releases are useful for chronology and Utah/manufacturing details, but performance claims need independent military or field evidence
