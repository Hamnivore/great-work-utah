# Autonomous Low-Collateral Counter-UAS Interception

**Speculative tier:** P-B - operationally relevant and scaling fast, but the enduring significance depends on real-world effectiveness against evolving drone threats
**Domain:** Defense & Security
**Type:** Counter-UAS system / autonomous interceptor
**Era:** 2018-present
**Location:** Lindon / Pleasant Grove, UT

## What it is

This is the work of detecting, tracking, and physically intercepting small drones with autonomous systems that avoid the debris, jamming, and collateral-damage problems of many counter-drone approaches. Fortem's DroneHunter and SkyDome family are the clearest Utah example: compact radar, command-and-control software, and drone-on-drone interceptors that can capture unauthorized drones using nets or other effectors.

The work is not simply "a defense company makes drones." The interesting part is low-collateral air defense against cheap, small, low-flying aerial threats in places where missiles, bullets, or broad jamming are bad answers: airports, borders, bases, stadiums, energy infrastructure, and dense civilian areas.

## Why this might become great work

Small drones have changed the economics of air threat. A cheap quadcopter can threaten aircraft, power infrastructure, prisons, public events, or military assets worth orders of magnitude more. If autonomous low-collateral interception becomes a standard layer of air defense, Utah-built systems may be part of the template.

The work could also matter outside war. Civil airspace needs ways to stop dangerous drones without showering crowds with debris or disabling legitimate communications.

## Signals so far

- DroneHunter was announced in 2018 as an autonomous counter-UAS system using radar-guided interception
- Fortem reports more than 70,000 test flights and 5,500 successful drone captures in Utah
- Its systems have been deployed in Ukraine, the Middle East, East Asia, U.S. border operations, and critical-infrastructure contexts
- A 2025 Lindon manufacturing facility brought radar and interceptor production under one roof, with stated capacity for hundreds of units per month
- In 2026, Fortem announced deliveries of DroneHunter 5.0 and a World Cup venue-protection order

## What would upgrade this

- Independent operational evaluations against realistic swarm, FPV, and high-speed drone threats
- Sustained procurement by U.S. or allied defense agencies
- Civilian deployment at major airports or mass events with a strong safety record
- Evidence that drone-on-drone capture is cost-effective compared with jamming, directed energy, or expendable interceptors

## Caveats

- Counter-UAS claims are hard to verify publicly because many deployments are sensitive
- The threat is moving quickly; FPV drones, swarms, autonomy, and electronic warfare may change the design target
- Defense work has moral complexity, especially when systems move from protective use into active conflict zones
- Company-reported capture counts and deployment claims need independent corroboration
