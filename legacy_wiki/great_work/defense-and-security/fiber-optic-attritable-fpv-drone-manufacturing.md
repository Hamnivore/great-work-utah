# Fiber-Optic Attritable FPV Drone Manufacturing

**Speculative tier:** P-B - strategically relevant and moving fast, but still needs independent battlefield validation and procurement durability
**Domain:** Defense & Security
**Type:** Defense manufacturing / small unmanned aerial systems
**Era:** 2025-present
**Location:** Bluffdale and Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Fiber-optic attritable FPV drone manufacturing is the attempt to build small military drones that can operate in jammed environments while being cheap enough to deploy at battlefield scale. The Utah example is Vector's Hammer platform, a long-range FPV quadcopter with fiber-optic integration, cameras for deep sensing, and optional payload configurations for strike missions.

The technical thrust is simple and grim: modern battlefields are full of electronic warfare, cheap drones, and short adaptation cycles. Radio-controlled drones can be jammed or detected. Fiber-optic control gives a drone a physical communications path, radio silence, and resistance to some electronic-warfare effects, at the cost of range, handling, spool weight, and operational complexity.

## Why this might become great work

The Ukraine war made small drones impossible to ignore. The United States now faces the hard problem of producing trusted, secure, attritable drones at volumes closer to consumer electronics than traditional defense acquisition. If a Utah manufacturing base can turn operator feedback into thousands or tens of thousands of reliable systems, it would be a real shift in the domestic drone industrial base.

The great-work version is not a single drone model. It is the manufacturing and iteration loop: warfighter-informed design, domestic supply chains, electronic-warfare-aware control, and production that keeps pace with field tactics.

## Signals so far

- Vector announced the Hammer FPV in July 2025 as a 10-inch long-range FPV drone with fiber-optic integration and Utah-based manufacturing
- The company says Hammer is designed for large-scale production and describes payload, flight-time, speed, camera, and jam-resistant control features
- In September 2025, Vector announced a $61 million Series A to scale production
- In March 2026, Utah Business reported a $20 million J.P. Morgan loan to expand domestic drone manufacturing at Vector's Utah facility
- 47G and Utah Business reported a live demonstration of Vector's smaller Dagger drone at Utah Aerospace & Defense Day on Capitol Hill in January 2026
- A 2025 press release reported a multi-million-dollar contract with Special Operations Command

## What would upgrade this

- Sustained procurement by U.S. or allied forces after field evaluation
- Independent operational data showing performance under jamming, GPS denial, harsh weather, and realistic attrition
- Evidence that the Utah facility can produce at the promised volume and quality
- A broader product-development loop where field learning quickly changes hardware, payloads, software, and manufacturing

## Caveats

- This is morally serious technology; tactical usefulness does not settle whether particular uses are wise or humane
- Many claims are company-reported and embedded in defense-sector marketing language
- Fiber-optic FPV is a visible trend in modern war, so the durable advantage may come from manufacturing and integration rather than novelty
- Procurement can move quickly during urgency and then collapse if budgets, doctrine, or battlefield lessons change
