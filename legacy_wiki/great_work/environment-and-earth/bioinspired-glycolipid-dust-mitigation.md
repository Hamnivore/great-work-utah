# Bioinspired Glycolipid Dust Mitigation

**Speculative tier:** P-C - promising environmental-health materials work with NIH Phase I/II support, but still needs field validation and durable adoption
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Environmental health technology / dust-control material / green chemistry
**Era:** 2022-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT, with University of Arizona collaboration

**Utah attribution:** Medium - GlycoSurf is a Salt Lake City company and NIH records list Utah recipient/performance addresses, but University of Arizona researchers and patents are central to the source trail.

## What it is

Bioinspired glycolipid dust mitigation is an attempt to use biodegradable, sugar-based surfactants to suppress fugitive mineral dust from mine tailings, haul roads, agricultural soils, and other dry disturbed surfaces. GlycoSurf's role is supplying and developing high-purity glycolipid surfactants; the NIH/NIEHS-funded project studies whether those materials can reduce respirable dust without the environmental downsides of some conventional dust-control products.

The specific work is not generic dust science. It is a materials-and-environmental-health method: testing bioinspired glycolipids as dust suppressants for hazardous dryland and mining contexts where particulate matter can carry arsenic, lead, and other contaminants downwind.

## Why this might become great work

Dryland dust is becoming a larger public-health and climate-adaptation problem as drought, disturbed soils, mining, tailings, construction, and shrinking water bodies expose more fine sediment. Dust-control products already exist, but they can be expensive, short-lived, petroleum-derived, corrosive, ecologically harmful, or impractical at large scale.

If bioinspired glycolipids suppress PM10 and PM2.5 under real weather, traffic, mining, and soil conditions while staying biodegradable and safe, they could become a useful environmental-health tool. The Utah-relevant version would matter for mining districts, industrial roads, dry lakebeds, and other Western dust sources where control has to be practical and ecologically defensible.

## Signals so far

- NIEHS Superfund Research Program records list a Phase I and Phase II project, "Bioinspired Green Glycolipids as Fugitive Dust Mitigation Agents," with GlycoSurf and project leader Chett Boxley
- SBIR.gov lists a 2025 Phase II NIH award to GlycoSurf in Salt Lake City for the dust-mitigation project
- HHS TAGGS lists GlycoSurf at a Salt Lake City address for R43ES034319 and R44ES034319 awards
- The project summary frames mining dust as a pathway for hazardous exposures including arsenic and lead, and calls for environmentally compatible mitigation technologies
- University of Arizona's Superfund Research Center describes the project as a collaboration among UArizona researchers and GlycoSurf to study green glycolipid surfactants for dust from tailings and haul roads
- NIEHS lists a pending 2022 patent, "Bioinspired Glycolipids as Fugitive Dust Suppression Agents," associated with University of Arizona DUST Center work
- GlycoSurf describes high-purity, biodegradable glycolipid surfactants and custom sugar-based surfactant chemistry from a Salt Lake City operation

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed bench and field data show strong PM10/PM2.5 suppression across realistic soils, tailings, roads, wind, UV, rain, and vehicle wear
- Independent toxicity and ecotoxicity studies show lower environmental risk than conventional suppressants
- Mine operators, public-land managers, agriculture users, or air-quality agencies deploy the method at meaningful scale
- Monitoring shows reduced downwind exposure to dust or dust-associated metals
- Cost, durability, application rate, and reapplication data make the method practical outside grant-funded pilots

## Caveats

- The clearest technical trail is still grant-stage, not field-adopted infrastructure
- The University of Arizona contribution is central; the Utah claim rests on the Salt Lake City company, recipient address, and material supply/development role
- Dust control is extremely site-specific, so success in one soil or tailings type may not transfer
- Biodegradable does not automatically mean harmless at the concentrations needed for field-scale suppression
- This may mature into a useful specialty chemical without becoming world-changing environmental infrastructure
