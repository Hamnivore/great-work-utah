# Bonneville Salt Flats — Land Speed Records

**Tier:** C — the world's preeminent site for land speed records since 1935; significant for motorsport engineering and geological science; the "work" is the racing achievements, not the geology
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Natural site / venue for achievement
**Era:** 1935–present (modern racing era)
**Location:** Tooele County, UT (near Wendover)

## What it is

The Bonneville Salt Flats are a 30,000-acre salt pan in northwestern Utah, remnant of ancient Lake Bonneville (a massive freshwater lake that covered much of the Great Basin 14,000–32,000 years ago). The flat is up to 7 feet thick and 13 miles long — an almost perfectly flat, hard surface ideal for high-speed vehicle runs.

Sir Malcolm Campbell set the first official land speed record at Bonneville on September 3, 1935: 301.1 mph. The record was broken 18 times at Bonneville between 1935 and 1970. Notable records:
- **Craig Breedlove, 1963** — first jet-powered car over 400 mph at Bonneville (407.45 mph)
- **Craig Breedlove, 1964** — first over 500 mph (526.28 mph) — the first human to travel over 500 mph on land
- **Art Arfons and Craig Breedlove, 1964–65** — traded the record four times in one year; the most intense speed record competition in history
- The absolute land speed record (763 mph, ThrustSSC, 1997) was set at Black Rock Desert, NV — not Bonneville — but Bonneville remains the primary venue for class records and speed week

Each October, Bonneville Speed Week draws thousands of racers and the full range of land speed record attempts — from motorcycles to piston aircraft engines to streamlined electric cars.

## Why it matters

Bonneville is where the physics of high-speed vehicle dynamics gets tested at extremes impossible anywhere else. The aerodynamic research, tire science, engine tuning, and drivetrain engineering developed for Bonneville record attempts has fed into production car and motorcycle development. It is the proving ground at the absolute limit.

## Geological significance

The salt crust records detailed information about the Pleistocene climate of the Great Basin. The layered salt deposits are studied by geologists and paleoclimatologists to reconstruct historical lake levels and regional climate patterns. The flat surface is also used as a calibration and navigation test area by aerospace and defense organizations — the GPS and inertial navigation systems on aircraft and missiles have been tested here.

## Caveats

- The "great work" here is the achievement of the record-setting teams, not work indigenous to Utah — the vehicles and engineering happen elsewhere; Utah provides the surface
- The flats are threatened: potash mining by Reilly Industries has drawn down the water table and the salt crust has thinned from 7 feet to inches in some areas; the 13-mile course is now effectively 8 miles or less
- The site is listed on the National Register of Historic Places but does not have strong federal protection against continued mining damage
