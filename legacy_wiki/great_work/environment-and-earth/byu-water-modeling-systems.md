# BYU Water Modeling Systems

**Tier:** B - widely used hydrology, hydraulics, and groundwater modeling software with a strong Utah origin and global professional adoption
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Scientific software / water-resources engineering infrastructure
**Era:** late 1980s-present
**Location:** Brigham Young University, Provo, UT; later Aquaveo, Provo, UT

## What it was

BYU's water modeling systems were a family of visual engineering tools for groundwater, surface-water, and watershed simulation. The major products were:

- **GMS** - Groundwater Modeling System
- **SMS** - Surface-water Modeling System
- **WMS** - Watershed Modeling System

They grew out of the Engineering Computer Graphics Laboratory at Brigham Young University, later the Environmental Modeling Research Laboratory. The work was supported heavily by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers and translated BYU's earlier computer-graphics capability into practical environmental modeling. In 2007, the main software-development team spun out as Aquaveo, which continued developing the tools in Provo.

## Why it mattered

Water engineering is full of high-stakes spatial questions: where a contaminant plume will move, how a flood wave will travel, how a river crossing will behave, how coastal currents and sediment will change, or how a watershed will respond to a storm. Before graphical model-building tools matured, engineers often interacted with numerical models through grids, meshes, and text inputs that were hard to inspect and painful to revise.

The BYU systems helped make water modeling visual and GIS-aware. Instead of forcing engineers to define every cell or element by hand, the software let them build conceptual models from familiar spatial objects such as wells, rivers, lakes, boundaries, soils, land-use layers, and polygons, then generate the numerical model from that higher-level description. That made model creation faster, easier to communicate, and more adaptable when assumptions changed.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was bridging three worlds that did not naturally fit together: GIS data, numerical simulation, and engineering judgment. A useful water model has to honor terrain, channels, aquifers, boundary conditions, structures, soils, land use, and calibration data. It also has to produce results that an engineer, planner, or emergency manager can understand quickly.

The BYU approach put a graphical conceptual-model layer between raw spatial data and the underlying numerical engines. GMS, SMS, and WMS could then act as pre- and post-processors around established models while giving users consistent visualization, meshing, and map-based editing tools.

## Lasting impact

- Aquaveo reports that its software is used by more than 12,000 consulting firms, universities, and government agencies in more than 120 countries
- BYU's own account in 2002 reported use in more than 100 countries and more than 9,000 organizations
- The tools were used in military, humanitarian, municipal, and infrastructure contexts, including Bosnia flood logistics, Mozambique and Honduras emergency flood analysis, Salt Lake Olympic security planning, floodplain design, river-erosion studies, and contaminated-aquifer cleanup planning
- The software helped turn a Utah computer-graphics lab into durable water-resources engineering infrastructure

## Caveats

- This is professional infrastructure, not a single breakthrough that changed public consciousness.
- Adoption figures partly come from BYU and Aquaveo sources, so the entry should not overstate independent validation.
- The numerical engines wrapped by GMS, SMS, and WMS often came from government and academic hydrology communities outside Utah. BYU's contribution was the visual modeling environment, workflow, and commercialization path, not every underlying solver.
- Aquaveo is proprietary software, unlike some other scientific infrastructure in this wiki.
