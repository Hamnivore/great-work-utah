# Cleveland-Lloyd Dinosaur Quarry

**Tier:** B — one of the densest Jurassic dinosaur fossil concentrations ever found and the world's great Allosaurus source
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Paleontology site / natural laboratory
**Era:** 1920s–present
**Location:** Emery County, UT

## What it is

The Cleveland-Lloyd Dinosaur Quarry, now part of Jurassic National Monument, is a dense Jurassic bonebed in central Utah's San Rafael Swell. More than 12,000 fossils have been excavated from the site, representing at least dozens of individual animals, with an extraordinary concentration of carnivorous dinosaurs, especially *Allosaurus fragilis*.

The quarry became a major research site through University of Utah work beginning in the 1920s and especially through mid-century excavations led by Utah paleontologists including William Lee Stokes and James H. Madsen Jr. Fossils from the quarry are displayed in museums around the world, and the site remains scientifically interesting because nobody has fully explained why so many predator bones accumulated there.

## Why it mattered

Cleveland-Lloyd made *Allosaurus* one of the best-known large Jurassic predators. The site's unusually large sample size lets paleontologists study variation, growth, pathology, and population structure in a way that a single beautiful skeleton cannot. It also helped establish Utah as one of the world's great dinosaur states, with a research identity that extends from Dinosaur National Monument to the Kaiparowits Plateau.

## The hard problem they solved

The scientific puzzle is taphonomy: what happened to create a dense, mixed, predator-heavy bonebed with few articulated skeletons? Researchers have proposed predator traps, drought-related death assemblages, pond deposits, mud traps, and other explanations. The quarry's value is partly that it refuses to be simple; it keeps forcing paleontologists to test how behavior, environment, sediment, and preservation interact.

## Lasting impact

- The quarry is the primary source of many *Allosaurus* specimens used in research and museum displays
- *Allosaurus* became Utah's official state fossil in 1988
- Cleveland-Lloyd fossils are held and displayed by institutions across the world
- The site remains an active public-facing paleontology classroom through Jurassic National Monument

## Key people

- **William Lee Stokes** — University of Utah geologist who helped document and interpret the quarry
- **James H. Madsen Jr.** — Utah's first state paleontologist; led major work on the quarry and *Allosaurus*
- **University of Utah / Natural History Museum of Utah** — major institutional stewards of Cleveland-Lloyd material
- **Bureau of Land Management** — current manager of Jurassic National Monument

## Caveats

- The fossils are Utah's natural endowment; the human work was excavation, interpretation, curation, and public education
- The quarry is scientifically famous, but its impact is mostly within paleontology and museums rather than broad technology or industry
- The central mystery of the bonebed remains unresolved
