# Physics-Based AI Air Quality Forecasting

**Speculative tier:** P-C - useful Utah climate-health technology with credible university roots, but still early and narrower than the strongest watchlist items
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Air-quality forecasting platform / climate-health decision support
**Era:** 2025-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Trace Air Quality is commercializing smoke and air-quality forecasting technology developed by University of Utah researchers. The platform combines physics-based atmospheric modeling with machine-learning and predictive-analytics augmentation to forecast wildfire smoke, dust, inversions, and other air-quality hazards for specific locations.

The work grew from University of Utah atmospheric science and chemical engineering research by Heather Holmes, Derek Mallia, and Taylor "Kai" Wilmot, with commercialization through Trace AQ in Salt Lake City.

## Why this might become great work

Wildfire smoke, dust, inversions, and pollution episodes are becoming routine health and operational problems. Better forecasts could help schools move recess, hospitals warn vulnerable patients, employers protect outdoor workers, event organizers adjust schedules, and cities plan mitigation.

The Utah angle is unusually natural: Salt Lake City has severe inversion and wildfire-smoke exposure, University of Utah has deep atmospheric science strength, and the Great Basin is a living testbed for smoke, dust, terrain, and air-chemistry problems.

## Signals so far

- Trace AQ launched from University of Utah technology and closed a $1.25 million seed round in 2025
- The core technology is described as coming from nearly ten years of University of Utah research into plume rise, emissions, fire-atmosphere interactions, transport, and atmospheric chemistry
- The Wilkes Center released a public demonstration smoke-forecast tool using Trace AQ capabilities in 2025
- The system uses EPA's open-source CMAQ modeling system as part of its forecast stack, with machine-learning augmentation
- The University of Utah Technology Licensing Office named Trace AQ its 2025 Startup of the Year
- Trace AQ's commercial Aero product advertises location-specific forecasts and four-day forecast playthroughs

## What would upgrade this

- Independent accuracy benchmarking against NOAA, EPA, PurpleAir, AirNow, and regional smoke models across multiple fire seasons
- Adoption by school districts, health systems, employers, insurers, utilities, or emergency managers
- Evidence that forecasts change behavior and reduce exposure, missed work, emergency visits, or event disruption
- Operational integration with Great Salt Lake dust monitoring, wildfire incident management, or public-health alerting
- Peer-reviewed model validation and public error metrics

## Caveats

- Forecasting smoke and air quality is difficult because emissions, plume rise, chemistry, and local terrain can change quickly
- The strongest evidence would be independent forecast skill, not a polished map or seed round
- The work is valuable but may remain a regional decision-support tool rather than world-changing infrastructure
- Public agencies and large weather providers may absorb similar capabilities over time
