# Seafloor Carbon Assessment Probes

**Speculative tier:** P-C - University of Utah marine-carbon measurement instrumentation with ARPA-E funding and early sensor publication, but still needs seafloor deployment proof
**Domain:** Environment & Earth
**Type:** Marine carbon dioxide removal measurement / environmental sensor
**Era:** 2023-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - SEASCAPE is led from the University of Utah Electrical and Computer Engineering department and Utah Nanofabrication Facility.

## What it is

SEASCAPE, short for SEAfloor Self-sustaining CO2 Assessment Probe Edge, is a University of Utah project to build micro-optical and micro-electronic probes for measuring carbon accumulation in ocean sediments. The probes are intended to be inserted directly into the seafloor and operate for more than a year across a wide measurement area.

The Utah-specific work is the sensor package: MEMS-style probes, low-power electronics, and dissolved carbon dioxide / pH measurement using a novel bubble mechanism and microbolometer-based infrared sensing.

## Why this might become great work

Marine carbon dioxide removal has a measurement problem. If carbon is stored in ocean sediments or moved through marine systems, developers, regulators, scientists, and communities need durable monitoring that can verify whether carbon is actually stored and whether local chemistry changes are safe.

If Utah-built probes can survive on the seafloor and provide reliable low-power carbon and pH data for long periods, they could become part of the measurement, reporting, and verification infrastructure needed before ocean-based carbon removal can be judged responsibly.

## Signals so far

- DOE/ARPA-E selected SEASCAPE under the SEA-CO2 program with a $2,004,554 award
- University of Utah ECE identifies Hanseup Kim, Carlos Mastrangelo, and Seungbeom Noh as project leads
- ARPA-E describes the project as a micro-optical, micro-electronic seafloor probe meant to extend the longevity and persistence of current seafloor carbon-storage measurement tools
- ARPA-E says the probes would be inserted directly into the seafloor and measure carbon accumulation in ocean sediments for more than a year
- A 2026 *Sensors* paper from the University of Utah team reports a MEMS microbolometer-based ATR mid-infrared sensor for direct dissolved CO2 detection and UV-induced sediment carbon assay, funded by ARPA-E under the SEASCAPE project
- The paper reports lab performance across environmental variables and frames the sensor as a field-deployable platform for aquatic carbon cycling and sediment organic carbon studies

## What would upgrade this

- Seafloor deployment data showing survival, drift, calibration, biofouling resistance, pressure tolerance, sediment-interface behavior, and power budget over months
- Independent comparison against established pCO2, pH, alkalinity, sediment-core, chamber, or benthic-flux measurements
- Use in a real marine carbon dioxide removal field trial with transparent measurement, reporting, and verification uncertainty
- Open datasets or peer-reviewed field papers showing the probes can detect carbon accumulation or chemistry changes relevant to governance
- Adoption by ocean-observing programs, CDR developers, national labs, or regulators

## Caveats

- Marine carbon dioxide removal is scientifically and politically unsettled; good sensors do not prove a removal pathway is safe, durable, or scalable
- Lab dissolved-CO2 sensing and UV-induced sediment assays are early steps, not year-plus seafloor operation
- Biofouling, sediment heterogeneity, corrosion, pressure, deployment logistics, and calibration drift can break elegant ocean sensors
- The project is valuable as measurement infrastructure, not as a claim that ocean carbon removal itself will work
- P-C is the right tier until field validation appears
