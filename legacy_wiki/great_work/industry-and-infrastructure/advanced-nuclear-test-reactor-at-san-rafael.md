# Advanced Nuclear Test Reactor at San Rafael

**Speculative tier:** P-B - historically notable if it reaches power operation and informs deployable advanced reactors, but currently a test platform under an accelerated and controversial path
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Advanced nuclear test reactor / energy research infrastructure
**Era:** 2025-present
**Location:** Emery County / Orangeville, UT

## What it is

The advanced nuclear test reactor at Utah's San Rafael Energy Lab is Valar Atomics' Ward 250 project: a high-temperature gas reactor test platform using TRISO fuel, helium coolant, and graphite moderation. The project is hosted by the Utah San Rafael Energy Lab, a unit within the Utah Office of Energy Development.

The near-term reactor is for research and validation, not commercial electricity generation. Its purpose is to test components, train teams, validate assumptions, and build a regulatory and operational path toward later high-temperature reactors for industrial power, hydrogen, data centers, and synthetic fuels.

## Why this might become great work

Advanced nuclear has a brutal commercialization problem: promising designs, slow licensing, high capital cost, and little recent U.S. construction muscle. If Utah becomes a credible test bed where a new reactor reaches meaningful operation quickly and safely, the state could matter in the broader return of American nuclear engineering.

The speculative upside is not just one test reactor. It is whether standardized high-temperature reactors can move from bespoke nuclear megaprojects toward repeatable industrial infrastructure.

## Signals so far

- Utah San Rafael Energy Lab says construction was scheduled to begin in 2025, with the test reactor expected to go online in 2026
- In September 2025, Utah officials and Valar announced groundbreaking for the Ward 250 site
- Valar was selected for the U.S. Department of Energy Nuclear Reactor Pilot Program, which targets criticality for multiple reactors by July 4, 2026
- Valar announced that its NOVA Core achieved zero-power criticality at the Nevada National Security Site in November 2025 through work with Los Alamos National Laboratory
- The Utah project has announced engineering, construction, architecture, and design partners including Kiewit, Goree, and Sprung
- The work aligns with Utah's Operation Gigawatt push to expand generation capacity and energy R&D

## What would upgrade this

- Ward 250 reaches safe power operation in Utah
- Public technical results show useful reactor-physics, component, safety, or operating data
- The project informs a licensable commercial reactor design
- Independent nuclear engineers and regulators view the test path as technically credible and safety-preserving

## Caveats

- Valar is not Utah-based; the Utah claim rests on the test site and Utah energy-lab role
- The test reactor is not a power plant and will not by itself generate commercial electricity
- Accelerated nuclear pathways are politically and technically controversial
- Criticality milestones are important but far from commercial deployment
- Advanced nuclear failures can be expensive, slow, and reputationally damaging even when the underlying physics works
