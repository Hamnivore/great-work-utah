# AI-Scale Behind-the-Meter Energy Gigasites

**Speculative tier:** P-B - could matter if Utah becomes a proving ground for power-first AI infrastructure, but the project is still early and many agreements are non-binding
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Energy infrastructure / data-center campus / industrial development
**Era:** 2025-present
**Location:** Delta, Millard County, UT

## What it is

AI-scale behind-the-meter energy gigasites are data-center campuses designed around dedicated power generation rather than ordinary utility interconnection. The Utah case is Creekstone Energy's Delta Gigasite in Millard County: a proposed multi-source power and digital-infrastructure campus combining natural gas, solar, long-duration storage, transmission, cooling, and possible future nuclear options for hyperscale AI computing.

The important idea is not just another data center. It is a power-first model for AI infrastructure: buy the land, secure zoning, build generation near the load, smooth it with storage, and treat energy supply as the core product.

## Why this might become great work

AI infrastructure is becoming constrained by electricity, cooling, transmission queues, transformer availability, and local political tolerance. If Utah can turn low-density desert land, industrial zoning, energy development, and transmission access into a repeatable campus model, it could shape where large-scale AI compute gets built in the western United States.

The upside case is an industrial template: co-located compute and power that does not wait years for the grid to catch up. The more important version would also prove that these campuses can add firm supply and renewable capacity without simply pushing costs, emissions, or water stress onto surrounding communities.

## Signals so far

- Creekstone says the Delta Gigasite has 1,143 acres owned, heavy-industrial zoning and conditional-use approvals, a gas interconnect under construction, geotechnical work complete, and Torus load-smoothing equipment installed
- In August 2025, Creekstone and BluSky AI announced an agreement for up to 50 MW of power and land for modular AI data centers
- In December 2025, Creekstone and EnergySolutions signed a non-binding MOU to study at least two gigawatts of potential next-generation nuclear capacity for the site or related locations
- In February 2026, Zeo Energy announced a non-binding MOU with Creekstone to develop about 280 MW of solar plus long-duration storage for the Utah AI data center
- In April 2026, Creekstone announced a long-term lease covering about 13,000 acres of Utah trust lands and Millard County zoning approval for the first phase of more than 1 GW of solar development

## What would upgrade this

- Data-center tenants operating at substantial load with measured uptime and power quality
- A completed mix of behind-the-meter generation and storage, not just MOUs
- Transparent accounting of emissions, water use, grid interactions, tax effects, and local environmental impacts
- A repeatable project-finance model that others can use without hidden subsidies or stranded infrastructure risk

## Caveats

- Several of the most ambitious pieces are non-binding MOUs or early-stage evaluations
- AI data-center demand can change quickly if model economics, hardware, regulation, or financing shift
- Behind-the-meter power can help speed deployment, but it can also bypass normal public planning if handled poorly
- The project could become regionally important infrastructure without becoming "great work" in the stronger historical sense
