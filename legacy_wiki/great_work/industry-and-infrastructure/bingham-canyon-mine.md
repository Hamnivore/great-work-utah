# Kennecott Bingham Canyon Mine

**Tier:** A — largest open-pit mine ever excavated and a global template for low-grade porphyry copper mass mining; legacy is economically enormous and environmentally mixed
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Mine / industrial operation
**Era:** 1906–present
**Location:** Bingham Canyon (Copperton), UT

## What it was

The Bingham Canyon Mine is the largest open-pit copper mine in the world by volume excavated — roughly 1.2 km deep and 4 km wide, visible from space. Operated by Kennecott Utah Copper (a subsidiary of Rio Tinto), it has produced more copper than any other mine in history: over 19 million tons since 1906. It also produces gold, silver, and molybdenum as byproducts. The pit itself is a National Historic Landmark — the only mine so designated.

## Why it mattered

Copper is foundational infrastructure: electrical wiring, plumbing, motors, and now EV powertrains and grid cables. Bingham Canyon's scale drove down copper prices in the early 20th century and supplied a significant fraction of U.S. copper demand through both World Wars and into the industrial expansion of the postwar era. Open-pit mining techniques developed at Bingham Canyon — including the use of rail haulage and later truck haulage at scale — were exported to mines worldwide. The mine belongs above a merely regional B tier because it helped prove the mass-mining model that made low-grade porphyry deposits central to global copper supply.

## The hard problem they solved

Low-grade porphyry copper ore (0.3–0.5% copper) is uneconomic unless you can process enormous volumes cheaply. Bingham Canyon developed and industrialized the mass-mining techniques (drill-blast-haul at scale) and concentrator technology needed to make low-grade deposits profitable — the same approach that now dominates large-scale mining globally.

## Lasting impact

- The open-pit mass-mining template developed at Bingham Canyon is the standard approach for large copper, gold, and iron mines worldwide
- The mine continues to operate; Rio Tinto has invested in extending its life to 2032 and beyond
- The 2013 landslide (one of the largest non-volcanic landslides in recorded history) destroyed much of the infrastructure but operations resumed — a notable case study in mine recovery engineering

## Key people

- **Daniel Jackling** — mining engineer who pioneered the mass-mining approach at Bingham Canyon starting in 1904; his techniques were adopted globally

## Caveats

- The mine has contaminated groundwater and tailings ponds in the Salt Lake Valley for over a century; remediation costs are ongoing and the full environmental ledger is negative
- The labor history includes violent strikes and suppression of organizing in the early 20th century
- The "National Historic Landmark" designation reflects industrial history, not an endorsement of the environmental impact
