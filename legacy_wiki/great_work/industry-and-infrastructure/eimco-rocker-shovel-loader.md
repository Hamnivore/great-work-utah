# EIMCO Rocker Shovel Loader

**Tier:** A - a Salt Lake City-manufactured mining machine that mechanized one of underground hard-rock mining's hardest manual jobs
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Mining equipment / mechanical engineering
**Era:** 1930s-1960s
**Location:** Eureka, Salt Lake City, and Park City, UT

## What it was

The EIMCO Model 12B Rocker Shovel Loader was a compressed-air underground mining loader introduced in 1938. It mechanized "mucking": the backbreaking job of removing blasted rock from narrow hard-rock mine tunnels and loading it into mine cars.

The original overshot-loader idea was developed by Edwin Burt Royle and John Spence Finlay, who had worked at the North Lilly Mine in Eureka, Utah. After Joseph Rosenblatt of Salt Lake City's EIMCO met them, Royle joined EIMCO as a consultant and designer. EIMCO developed the design into the Model 12B, manufactured it in Salt Lake City, and sold it internationally.

## Why it mattered

Underground mines could drill and blast faster than they could clear broken rock. Human muckers with shovels were slow, exhausted, exposed to dust and hazards, and constrained by tunnel geometry. A compact machine that could drive into rubble, scoop it, and throw it backward into a mine car changed the pace and labor structure of underground hard-rock mining.

ASME calls the Model 12B the first successful mining device to replace human labor in removing rubble from underground hard-rock blasting. By 1969, about 29,000 rocker shovel loaders had been sold, with use and licensed manufacturing spread across North America, South America, Europe, Africa, Asia, and Australia.

## The hard problem they solved

The machine had to do a worker's shoveling motion inside a cramped, rail-bound tunnel, using mine-available compressed air rather than delicate electric or gasoline systems. It had to be rugged enough for rock impact, dust, water, vibration, bad track, and rough maintenance.

The rocker-shovel mechanism solved this with a bucket mounted on rocker arms. The operator drove the bucket into the muck pile, then actuated a bucket drive that rolled the bucket upward and rearward, flinging the rock into a mine car behind the machine.

## Lasting impact

- The Model 12B became an ASME Historic Mechanical Engineering Landmark
- It helped mechanize underground hard-rock mining during a period when mining still depended heavily on manual labor
- EIMCO licensed manufacturing rights in Great Britain, India, South Africa, and Japan
- Sales expanded to countries including Australia, Canada, Chile, France, Mexico, Spain, and Zambia
- The design became a recognizable machine category: the rocker shovel, mucker, or overshot loader

## Key people

- **Edwin Burt Royle** - co-designer and patent inventor of the loading machine
- **John Spence Finlay** - co-developer of the early overshot-loader concept
- **Joseph Rosenblatt** - EIMCO leader who connected the design to Salt Lake City manufacturing
- **EIMCO / Eastern Iron Metals Company** - Salt Lake City company that developed, manufactured, and sold the Model 12B internationally

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah tie is unusually strong. The early machine came from Utah mining practice at the North Lilly Mine in Eureka. EIMCO then developed and manufactured the successful Model 12B in Salt Lake City. The ASME landmark example is in Park City, and the ASME plaque explicitly credits Salt Lake City's EIMCO Corporation.

## Caveats

- Mining mechanization increased productivity but also displaced some kinds of labor and intensified extractive industry
- The machine improved a dangerous job but did not make underground hard-rock mining safe
- The strongest claim is sectoral: the Model 12B mattered greatly to mining, not to every industry
- Royle and Finlay's original concept predated EIMCO's commercial product, so the credit belongs to both mine-originated invention and Salt Lake City industrialization
