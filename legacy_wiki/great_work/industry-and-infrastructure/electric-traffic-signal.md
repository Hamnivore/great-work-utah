# Electric Traffic Signal

**Tier:** B - an early Utah-built electric traffic signal with a direct local invention claim, but later patented and standardized systems carried more of the global traffic-control lineage
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Urban infrastructure / traffic-control invention
**Era:** 1912-1917
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

In 1912, Salt Lake City police officer Lester Farnsworth Wire built and installed an electric traffic signal at Main Street and 200 South in downtown Salt Lake City. The device was a manually controlled box with red and green lights, powered from overhead trolley or power wires, intended to move traffic through a busy intersection more safely than an officer standing in the street.

Wire did not patent the device, and later traffic-signal systems evolved through other inventors and cities. But Utah transportation historians, UDOT, and multiple traffic-history accounts credit Wire's Salt Lake City signal as the first electric traffic light or one of the first recognizably modern electric traffic signals.

## Why it mattered

Traffic signals are now mundane infrastructure, which is exactly why they matter. They made dense urban intersections more legible, reduced dependence on officers physically directing every stream of movement, and allowed cities to coordinate vehicles, streetcars, pedestrians, and later large road networks.

The early Salt Lake City signal was not the whole modern system. It had no yellow phase, was manually controlled, and lacked the patented emergency and centralized features that came later. But it showed the core move: replace a person in the intersection with electrically visible, standardized right-of-way commands. That makes the Utah work real, but the contested documentary trail and limited direct standardization path make B tier a better fit than A.

## The hard problem they solved

Early automobile-era streets were mixed systems: pedestrians, horses, wagons, streetcars, bicycles, and cars moved through the same intersections without the mature right-of-way rules and signal standards that later drivers take for granted.

The hard problem was not only building a light. It was inventing a visible, trusted control language for people approaching from different directions. Wire's design borrowed from railroad and maritime signaling and adapted that logic to city streets.

## Lasting impact

- Wire's 1912 Salt Lake City signal is remembered by UDOT as the first traffic signal or first electric traffic signal
- UDOT maintains a Lester Wire collection and has displayed replicas of the original signal
- Salt Lake City installed an interconnected traffic-signal system in 1917, with multiple intersections controlled from a manual switch
- Later traffic-signal systems added patents, automation, yellow phases, pedestrian signals, vehicle detection, and networked operations, but kept the basic idea of electric intersection right-of-way control

## Key people

- **Lester Farnsworth Wire** - Salt Lake City police officer and traffic-bureau head who designed the 1912 signal
- **Salt Lake City Police Department** - institution that used the signal to manage downtown traffic
- **Utah Department of Transportation** - later steward of Wire's traffic-signal history and replicas
- **James Hoge, Garrett Morgan, William Potts, and other traffic-signal inventors** - non-Utah contributors whose patented and three-color systems helped standardize later traffic control

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. Wire built and installed his signal in Salt Lake City for a Salt Lake City intersection. The later 1917 interconnected signal system was also installed in Salt Lake City. The story belongs to Utah's early twentieth-century urban infrastructure, even though later commercialization and standardization happened elsewhere.

## Caveats

- The "first traffic light" claim depends on definitions. London had an earlier gas-lit semaphore signal in 1868, and Cleveland's 1914 electric system based on James Hoge's design is often cited as the first widely recognized electric traffic signal installation
- Wire did not patent his device, so the documentary trail is thinner than for patented systems
- Modern traffic lights owe much to later three-color, automated, interconnected, pedestrian, and actuated-control innovations
- The original models were lost, so current displays rely on replicas and archival accounts
