# Hybrid Flywheel-Battery Modular Power Plants

**Speculative tier:** P-B - commercially deployed and scaling quickly, but needs independent long-term proof across grid and industrial sites
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Energy storage / distributed grid infrastructure
**Era:** 2021-present
**Location:** South Salt Lake, UT

## What it is

Hybrid flywheel-battery modular power plants pair fast mechanical energy storage with chemical batteries and software controls. Torus is the Utah example: it builds small inertial power systems that can sit at the edge of the grid or behind the meter, respond to signals in milliseconds, and provide power quality, peak shaving, backup, and grid services.

The interesting work is the combination. Flywheels are good at fast, frequent power bursts; batteries are good at longer-duration delivery. A hybrid system can use the flywheel for spikes and grid-stabilizing work while preserving battery life for sustained energy.

## Why this might become great work

Electricity demand is rising faster than grid infrastructure can be built, especially around AI data centers, electrification, industrial loads, and distribution-level constraints. If modular inertial storage can be deployed quickly at customer sites and aggregated into utility programs, it could become a practical layer of grid modernization.

The world-scale claim is not that Utah invented flywheels. It is that a Utah company might make inertial storage boring, modular, financeable, and useful at the exact moment the grid needs fast local power quality and demand flexibility.

## Signals so far

- Torus is based in South Salt Lake and was founded in 2021
- Its Torus Spin / Nova Spin flywheel stores energy kinetically and is paired with Torus Pulse chemical batteries
- The company reports millisecond response, 25-year service life for the flywheel, high recyclability, and support for frequency regulation, voltage support, black start, and peak-demand shaving
- In 2025, Torus announced a $200 million investment from Magnetar to accelerate modular power-plant deployment
- Torus reports partnerships and demand-response commitments with PacifiCorp, including a 70 MW agreement and an MOU covering up to 500 MW
- The company says it is preparing a 540,000-square-foot GigaOne manufacturing campus in Salt Lake City, with production intended to scale beyond one gigawatt per quarter within three years

## What would upgrade this

- Independent measurement of uptime, response, energy savings, and battery-life extension across many customer sites
- Utility-scale aggregation that performs reliably during grid events
- Bankable project-finance structures for fleets of modular plants
- Evidence that hybrid flywheel-battery systems beat battery-only alternatives on total cost and operational value

## Caveats

- Many performance claims are company-reported
- Flywheels have attractive physics but have not historically displaced chemical batteries at broad scale
- Behind-the-meter economics depend on tariffs, demand charges, incentives, and utility program design
- Data-center demand may create urgency, but it can also create overheated infrastructure narratives
