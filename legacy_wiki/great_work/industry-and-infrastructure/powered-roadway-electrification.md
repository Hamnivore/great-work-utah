# Powered Roadway Electrification

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a serious Utah-led infrastructure research center with strong technical demonstrations, but still short of scaled public-road adoption
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Research center / transportation electrification infrastructure
**Era:** 2020-present
**Location:** Logan and North Logan, UT

## What it is

ASPIRE, headquartered at Utah State University, is an NSF Engineering Research Center developing powered infrastructure for roadway electrification. Its core idea is that vehicle electrification should not depend only on bigger batteries and scattered plug-in chargers. Charging can be built into roads, depots, parking structures, freight routes, grids, and traffic systems so that electric vehicles receive energy where they already move and wait.

The Utah work includes dynamic wireless charging test infrastructure at USU's ASPIRE track in North Logan and high-power wireless charging for heavy vehicles. The center brings together USU, Purdue, the University of Colorado, the University of Texas at El Paso, the University of Auckland, industry partners, utilities, and transportation agencies.

## Why this might become great work

Heavy trucks, buses, ports, airports, campuses, and rural routes are some of the hardest parts of transportation to electrify. Large batteries add cost and weight, depot charging can create grid peaks, and public fast-charging networks are not naturally shaped around freight duty cycles.

If ASPIRE's work helps make dynamic or depot wireless charging reliable, safe, standard, and cheap enough for real fleets, Utah would have helped define a new layer of transportation infrastructure: roads and logistics nodes that behave partly like fuel infrastructure and partly like grid assets.

## Signals so far

- NSF launched ASPIRE in 2020 as a Generation 4 Engineering Research Center led by Utah State University
- The ERC program lists ASPIRE's location as Logan, Utah, with USU as lead institution and Purdue, Colorado, and UTEP as core partners
- ASPIRE and Electreon announced a 50-meter dynamic in-road wireless charging demonstration at the USU ASPIRE track in North Logan
- The ERC program reports that ASPIRE developed a wireless megawatt charging pad for semi-trailer trucks
- A demonstration pad smaller than 2 square meters reportedly transferred 850 kW at 95% efficiency
- ASPIRE-related pilots include Utah truck and battery-electric train work, plus partner roadway-electrification projects in Florida, Michigan, and Indiana

## What would upgrade this

- Multi-year fleet operation with public uptime, efficiency, maintenance, safety, and cost data
- Public-road or depot deployments used by freight, transit, port, airport, or municipal fleets outside a demonstration setting
- Interoperability standards that let multiple vehicle and infrastructure vendors use the same charging ecosystem
- Independent total-cost comparisons versus plug-in depot charging, battery swapping, hydrogen, and conventional diesel operations
- Evidence that grid integration reduces peaks or improves asset utilization rather than shifting costs elsewhere

## Caveats

- Wireless roadway charging has a long history of compelling demos and hard deployment economics
- Embedded road hardware has to survive weather, pavement maintenance, utility coordination, standards friction, and public procurement cycles
- Electreon and other partners bring substantial non-Utah technology; the Utah claim is strongest for ASPIRE's center leadership, testbed, systems integration, and research ecosystem
- Electrified roads may be best for specific fleet corridors rather than general passenger-vehicle use
