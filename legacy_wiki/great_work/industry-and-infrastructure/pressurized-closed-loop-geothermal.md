# Pressurized Closed-Loop Geothermal

**Speculative tier:** P-B - technically plausible clean-firm-power approach with a Utah pilot, but the economics are not proven yet
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Advanced geothermal system / closed-loop power generation
**Era:** 2022-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City and Beaver / Millard counties, UT

## What it is

Rodatherm is developing a fully cased, pressurized, closed-loop geothermal system for hot sedimentary basins. The working fluid stays isolated inside the loop and is used directly in a power-generating turbine, making the system closer to a heat pump at depth than a conventional water-based geothermal reservoir.

The planned Utah work is a pilot system on BLM-managed land in Beaver and Millard counties, with Salt Lake City as one of the company's operating bases. The pilot is meant to validate the design at commercial scale and support a path toward a 100 MW project at the same site.

## Why this might become great work

Enhanced geothermal is exciting but hard: water use, reservoir uncertainty, induced seismicity, scaling, corrosion, and drilling cost all matter. A closed-loop system that extracts useful heat without producing reservoir fluids could reduce water use, contamination risk, and some reservoir-management problems.

If Rodatherm's approach works at attractive cost, it would expand where geothermal can be built, especially in places where open-loop geothermal is constrained by water, chemistry, permitting, or sensitive land use.

## Signals so far

- BLM approved the Rodatherm Geothermal Test Bed project in 2023 to test two closed geothermal loops and drill five wells on public land in Utah
- Rodatherm emerged from stealth with a $38 million Series A in September 2025, led by Evok Innovations with participation from TDK Ventures, Toyota Ventures, MCJ, and others
- The company claims its working-fluid cycle can achieve roughly 50% higher efficiency than water-based binary-cycle geothermal systems
- Rodatherm says the first Utah module will include secured offtake and generate production data for potential expansion to 100 MW
- TechCrunch framed the key question plainly: the design may be more efficient, but it still has to prove it is cheaper enough after drilling and installation costs

## What would upgrade this

- Successful Utah pilot operation with public power, uptime, temperature, flow, and maintenance data
- Independent validation of efficiency and levelized cost versus water-based EGS and other closed-loop competitors
- Expansion from pilot module to a financed multi-megawatt or 100 MW project
- Evidence that the system avoids fluid-loss, corrosion, scaling, induced-seismicity, or water-use problems that limit other geothermal approaches
- Replication in another sedimentary basin

## Caveats

- Closed-loop geothermal has a long history of elegant physics meeting difficult heat-transfer economics
- The pilot still has to prove that extra casing, working fluid, and drilling complexity are worth it
- Company claims about efficiency and cost need independent operating data
- Fervo, XGS, Sage, Quaise, Eavor, and others are all racing toward different geothermal architectures
