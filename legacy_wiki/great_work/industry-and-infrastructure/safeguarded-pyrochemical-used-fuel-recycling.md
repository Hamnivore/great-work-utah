# Safeguarded Pyrochemical Used-Fuel Recycling

**Speculative tier:** P-C - an early lab-stage fuel-cycle idea with a clear technical target, but far from demonstrated or deployable
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Nuclear fuel-cycle process / molten-salt electrochemical separation
**Era:** 2020-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Safeguarded pyrochemical used-fuel recycling is the attempt to convert spent light-water-reactor oxide fuel into a uranium/transuranic product for advanced reactors using molten salt and electrochemistry, while designing the process around material accountability from the start.

The Utah case is University of Utah materials science and engineering professor Michael Simpson's ARPA-E CURIE project. The process target is to dissolve oxide used nuclear fuel in molten lithium chloride-potassium chloride salt, then electrochemically recover uranium and transuranic metal on a cathode. ARPA-E describes the concept as a two-step separation that could occur in a single reaction vessel and, if successful, would be the first pyrochemical process to feature an input accountability tank for safeguards.

## Why this might become great work

Advanced nuclear has a fuel-cycle problem as much as a reactor-design problem. Used fuel contains energy-rich uranium and transuranic actinides, but recycling it is technically, politically, and proliferation-sensitive. Conventional aqueous reprocessing can separate pure plutonium, which is one reason the U.S. has avoided commercial PUREX-style reprocessing.

Pyroprocessing is attractive because uranium, plutonium, and minor actinides can be recovered together rather than as a pure plutonium stream. If a Utah-developed process could directly handle commercial oxide fuel, reduce handling steps, integrate safeguards, and produce usable feedstock for sodium-cooled fast reactors or molten-salt-fueled reactors, it would matter to nuclear waste strategy and advanced-reactor fuel supply.

## Signals so far

- ARPA-E awarded the University of Utah roughly $1.45 million through the CURIE program for "Pyrochemical Dissolution of LWR Spent Fuel with Actinide Recovery for Advanced Reactors"
- The project aims to dissolve oxide used nuclear fuel in molten LiCl-KCl and recover uranium/transuranic metal electrochemically
- ARPA-E says the concept could reduce material handling, lower space requirements, consolidate processing units, and use a single solvent salt
- University of Utah reporting says the project's technical goals are nearly complete dissolution of spent oxide fuel followed by near complete electrochemical recovery of U/TRU
- The Utah team explicitly frames complete dissolution and input accountability as key to safeguards against diversion
- The approach grew from Simpson lab work with graduate student Jarom Chamberlain on chlorinating uranium oxide in molten salt

## What would upgrade this

- Bench-scale data showing near-complete oxide-fuel dissolution and near-complete U/TRU recovery under realistic molten-salt conditions
- Measured fission-product behavior, recovered-product composition, and waste-form implications
- Demonstrated material accountancy that can satisfy independent safeguards experts
- Peer-reviewed replication or national-lab validation beyond depleted-uranium surrogates
- A credible pathway from lab cells to hot-cell tests with irradiated commercial oxide fuel
- Explicit proliferation-resistance review showing that the process avoids pure plutonium streams and is monitorable in practice

## Caveats

- This is a research project, not a working nuclear recycling plant
- Pyroprocessing has a long global history; the Utah contribution would be a specific oxide-fuel dissolution, recovery, and safeguards architecture
- Nuclear fuel recycling is technically hard, heavily regulated, politically exposed, and expensive to demonstrate
- Safeguards claims are only meaningful if independent nuclear-material-accountancy experts can verify them under realistic operating conditions
- Even successful recycling may not be economically attractive without advanced reactors that actually need and qualify the recovered fuel
