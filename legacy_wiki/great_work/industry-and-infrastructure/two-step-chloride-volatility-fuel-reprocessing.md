# Two-Step Chloride Volatility Fuel Reprocessing

**Speculative tier:** P-C - a Utah-led ARPA-E nuclear fuel-cycle process with clear chemistry targets and active lab signals, but still at simulated-fuel and early batch-demonstration scale
**Domain:** Industry & Infrastructure
**Type:** Nuclear fuel-cycle process / advanced-reactor waste reduction / chloride volatility separation
**Era:** 2022-present
**Location:** Provo, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - Brigham Young University in Provo is the ARPA-E ONWARDS awardee, and Devin Rappleye's BYU PyRO lab is carrying the molten-salt and chloride-volatility research program.

## What it is

Two-step chloride volatility, or TSCV, is a proposed dry reprocessing method for advanced-reactor used nuclear fuel. Instead of dissolving used fuel in aqueous solvents, the process uses gaseous chlorinating chemistry and volatility differences among uranium and transuranic chlorides. BYU's ARPA-E project is trying to quantify those volatility relationships in simulated used-fuel mixtures, optimize uranium/transuranic extraction, and demonstrate the process at up to one-kilogram batch size.

The technical ambition is a solventless separation scheme that co-extracts uranium and transuranic elements rather than creating a pure plutonium stream, while recycling hydrogen chloride and chlorine gases inside the process.

## Why this might become great work

Advanced reactors often promise better fuel use and smaller waste burdens, but those promises depend on credible fuel-cycle infrastructure. If used fuel can be separated, recycled, and packaged with fewer liquid waste streams and lower repository burden, advanced reactors become easier to justify technically and politically.

The Utah version matters if BYU can turn chloride volatility from a plausible chemistry into a measured, scalable, safeguards-compatible process. A working route would not solve nuclear waste by itself, but it could become one piece of the physical infrastructure behind advanced-reactor deployment.

## Signals so far

- DOE announced BYU as one of eleven ARPA-E ONWARDS awardees in 2022, with a $900,217 award to reduce risk and uncertainty in a separation process for used nuclear fuel
- ARPA-E's ONWARDS project description says BYU will quantify volatility of uranium/transuranic chlorides in simulated used-fuel mixtures, optimize process parameters, and demonstrate TSCV up to one-kilogram batch size
- ARPA-E describes TSCV as a solventless separation scheme that co-extracts uranium and transuranics, avoids high-level liquid waste from solvent reprocessing, and recycles hydrogen chloride and chlorine gases
- DOE Environmental Management webinar material describes BYU's project target as co-recovering uranium and transuranic elements at an equivalent rate of at least 1 kg/day while reducing waste to roughly 5% of used nuclear fuel
- BYU's PyRO lab publication list shows a continuing chloride-volatility and molten-salt program, including 2023-2025 conference presentations on a novel chloride volatility scheme, in-situ chlorine generation, chloride volatility products, and progress on TSCV for advanced-reactor fuel
- The same lab's recent papers cover molten chloride electrochemistry, chloride reference electrodes, chlorine evolution, and vapor-pressure correlations for lanthanide and actinide chlorides, all adjacent to the measurement problems TSCV has to solve

## What would upgrade this

- Public bench results showing selective U/TRU chloride volatility in realistic simulated advanced-reactor fuel mixtures
- A one-kilogram batch demonstration with mass balance, fission-product behavior, gas recycle, and waste-stream accounting
- Independent national-lab or peer-reviewed validation of the thermodynamic and kinetic measurements
- Safeguards review showing how co-recovery, gas handling, and process monitoring avoid proliferation-sensitive streams
- Hot-cell work with irradiated material or a credible path from simulated fuel to licensed nuclear-material testing

## Caveats

- The work is early, and public sources mostly describe goals, conference signals, and supporting molten-salt chemistry rather than completed reprocessing performance
- Chloride and fluoride volatility concepts have older international histories; the Utah claim is the specific BYU TSCV risk-reduction and measurement program
- Advanced-reactor fuel cycles are entangled with licensing, safeguards, economics, and public acceptance, not just separations chemistry
- Any real used-fuel process would need rigorous material accountancy, off-gas treatment, corrosion control, worker safety, and waste-form integration
