# Bestvina-Brady Groups

**Tier:** B - a named Utah-origin construction in geometric group theory that produced important examples with exotic finiteness properties
**Domain:** Mathematics & Theoretical Science
**Type:** Mathematical construction / theorem / research method
**Era:** 1990s-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Mladen Bestvina and Noel Brady's 1997 paper "Morse theory and finiteness properties of groups" introduced what are now called Bestvina-Brady groups: kernels of natural homomorphisms from right-angled Artin groups to the integers. These groups connect the algebraic finiteness properties of a group to the topology of a simplicial complex built from a graph.

The paper used a form of Morse theory on cubical complexes to construct groups with unusual combinations of properties. In particular, Bestvina and Brady found an example of a group that is homologically finite in a strong sense, but not finitely presented.

## Why it mattered

Geometric group theory studies abstract groups by making them act on spaces. Bestvina-Brady groups gave mathematicians a clean machine for translating topology into group-theoretic finiteness phenomena. Instead of isolated examples, the construction produced families whose behavior could be read from the connectivity of associated flag complexes.

That mattered because finiteness properties are basic questions about how complicated a group is: whether it can be described with finitely many generators, finitely many relations, or finite higher-dimensional data. Bestvina-Brady groups showed that these levels of finiteness can split apart in subtle ways.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was building explicit groups that separate homological and geometric finiteness conditions. A group can be finitely generated, finitely presented, or have a classifying space with finite skeleta in higher dimensions; these conditions look similar at first, but they diverge.

Bestvina and Brady's key move was to apply Morse theory to right-angled Artin groups acting on cubical complexes. The topology of ascending and descending links controlled the finiteness properties of the kernel group. This gave a flexible construction rather than a one-off counterexample.

## Lasting impact

- "Bestvina-Brady groups" became a standard term in geometric group theory
- The construction remains a testbed for finiteness properties, right-angled Artin groups, CAT(0) geometry, Dehn functions, and related questions
- Later papers study algebraic invariants, subgroups, residual properties, and Dehn functions of Bestvina-Brady groups
- Bestvina's broader Utah work helped make the University of Utah a visible node in geometric topology and geometric group theory

## Key people

- **Mladen Bestvina** - University of Utah mathematician and major figure in geometric group theory
- **Noel Brady** - University of Utah coauthor on the original Bestvina-Brady construction
- **Right-angled Artin group and geometric group theory communities** - subsequent researchers who developed the construction's consequences and variants

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. The original Inventiones Mathematicae paper lists both Bestvina and Brady with the University of Utah Department of Mathematics. Bestvina joined the University of Utah in 1993 and became a distinguished professor there; the department later highlighted his geometric group theory work and invited plenary-level recognition.

## Caveats

- This is pure mathematics, so the impact is field-shaping rather than technological
- The entry focuses on a named construction and method, not all of Bestvina's career
- Some related examples and finiteness phenomena predate Bestvina-Brady groups, including Stallings-Bieri groups
- Geometric group theory is broad and international; the Utah work is one important node in a much larger field
