# Cannon-Thurston Maps and Cannon's Conjecture

**Tier:** B - field-shaping mathematical ideas with a real Utah/BYU chapter, but with important collaborative and timing caveats
**Domain:** Mathematics & Theoretical Science
**Type:** Theorem / conjecture / research program
**Era:** 1980s-present; BYU era 1986-2012
**Location:** Brigham Young University, Provo, UT; University of Utah Ph.D. lineage

## What it was

James W. Cannon is a low-dimensional topologist and geometric group theorist whose name is attached to two durable ideas at the boundary between topology, geometry, and group theory: Cannon-Thurston maps and Cannon's conjecture. Cannon received his Ph.D. from the University of Utah in 1969, spent a major middle-career period at the University of Wisconsin, and became an Orson Pratt Professor of Mathematics at BYU in 1986.

The Cannon-Thurston map began with Cannon and William Thurston's mid-1980s work on "Group invariant Peano curves," eventually published in *Geometry & Topology* in 2007. The result showed that, for a closed hyperbolic 3-manifold fibering over the circle, the lifted inclusion of the hyperbolic surface fiber into hyperbolic 3-space extends continuously to the boundary, producing an equivariant space-filling curve from a circle onto a sphere.

Cannon's conjecture, developed in the 1990s and associated especially with Cannon's BYU-era work with collaborators such as Eric Swenson, asks whether every word-hyperbolic group with boundary homeomorphic to the 2-sphere is essentially a Kleinian group: equivalently, whether it acts geometrically on hyperbolic 3-space. It remains one of the central open problems linking geometric group theory, 3-manifold topology, and analysis on metric spaces.

## Why it mattered

These ideas made the boundary of a group into a place where deep geometry can be seen. Cannon-Thurston maps showed that wildly distorted inclusions can still have well-behaved boundary extensions, turning a paradoxical circle-to-sphere situation into a precise object of study. Cannon's conjecture gave geometric group theorists a crisp test case for whether the topology and analysis of a group's boundary can force the group to come from hyperbolic 3-geometry.

Without this line of work, a large body of later research on hyperbolic group boundaries, Kleinian groups, ending laminations, local connectivity of limit sets, and combinatorial conformal geometry would have had a less focused organizing problem.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard conceptual problem was how to compare the large-scale geometry of a group or surface subgroup with the topology visible "at infinity." In the Cannon-Thurston setting, the subgroup is highly distorted: the universal cover of the fiber surface sits inside hyperbolic 3-space in a way that does not behave like a nice embedded disk at infinity. The theorem showed that the boundary behavior could still be continuous and equivariant.

Cannon's conjecture sharpened a different hard problem: deciding when the visual boundary of an abstract hyperbolic group is enough to recover classical hyperbolic 3-dimensional geometry. That is still open, which is part of why the conjecture remains so influential.

## Lasting impact

- "Cannon-Thurston map" is now a named object in geometric group theory and Kleinian-group theory
- The original Cannon-Thurston preprint circulated for decades before publication and was cited by many later papers before it formally appeared
- Cannon's conjecture remains a benchmark open problem in geometric group theory
- The conjecture motivated work on combinatorial conformal geometry, hyperbolic group boundaries, quasiconformal analysis, and surface subgroups
- BYU became one of the Utah homes of geometric topology and geometric group theory through Cannon's long professorship and collaborators

## Key people

- **James W. Cannon** - University of Utah Ph.D.; BYU Orson Pratt Professor; central figure behind Cannon-Thurston maps, Cannon's conjecture, and related combinatorial conformal geometry
- **William P. Thurston** - collaborator on the original Cannon-Thurston map theorem; towering figure in 3-manifold geometry
- **Eric L. Swenson** - BYU collaborator on Cannon's conjecture and hyperbolic group boundary work
- **William Floyd and Walter Parry** - BYU collaborators on finite subdivision rules and combinatorial conformal approaches related to Cannon's program
- **Mahan Mj, Brian Bowditch, Yair Minsky, Curt McMullen, Vladimir Markovic, and others** - later researchers who extended or attacked the Cannon-Thurston and Cannon-conjecture programs

## Caveats

- The Utah claim is not cleanly "the theorem was discovered in Utah." Cannon-Thurston maps originated in a mid-1980s collaboration with Thurston, just before or around Cannon's move from Wisconsin to BYU, and the 2007 paper reflects a long-circulated preprint.
- Cannon's conjecture has strong BYU-era ties, but it is still open, so its importance is as a field-organizing conjecture rather than as a solved theorem.
- The work is collaborative and international in its later development. Utah's role is best understood as Cannon's long BYU mathematical program and institutional presence, not exclusive ownership of the field.
