# Hacon-McKernan and the Minimal Model Program

**Tier:** A - a transformational contribution to birational algebraic geometry, with a major Utah-based coauthor and clear international recognition
**Domain:** Mathematics & Theoretical Science
**Type:** Theorem / research program
**Era:** 2000s-2010s
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT; collaboration with UC Santa Barbara, UC San Diego, Cambridge, and Imperial College

## What it was

Christopher D. Hacon, a University of Utah mathematician, was one of the central contributors to major advances in higher-dimensional birational algebraic geometry and the minimal model program. With James McKernan, and in important joint work with Caucher Birkar and Paolo Cascini, Hacon helped prove results that had been sought for decades: the existence of minimal models for broad classes of algebraic varieties and the finite generation of canonical rings.

The best-known paper in this line, "Existence of minimal models for varieties of log general type," proved that the canonical ring of a smooth projective variety is finitely generated. In geometric terms, it gave mathematicians a much stronger way to classify complicated higher-dimensional shapes defined by polynomial equations.

## Why it mattered

Algebraic geometry studies spaces cut out by polynomial equations. For curves and surfaces, mathematicians had powerful classification theories. In higher dimensions, classification became far harder: spaces can have singularities, different birational models, and many possible transformations that preserve most of the geometry while changing the presentation.

The minimal model program is a grand strategy for taming that complexity. It tries to replace a complicated variety with a simpler birational model that preserves essential information, somewhat like simplifying a fraction without changing its value. Hacon, McKernan, Birkar, and Cascini proved key pieces needed to make that strategy work in high dimensions.

The impact is internal to mathematics rather than technological, but it is field-level: the work changed what algebraic geometers could assume, prove, and build on.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was controlling higher-dimensional geometry when the usual low-dimensional intuition breaks. Minimal models are not unique in a naive sense, singularities are unavoidable, and the process of simplifying a variety can in principle fail to terminate or produce objects that are too wild to classify.

The BCHM work combined birational geometry, singularity theory, convex geometry, vanishing theorems, and careful control of log pairs to prove finite generation and existence results that had resisted earlier attacks. These results helped turn the minimal model program from a conjectural roadmap into a working infrastructure for broad classes of varieties.

## Lasting impact

- The BCHM finite-generation theorem became a standard reference point in modern birational geometry
- Hacon and McKernan received the 2018 Breakthrough Prize in Mathematics for "transformational contributions to birational algebraic geometry, especially to the minimal model program in all dimensions"
- Hacon was elected to the National Academy of Sciences in 2018 and the Royal Society in 2019
- The work strengthened the University of Utah's reputation as a serious node in algebraic geometry
- Later research in moduli spaces, Fano varieties, singularities, and higher-dimensional classification builds on this line of work

## Key people

- **Christopher D. Hacon** - University of Utah mathematician; co-recipient of the 2018 Breakthrough Prize in Mathematics
- **James McKernan** - Hacon's major collaborator and co-recipient of the 2018 Breakthrough Prize
- **Caucher Birkar and Paolo Cascini** - coauthors on the BCHM finite-generation/minimal-model paper
- **Janos Kollar, Shigefumi Mori, Vyacheslav Shokurov, Yujiro Kawamata, Miles Reid, Yum-Tong Siu, and many others** - earlier and parallel contributors to the minimal model program

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is substantial but collaborative. Hacon has been a University of Utah professor, and the major BCHM paper lists him with the University of Utah Department of Mathematics. The Breakthrough Prize citation named him at the University of Utah, and Utah's College of Science identifies his work as one of the university's top scientific recognitions.

This is not a solely Utah theorem. It is international mathematics with a central Utah-based contributor.

## Caveats

- The minimal model program is a vast international research program; no single person or institution owns it
- The most important BCHM results were collaborative and involved coauthors based outside Utah
- The work is mathematically world-class, but its impact is mostly inside pure mathematics rather than a direct industrial or consumer technology
- Some underlying ideas and conjectures were developed over decades before the 2000s breakthroughs
