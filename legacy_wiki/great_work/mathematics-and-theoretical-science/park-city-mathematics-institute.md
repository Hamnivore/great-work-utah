# Park City Mathematics Institute

**Tier:** B - a durable Utah-born mathematics institute that became one of the Institute for Advanced Study's major outreach programs and a distinctive model for linking research mathematics, students, faculty, and teachers
**Domain:** Mathematics & Theoretical Science
**Type:** Institute / summer research and education program
**Era:** 1991-present
**Location:** Begun at the University of Utah; held in Park City, UT

## What it was

The Park City Mathematics Institute, now the IAS/Park City Mathematics Institute, is a three-week residential summer institute held in Park City, Utah. It brings together research mathematicians, postdocs, graduate students, undergraduates, undergraduate faculty, mathematics education researchers, and secondary-school teachers around a shared annual mathematical theme.

PCMI began in 1991 as one of the National Science Foundation's Regional Geometry Institutes, led by Herb Clemens at the University of Utah with Dan Freed and Karen Uhlenbeck at the University of Texas at Austin. In 1993 the founders asked the Institute for Advanced Study to take institutional responsibility; by 1994 the program had become IAS/PCMI.

## Why it mattered

Most advanced mathematics programs separate research, graduate training, undergraduate teaching, and K-12 teacher development into different rooms, institutions, and status hierarchies. PCMI made the opposite bet: put the whole mathematical community in one place, around one serious topic, and let the research and education sides see each other at work.

That model mattered because it treated mathematical research culture and mathematical teaching culture as parts of the same ecosystem. Over decades, PCMI became a recognized venue where high-level topics could be taught intensively while teachers and faculty developed deeper mathematical knowledge and professional networks.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was social and institutional as much as technical. It is difficult to make a program valuable to Fields Medal-level researchers, graduate students, undergraduates, undergraduate faculty, education researchers, and classroom teachers at the same time. Too much research intensity can make teachers peripheral; too much professional-development framing can make researchers disengage.

PCMI's solution was parallel programming around a common theme, with cross-program lectures, shared spaces, informal mathematical contact, and a structure that respected different levels without isolating them completely.

## Lasting impact

- PCMI has run since 1991, giving it unusual continuity among U.S. mathematics summer programs
- It became a major outreach program of the Institute for Advanced Study while remaining physically rooted in Park City
- Its lecture series, summer schools, and topic archives document decades of graduate-level mathematical exposition
- The teacher and faculty programs helped make serious mathematical professional development part of the same environment as research mathematics
- The program helped keep Utah visible in the national and international mathematics community beyond the output of individual departments

## Key people

- **Herbert Clemens** - University of Utah mathematician; leader of the original NSF Regional Geometry Institute proposal and early PCMI formation
- **Dan Freed and Karen Uhlenbeck** - University of Texas mathematicians and founding collaborators who helped shape the institute's integrated research-and-education vision
- **Institute for Advanced Study leadership and staff** - provided the long-term institutional home beginning in the 1990s
- **PCMI organizers, lecturers, teachers, and participants** - the rotating community that makes the institute work across different mathematical themes

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is real but institutional rather than exclusive. PCMI began at the University of Utah, grew out of a Utah-led NSF Regional Geometry Institute, and has held its flagship summer session in Park City. Its long-term institutional sponsor is the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, so the mature program is best understood as an IAS program with Utah origins and a continuing Utah venue.

## Caveats

- PCMI is not a mathematical discovery; its impact is through training, networks, exposition, and professional culture
- The strongest institutional credit after 1994 belongs partly to the Institute for Advanced Study, not just to Utah
- Measuring educational and community impact is harder than measuring citations to a theorem
- It belongs below theorem-level entries such as Hacon-McKernan because the world-changing claim is diffuse
