# 3D-Printed Microfluidics for Lab-on-a-Chip Devices

**Speculative tier:** P-A - a strong Utah-originated technical platform with peer-reviewed breakthroughs and field-wide relevance; upgrade depends on broad adoption in diagnostics, biology, or microfluidic manufacturing
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Microfabrication method / lab-on-a-chip platform
**Era:** 2015-present
**Location:** Provo, UT

## What it is

BYU researchers developed high-resolution 3D printing methods for making truly microfluidic lab-on-a-chip devices: chips with tiny channels, valves, pumps, mixers, serial diluters, and integrated fluidic control features printed directly rather than assembled through conventional cleanroom-style soft lithography.

The work centers on custom DLP stereolithography printers, custom resins, optical-dose control, and multi-resolution printing methods that push 3D-printed microfluidics into the size range needed for serious biological and chemical assays. BYU groups led by Gregory Nordin, Adam Woolley, and collaborators have reported channels and valves at scales that were previously difficult or impossible for ordinary 3D printing.

## Why this might become great work

Microfluidics promised cheap, compact diagnostics and biology tools for decades, but fabrication has often remained slow, specialized, and unfriendly to iteration. If high-resolution 3D printing can make integrated microfluidic chips quickly and cheaply, it could change how labs prototype assays, build point-of-care diagnostic devices, run cell experiments, and manufacture small-batch biomedical tools.

The especially interesting Utah contribution is not just printing plastic channels. It is collapsing design, fabrication, valves, pumps, mixers, and integration into a digital manufacturing workflow.

## Signals so far

- BYU reported high-density 3D printed microfluidic valves, pumps, and multiplexers in *Lab on a Chip* in 2016
- BYU announced what it described as the first truly microfluidic 3D-printed lab-on-a-chip device in 2017, with channel cross sections well below 100 micrometers
- A 2021 *Nature Communications* paper introduced spatially and optically tailored 3D printing for highly miniaturized and integrated microfluidics, including 15 micrometer by 15 micrometer valves
- A 2026 *Microsystems & Nanoengineering* paper reported fast multi-resolution 3D printing enabling 2 micrometer channels and ultra-compact mixers
- Funding signals include NIH and NSF support for the platform

## What would upgrade this

- Widespread adoption by biology, diagnostics, or organ-on-chip labs outside BYU
- Commercial printers, resins, or service workflows based on the Utah methods
- Demonstrated point-of-care diagnostic or biomedical product built around these chips
- Citations and replication showing the methods become a standard route for integrated microfluidics

## Caveats

- Microfluidics is full of beautiful prototypes that never become robust products
- Materials, biocompatibility, optical clarity, clogging, sealing, and manufacturing repeatability can limit adoption
- The work may remain a powerful research fabrication method without becoming a broad commercial platform
