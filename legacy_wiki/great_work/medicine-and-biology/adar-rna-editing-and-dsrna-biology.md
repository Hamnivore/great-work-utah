# ADAR RNA Editing and Double-Stranded RNA Biology

**Tier:** A - a University of Utah RNA biology program that helped define how cells edit double-stranded RNA, distinguish self from viral RNA, and use dsRNA-processing enzymes in gene regulation and immunity
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Basic research program / RNA biology / molecular mechanism
**Era:** 1990s-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Brenda Bass's University of Utah lab became one of the central research programs in double-stranded RNA biology. The work focused on ADAR enzymes, which edit RNA by converting adenosine to inosine in double-stranded RNA, and on related dsRNA-processing systems such as Dicer and antiviral RNA-sensing pathways.

The original discovery of the dsRNA-modifying activity happened during Bass's postdoctoral work with Harold Weintraub at Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center, so the Utah claim is not that the entire field began in Salt Lake City. The Utah claim is that Bass's independent lab helped turn a surprising biochemical activity into a sustained field: defining ADAR specificity, natural substrates, structures, biological relevance, dsRNA mapping, and links between RNA editing, gene silencing, innate immunity, and disease.

## Why it mattered

RNA is not just a passive copy of DNA. ADAR editing can change codons, regulate RNA fate, alter neuronal proteins, tune immune recognition, and prevent endogenous double-stranded RNAs from being mistaken for viral infection. Dicer and related dsRNA pathways are also central to RNA interference, genome defense, and antiviral biology.

Without this line of work, the modern picture of RNA regulation would be poorer: fewer mechanisms connecting RNA sequence, RNA structure, innate immunity, neurological function, and therapeutic targeting. ADARs are now part of RNA biology textbooks and a growing area of RNA-therapeutics interest.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was that double-stranded RNA is both information and danger signal. Cells make their own structured RNAs, viruses make dsRNA-like molecules, and many enzymes recognize shape rather than a clean sequence barcode. Understanding how editing enzymes pick sites, how Dicer handles substrates, and how cells tell self from non-self required biochemistry, genetics, structural biology, genomics, and careful model-building.

Bass's Utah lab contributed across that stack: enzyme specificity, dsRNA-binding proteins, ADAR structure and cofactor work, Dicer models, dsRNAome mapping, and mechanistic links to innate immune regulation.

## Lasting impact

- ADAR RNA editing is now a core mechanism in RNA biology, especially in nervous-system function and innate-immune regulation
- Bass's work helped define ADAR specificity and natural substrates, not merely the existence of RNA editing activity
- Her lab and collaborators contributed structural insight into ADARs and Dicer-dsRNA interactions
- The program helped establish models for Dicer's role in RNA silencing
- The broader field now informs RNA modification mapping, antiviral immunity, inflammatory disease biology, and efforts to engineer RNA-editing therapeutics
- Bass was elected to the National Academy of Sciences and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, received NIH Pioneer and Transformative Research awards, and was selected for the RNA Society's 2026 Lifetime Achievement in Science Award

## Key people

- **Brenda L. Bass** - University of Utah biochemist whose lab drove much of the Utah dsRNA, ADAR, and Dicer work
- **Harold Weintraub** - Fred Hutchinson mentor in whose lab Bass made the initial dsRNA-modifying-activity discovery before moving to Utah
- **University of Utah Department of Biochemistry** - institutional home for the long-running research program
- **Bass lab trainees and collaborators** - contributors to ADAR specificity, dsRNAome, Dicer, structural, and innate-immunity work

## Caveats

- The first discovery of the ADAR-like dsRNA-modifying activity was not made in Utah; it came from Bass's postdoctoral work in Seattle
- ADAR biology is a distributed international field, with major contributions from Mary O'Connell, Kazuko Nishikura, and many other non-Utah researchers
- This is basic research impact rather than a Utah-built product or clinical intervention
- The strongest Utah claim is about field-shaping follow-through: mechanism, substrates, structures, biological relevance, and sustained training
