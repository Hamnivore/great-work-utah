# Aqueous Ionic Liquid Embolic Device

**Speculative tier:** P-B - Utah-built liquid embolic platform with peer-reviewed first-in-human data and an FDA IDE pivotal-trial pathway, but no marketing clearance or broad adoption yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / embolization material / interventional radiology platform
**Era:** 2015-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - Fluidx Medical Technology is based in Salt Lake City, and the peer-reviewed GPX clinical paper identifies the device as Fluidx's Salt Lake City embolic agent; University of Utah biomedical-engineering authors also contributed to the material-characterization work.

## What it is

The GPX Embolic Device is a water-based, in situ solidifying embolic material for catheter-based vessel occlusion. Instead of relying on particles, coils, solvent-based liquid embolics, or operator-mixed glue, GPX is designed as a ready-to-use ionic polymer solution that solidifies when delivered into blood, where counterions dissipate and the polymer forms an occlusive gel.

The specific Utah work is the embolic material and delivery platform, not embolization as a procedure. Fluidx is trying to make a controllable, radiopaque, simple-prep embolic that can penetrate distal vessel beds for uses such as tumor devascularization, renal embolization, portal-vein embolization, hemorrhage control, and possibly later neurovascular indications.

## Why this might become great work

Embolization is a major interventional-radiology tool, but current devices involve tradeoffs. Particles can be hard to visualize and may reflux or clog catheters; coils can be expensive or incomplete for distal beds; solvent-based liquid embolics can require special technique and carry catheter-entrapment, toxicity, or preparation issues.

If a single easy-to-prepare embolic gel can reliably reach distal vasculature, remain visible, avoid catheter entrapment, and work across tumor, bleeding, renal, portal, and neurovascular procedures, it could become a platform technology rather than a niche device. That would matter in oncology, trauma, hepatobiliary surgery planning, vascular malformations, and emergency bleeding control.

## Signals so far

- A 2024 Journal of Vascular and Interventional Radiology first-in-human early feasibility study evaluated GPX in 17 patients needing deep distal embolization and reported 100% technical success with immediate full target-region occlusion on the first post-delivery angiogram
- The same study reported no unanticipated adverse events, 15 of 17 patients free from device-related severe adverse events, and persistent occlusion in all six patients with follow-up imaging available at about 30 days
- A 2023 Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology material-characterization paper included University of Utah Biomedical Engineering and Fluidx authors and described GPX as a versatile in situ solidifying embolic platform
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists a 114-patient, prospective, multicenter, multinational pivotal GPX study for distal peripheral embolization in vascular tumors, renal embolization, and portal-vein branches
- CMS lists the GPX Trial as IDE G230312 / NCT06453642, category B, with CMS approval dated July 24, 2024
- Fluidx reports that the pivotal trial enrolled 114 patients across 18 sites in the United States, Canada, and New Zealand, with topline results presented at SIR 2026
- The company still states that GPX, ULTRA, and IMPASS are under development and do not have marketing clearance or approval in any market

## What would upgrade this

- FDA clearance or approval for GPX with a clear indication and public summary of safety and effectiveness
- Peer-reviewed pivotal results showing low major-adverse-event rates, reliable target occlusion, manageable reflux/catheter issues, and durable follow-up beyond 30 days
- Independent comparative evidence against particles, coils, nBCA, Onyx-type materials, or other liquid embolics in specific use cases
- Routine use in oncology, trauma, renal, portal-vein, or neurovascular embolization with evidence of shorter procedures, fewer adjunctive devices, lower costs, better tumor devascularization, better hypertrophy after portal-vein embolization, or fewer reinterventions
- Evidence that the same material platform can safely support multiple device variants rather than one narrow indication

## Caveats

- The first-in-human study was small, single-arm, and included Fluidx employees and investigators with company relationships
- The pivotal GPX trial is also single-arm rather than randomized, so comparative claims will need separate evidence
- Thirty-day occlusion and procedural success are important but do not by themselves prove better clinical outcomes
- Embolization use cases are heterogeneous; a device that works well for renal angiomyolipoma or tumor devascularization may not automatically translate to hemorrhage, prostate, malformation, or neurovascular use
- FDA IDE and CMS category-B approval are development signals, not proof of safety, effectiveness, or adoption
