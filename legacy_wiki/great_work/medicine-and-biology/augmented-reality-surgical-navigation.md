# Augmented-Reality Surgical Navigation

**Speculative tier:** P-B - Utah-built AR surgical-planning and navigation systems with FDA clearances and early clinical literature, but still awaiting broad independent outcome evidence and routine hospital adoption
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Surgical navigation system / medical imaging software / augmented-reality device
**Era:** 2017-present
**Location:** American Fork and Provo, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - FDA records list Novarad Corporation at Utah addresses for OpenSight and VisAR, and the peer-reviewed spine-procedure paper includes Novarad and Provo affiliations.

## What it is

OpenSight and VisAR are augmented-reality surgical visualization and navigation systems from Utah medical-imaging company Novarad. The work takes CT, MRI, or other medical-imaging data and renders it as a patient-registered holographic overlay so surgeons can see planned anatomy, trajectories, targets, and instruments in spatial context instead of constantly translating between a monitor and the patient.

The specific Utah work is not generic AR enthusiasm. OpenSight received FDA 510(k) clearance as a radiological image-processing system for preoperative planning in 2018, with Novarad in American Fork as applicant. VisAR followed in 2022 as a Provo-based orthopedic stereotaxic instrument for precision-guided intraoperative spine surgery.

## Why this might become great work

Surgical navigation is powerful but often expensive, equipment-heavy, and workflow-sensitive. If an optical AR system can make image guidance faster to set up, easier to use, and good enough for common spine, orthopedic, interventional-radiology, and minimally invasive procedures, it could move navigation from specialized rooms into more ordinary operating rooms.

The world-scale version would be a practical interface shift: surgeons looking at the patient while seeing the relevant internal map, rather than mentally aligning a separate screen, fluoroscopic image, or printed plan. That would matter most if it improves targeting accuracy, reduces radiation or repeated imaging, shortens operating time, avoids complications, or lets smaller hospitals use navigation that would otherwise be impractical.

## Signals so far

- FDA 510(k) K172418 lists OpenSight as a Novarad radiological image-processing system, with an American Fork, Utah applicant address and a September 21, 2018 substantially equivalent decision
- FDA 510(k) K220146 lists VisAR as a Novarad orthopedic stereotaxic instrument, with a Provo, Utah applicant address and a May 27, 2022 substantially equivalent decision
- Novarad describes OpenSight as the first AR surgical system using Microsoft HoloLens to receive FDA clearance
- A 2020 European Spine Journal paper reported direct clinical use of AR navigation, including a virtual-needle asset, across 18 percutaneous image-guided spine procedures in 10 patients, with Novarad and Provo affiliations among the authors
- Independent spine-surgery reviews identify augmented reality as an active surgical-navigation category, while also showing that the evidence base is still early and fragmented
- Novarad's public product trail shows a progression from OpenSight preoperative planning into VisAR intraoperative spine navigation, rather than a one-off demonstration

## What would upgrade this

- Independent multicenter studies showing better screw, needle, catheter, or incision-placement accuracy than conventional navigation, fluoroscopy, freehand planning, or robotics in matched cases
- Evidence of lower radiation exposure, shorter setup time, shorter operating time, fewer revisions, fewer complications, or lower total cost
- Routine use across hospitals outside the originating customer and distributor network
- FDA-cleared or regulator-recognized expansion into additional surgical indications with published clinical outcomes
- Comparative data showing that AR navigation can democratize surgical navigation rather than merely adding another high-end visualization layer

## Caveats

- FDA 510(k) clearance establishes substantial equivalence for a marketed device; it does not prove superior clinical outcomes.
- The OpenSight clearance was for preoperative planning, which is important but less outcome-proving than intraoperative therapeutic use.
- The early clinical paper is small and includes Novarad-affiliated authors, so independent replication matters.
- Surgical AR has many competitors and parallel academic systems; Novarad is a notable Utah contributor, not the sole creator of AR surgery.
- Holographic overlays must remain accurate under patient motion, tissue deformation, line-of-sight limits, headset ergonomics, and ordinary operating-room messiness.
