# Bidirectional LUKE Arm Neuroprosthesis

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a compelling Utah-developed nerve-interface and sensory-feedback system for dexterous prosthetic arms, but not yet a commercial take-home medical product
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Neuroprosthesis / peripheral nerve interface / bionic arm control
**Era:** 2000s-present; major human results in 2017-2025
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The bidirectional LUKE Arm neuroprosthesis is the University of Utah's effort to let an advanced prosthetic arm move from and talk back to the user's nervous system. The mechanical arm itself was developed by DEKA Research & Development in New Hampshire. Utah's contribution is the direct neural interface, decoding, and biologically realistic sensory feedback that make the arm feel less like a tool and more like a body part.

The system uses implanted Utah Slanted Electrode Arrays in residual arm nerves, intramuscular recording leads, decoding algorithms, and sensors in the prosthetic hand. The user's motor intent is decoded to control a multi-degree-of-freedom DEKA LUKE Arm, while touch information from the hand is converted into nerve stimulation that can evoke tactile percepts on the missing hand.

## Why this might become great work

Upper-limb prosthetics still fail many users because they are hard to control, lack natural sensation, and demand constant visual attention. A hand that can be controlled intuitively and provide useful touch feedback would change what prosthetic limbs can mean: not just grasping, but embodiment, delicate manipulation, confidence, and social touch.

If the Utah interface proves durable and portable outside the lab, it could become a key pattern for future neuroprostheses: bidirectional peripheral nerve interfaces that restore both action and sensation without requiring a brain implant.

## Signals so far

- University of Utah researchers reported in 2019 that a participant could use the LUKE Arm to handle delicate objects such as grapes and eggs with sensory feedback
- A Science Robotics paper described biomimetic sensory feedback through peripheral nerve stimulation using chronically implanted Utah Slanted Electrode Arrays
- A Journal of Neuroscience Methods paper described modified-Kalman-filter control of six degrees of freedom in the DEKA LUKE Arm
- A Journal of NeuroEngineering and Rehabilitation paper reported multiple cutaneous and proprioceptive percepts evoked by intraneural stimulation in human amputees
- University of Utah teams began a 2025 clinical trial stage testing day-to-day use outside the lab
- The work draws on Utah's deeper neural-interface ecosystem: the Utah Slanted Electrode Array, University of Utah biomedical engineering, Blackrock Microsystems/Neurotech, and Ripple Neuro

## What would upgrade this

- Participants using the system at home for months or years with reliable function and low complication burden
- A portable, wireless, clinically usable version rather than a lab-tethered prototype
- FDA clearance or approval for a defined prosthetic-restoration indication
- Clear functional gains over high-end myoelectric prostheses, targeted muscle reinnervation, and less invasive sensory-feedback approaches
- Independent replication or adoption by prosthetics companies, rehabilitation systems, or military/veterans health programs

## Caveats

- The DEKA LUKE Arm hardware is not a Utah invention; the Utah claim is the neural interface, control, and sensory-feedback layer
- The human evidence remains small-cohort and research-intensive
- Percutaneous or implanted electrode systems carry infection, maintenance, durability, surgical, and reimbursement risks
- Many users may prefer less invasive prosthetic systems if those provide enough function
- The page overlaps with the broader Utah Array / Blackrock / Ripple ecosystem, so it should stay focused on peripheral nerve prosthetic restoration rather than all BCI work
