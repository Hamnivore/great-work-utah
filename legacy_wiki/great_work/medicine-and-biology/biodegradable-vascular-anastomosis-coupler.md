# Biodegradable Vascular Anastomosis Coupler

**Speculative tier:** P-C - a Utah-developed microsurgical device platform with patents, NIH SBIR support, and preclinical evidence, but no clear human clinical or regulatory adoption yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Surgical device / microsurgery tool / vascular repair method
**Era:** 2010s-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The biodegradable vascular anastomosis coupler is a device system meant to connect two cut blood vessels end-to-end without traditional hand suturing. The Utah design uses paired coupling devices and installation tools so that small arteries or veins can be joined more quickly, with the vessel ends aligned and held in place while avoiding foreign material inside the blood-flow lumen.

The work came from University of Utah surgery and engineering collaboration around Jay Agarwal, Bruce Gale, Huizhong Li, Himanshu Sant, and colleagues, with later commercialization work through Salt Lake City-based Microsurgical Innovations. The core claim is not simply "a vascular coupler"; nonabsorbable couplers and other anastomosis tools already exist. The Utah contribution is a biodegradable, lumen-sparing coupler intended for both arteries and veins and designed to reduce microsurgical time and operator difficulty.

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the dissertation work, patents, peer-reviewed studies, and SBIR-backed development trace to University of Utah labs, inventors, and a Salt Lake City company.

## Why this might become great work

Microsurgical anastomosis is a bottleneck in free-flap reconstruction, traumatic limb and digit repair, vascular repair, transplant-adjacent procedures, and battlefield or austere surgery. Hand suturing small vessels requires rare skill, magnification, time, and steady conditions. A reliable biodegradable coupler that worked for arteries and veins could shorten operating time, reduce technical failures, broaden who can perform difficult repairs, and make complex reconstruction more accessible.

The bigger possibility is surgical infrastructure: turning a high-skill manual step into a more standardized device-assisted procedure. That would matter most if it preserved patency, avoided thrombosis and inflammation, degraded safely, and worked across real vessel sizes and tissue quality.

## Signals so far

- Huizhong Li's 2015 University of Utah dissertation described a metal-free vascular coupling system for end-to-end anastomosis, including installation tools, tensile testing, leak testing, imaging, and cadaver/live-animal demonstrations
- A 2018 peer-reviewed Journal of Medical and Biological Engineering paper reported a revised biodegradable vascular coupling device with faster installation than hand suturing in test settings, lower leakage than sutured comparisons, tensile testing, and swine carotid-artery installation
- NIH SBIR records show Microsurgical Innovations received Phase I and Phase II support for a biodegradable vascular coupling device for end-to-end anastomosis
- The SBIR trail names University of Utah surgery and mechanical-engineering leadership and describes access to Utah core facilities for mechanical testing, 3D printing, animal studies, histology, and imaging
- University of Utah-linked patent families cover vascular coupling devices, with inventors including Jayant Agarwal, Bruce Gale, Huizhong Li, and Himanshu Sant and the University of Utah Research Foundation as assignee
- A 2021 University of Utah paper studied compression of the vascular wall to create a friction fit in a vascular anastomotic coupler, suggesting continued method refinement

## What would upgrade this

- FDA 510(k), De Novo, IDE, or other regulator-visible progress for a specific vascular-coupler product
- Published large-animal data showing long-term patency, degradation, inflammation, thrombosis, intimal healing, and failure modes
- Human feasibility data in microsurgical reconstruction, trauma, or vascular repair
- Direct comparison with hand suturing and existing commercial couplers across vessel sizes, arteries versus veins, and difficult tissue conditions
- Evidence of surgeon adoption, reduced operative time, fewer revisions, lower thrombosis, or better access to microsurgical repair

## Caveats

- The public trail is still mostly bench, cadaver, and animal work; no human clinical trial surfaced in this sweep.
- Anastomosis devices have a long history, and existing couplers already work well in selected venous microsurgery settings.
- Arterial use, small-vessel variability, diseased or damaged tissue, thrombosis risk, and degradation biology are hard clinical tests.
- Faster installation is valuable only if patency, safety, and long-term healing match or beat expert suturing.
- The company signal is modest; the strongest evidence remains university and SBIR-stage development rather than product adoption.
