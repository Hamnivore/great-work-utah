# Cell-Impermeable Dialysis-Access Endoprosthesis

**Speculative tier:** P-B - FDA-approved Utah medical-device work with clinical data, but too recent to judge durable adoption or patient-scale impact
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / vascular access / hemodialysis infrastructure
**Era:** 2010s-present; FDA PMA in 2024
**Location:** South Jordan, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - WRAPSODY is a Merit Medical Systems device from South Jordan, with the FDA PMA applicant and product address in Utah.

## What it is

The WRAPSODY Cell-Impermeable Endoprosthesis is a flexible self-expanding stent-graft-style device for hemodialysis patients whose vascular access circuits narrow or occlude. The Utah work is the device architecture: a nitinol-supported, multilayer fluoropolymer covering with a spun PTFE luminal layer, a cell-impermeable middle layer intended to limit tissue ingrowth through the graft wall, and an outer layer intended to anchor the device.

It is not a generic dialysis-access stent entry. The specific claim is that Merit built a covered endoprosthesis for one of dialysis care's chronic maintenance problems: keeping an arteriovenous fistula or graft usable long enough to avoid repeated procedures, thrombosis, access abandonment, and inadequate dialysis.

## Why this might become great work

Dialysis access is a quiet infrastructure problem inside medicine. Hemodialysis depends on reliable blood flow through an access circuit, but stenosis and restenosis drive repeated angioplasties, stent placements, hospital visits, failed access sites, and interrupted treatment. A device that materially extends access patency could reduce procedural burden for a large, medically fragile population.

The world-scale version of this work is not that one Utah company sells another vascular implant. It is a more durable way to maintain dialysis access, validated across real-world anatomy and practice, that becomes part of standard vascular-access care.

## Signals so far

- FDA's PMA database lists WRAPSODY Cell-Impermeable Endoprosthesis, applicant Merit Medical Systems in South Jordan, Utah, with PMA P240023 and decision date December 19, 2024
- The FDA-approved indication covers treatment of stenosis or occlusion within the dialysis access outflow circuit, including peripheral veins of AV fistula patients and venous anastomosis of synthetic AV grafts
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists the WAVE pivotal study as a prospective randomized controlled multicenter study comparing WRAPSODY with percutaneous transluminal angioplasty in hemodialysis access stenosis or occlusion
- A 2021 open-access CardioVascular and Interventional Radiology first-in-human study reported 46 treated hemodialysis patients, 97.8% freedom from access-circuit-related safety events through 30 days, and 84.6% target-lesion primary patency at 12 months
- FDA post-approval study records require completion of the WAVE study and a WRAP registry to collect real-world safety and effectiveness data
- Merit has received CE mark and US PMA approval, moving the work beyond pilot status into commercial and post-market evidence collection

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed pivotal WAVE results show sustained superiority or meaningful clinical advantage over angioplasty and competing covered stents
- Post-approval registry data show durable real-world patency, fewer reinterventions, lower access abandonment, and acceptable complication rates
- Dialysis access specialists adopt the device broadly because it changes patient management rather than merely filling a narrow product niche
- Independent health-economic studies show fewer procedures, less hospitalization, or better dialysis continuity
- Long-term follow-up confirms that the cell-impermeable design reduces restenosis mechanisms without creating new safety tradeoffs

## Caveats

- FDA PMA establishes safety and effectiveness for the approved indication; it does not by itself prove world-scale impact
- The device category already includes angioplasty, bare stents, and other covered stent options, so the Utah contribution must prove comparative value
- Early clinical studies were sponsor-linked and partly nonrandomized; the pivotal and post-market evidence matters more
- Dialysis access outcomes depend heavily on anatomy, operator practice, patient comorbidities, surveillance, and reintervention thresholds
- Commercial success could overstate clinical significance if real-world gains are modest or limited to selected lesions
