# Conotoxins and Prialt

**Tier:** A — University of Utah cone-snail venom research produced a first-in-class severe-pain drug and a major toolkit for neuroscience
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Research program / drug discovery / molecular neuroscience
**Era:** late 1970s–present; Prialt approved 2004
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Baldomero "Toto" Olivera's University of Utah lab turned venom from predatory cone snails into a molecular toolkit for studying the nervous system. Cone snails use complex cocktails of small peptides to paralyze prey. Olivera's lab purified and characterized individual conotoxins, showing that many of them target specific ion channels and receptors with extraordinary selectivity.

One of those peptides, omega-conotoxin MVIIA from *Conus magus*, became the basis for ziconotide, sold as Prialt. The natural form was discovered at the University of Utah by J. Michael McIntosh while working in Olivera's lab. The FDA approved Prialt in 2004 for severe chronic pain delivered by intrathecal infusion.

## Why it mattered

Pain treatment has long depended heavily on opioids, which are powerful but dangerous. Ziconotide opened a non-opioid route for severe pain by blocking N-type voltage-gated calcium channels involved in pain signaling. It is not an easy drug to use, but it proved that venom peptides could become approved human medicines for nervous-system targets.

The broader conotoxin program mattered beyond Prialt. Conotoxins became precision probes for ion channels, synapses, neuromuscular signaling, nicotinic receptors, NMDA receptors, sodium channels, calcium channels, and other molecular machinery. They helped turn an obscure marine predator into a reusable library of neuroscience reagents and drug leads.

## The hard problem they solved

Venom is messy: hundreds of small molecules, similar-looking peptides, disulfide bonds, tiny quantities, and powerful biological effects. Olivera's lab had to purify individual components, determine structures, synthesize analogs, identify molecular targets, and connect a toxin's animal effect to a human-relevant mechanism.

The scientific move was to treat venom not as a crude poison but as an evolved combinatorial library. Each peptide could be a key for one molecular lock in the nervous system.

## Lasting impact

- Prialt became an FDA-approved non-opioid treatment for severe chronic pain
- The Olivera lab's conotoxins became important tools for calcium-channel, sodium-channel, acetylcholine-receptor, and NMDA-receptor research
- The work helped establish venom-derived peptides as serious drug-discovery leads
- University of Utah undergraduate researchers played unusually visible roles in the discovery pipeline
- The program continues to produce new leads for pain, diabetes, hormone disorders, and neural signaling research

## Key people

- **Baldomero M. Olivera** — University of Utah biologist; founder and leader of the conotoxin research program
- **J. Michael McIntosh** — discovered the natural form of the Prialt peptide as a young University of Utah researcher; later U faculty
- **University of Utah conotoxin researchers and students** — purified, characterized, synthesized, and tested many venom peptides
- **Elan Corporation** — developed ziconotide into a commercial drug

## Caveats

- Prialt is important but niche: it requires intrathecal delivery and can have serious neurological side effects
- The approved drug depended on development work outside Utah after the initial discovery
- Cone snail venom work is globally distributed; the Utah claim is strongest for Olivera's long-running lab, the discovery of the Prialt peptide, and the conotoxin toolkit
