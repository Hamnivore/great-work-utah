# Dwarf Crops for Spaceflight

**Tier:** B - Utah State crop-physiology work that produced the first crop bred specifically for spaceflight and helped define controlled-environment food production for long-duration missions
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Plant biology / controlled-environment agriculture / space life-support research
**Era:** 1980s-present
**Location:** Utah State University, Logan, UT

## What it was

Utah State University's Crop Physiology Laboratory developed dwarf crop varieties and controlled-environment growing methods for NASA's bioregenerative life-support research. The best-known result was **USU-Apogee wheat**, a full-dwarf hard red spring wheat released in 1996 by the Utah Agricultural Experiment Station in cooperation with NASA.

USU-Apogee was bred for conditions that normal field wheat was not designed for: hydroponic culture, artificial light, continuous illumination, tight plant spacing, low height, rapid development, and high harvest index. USU later followed it with **USU-Perigee**, an even shorter wheat cultivar for confined controlled environments.

## Why it mattered

Long-duration human spaceflight has a food problem that cannot be solved only by packaged meals. If humans are going to live for long periods in orbit, on the Moon, or on Mars, plant systems have to provide food, oxygen, carbon-dioxide recycling, water recycling, and psychological value inside small, energy-constrained habitats.

The Utah work made that abstract life-support problem concrete. Instead of merely asking whether plants could grow in space, USU researchers optimized a real staple crop for the strange economics of spacecraft agriculture: grams of edible biomass per square meter, per day, per watt, and per unit of crew time.

## The hard problem they solved

Field crops are bad spacecraft crops. They are too tall, too slow, too wasteful of volume, and often poorly adapted to continuous light and hydroponic systems. A space crop has to mature quickly, fit inside a chamber, produce mostly edible biomass, tolerate rapid growth conditions, and behave predictably under artificial environmental control.

Bugbee, Salisbury, and collaborators screened wheat germplasm, ran controlled-environment productivity studies, and selected for traits that made wheat plausible as a life-support crop. USU-Apogee emerged as a shorter, higher-yielding alternative to the field cultivars previously used in controlled-environment work, with resistance to leaf-tip injury seen under rapid-growth conditions.

## Lasting impact

- NASA described USU-Apogee in 1996 as the first crop developed specifically for growth in space
- USU reports that USU-Apogee was grown aboard the International Space Station from April to June 2003
- The cultivar registration and NASA technical literature made USU-Apogee and USU-Perigee reference crops for controlled-environment and space-agriculture studies
- The program helped train space crop scientists who later worked on NASA plant experiments and advanced plant habitats
- USU's Crop Physiology Laboratory remains part of NASA-linked controlled-environment agriculture work, including newer root-module and plant-growth-system studies

## Key people

- **Bruce Bugbee** - Utah State crop physiologist; central figure in USU-Apogee, USU-Perigee, controlled-environment crop productivity, and space plant-growth research
- **Frank B. Salisbury** - Utah State plant physiologist; early leader in controlled ecological life-support crop research
- **G. Koerner, R. Albrechtsen, W. Dewey, and S. Clawson** - coauthors on the USU-Apogee cultivar registration
- **Utah Agricultural Experiment Station** - institutional home for the crop release, in cooperation with NASA

## Caveats

- This is not yet a world-changing agricultural crop. Its impact is mostly in space life-support and controlled-environment research.
- Space agriculture remains experimental; no space mission has depended on wheat production for routine crew calories.
- USU's work is one part of a broader international plant-in-space lineage that includes Soviet/Russian, NASA, university, and commercial experiments.
- "First crop developed specifically for space" is a strong but narrow claim; the broader question is whether such crops eventually matter operationally.
