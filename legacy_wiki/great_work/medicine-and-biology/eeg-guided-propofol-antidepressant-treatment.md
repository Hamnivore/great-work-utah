# EEG-Guided Propofol Antidepressant Treatment

**Speculative tier:** P-C - a Utah-origin clinical protocol with pilot and randomized-trial signals, but the controlled evidence is still small, preprint-level, and not statistically decisive
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Clinical protocol / neuropsychiatric treatment / anesthetic pharmacology
**Era:** 2010s-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

This is the use of high-dose propofol anesthesia, guided by electroencephalography, as an experimental treatment for treatment-resistant depression. The Utah protocol doses propofol to produce a predefined brain state, especially EEG burst suppression, rather than treating propofol as an ordinary sedative infusion.

The specific work is not "propofol exists" or "anesthetics can affect mood." It is a University of Utah psychiatry, anesthesiology, biomedical-engineering, and Huntsman Mental Health Institute effort to test whether repeated EEG-targeted propofol treatments can produce rapid antidepressant effects in people who have not responded to standard therapies.

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the pilot study, randomized trial, EEG-guided dosing work, and clinical recruitment were run through University of Utah / Huntsman Mental Health Institute teams in Salt Lake City.

## Why this might become great work

Treatment-resistant depression remains one of medicine's hardest clinical problems. Electroconvulsive therapy can be highly effective but carries stigma, access barriers, anesthesia burden, cognitive side effects for some patients, and uneven availability. Ketamine and other rapid-acting treatments have expanded the field, but many patients still do not respond or cannot tolerate existing options.

If a short series of EEG-guided propofol treatments could reliably trigger durable antidepressant effects with fewer cognitive side effects than ECT, it would open another route into rapid neuropsychiatric treatment. The interesting idea is pharmacodynamic precision: using an anesthetic to reach a target brain state, then asking whether that state is therapeutically active.

## Signals so far

- The University of Utah open-label pilot study, registered as NCT02935647, treated ten otherwise healthy patients with medication-resistant depression using propofol dosed to suppress EEG activity
- The 2018 peer-reviewed pilot paper reported that treatments were feasible and well tolerated, with six of ten subjects meeting response criteria and five responders remaining well for at least three months
- The University of Utah registered a later randomized, blinded trial, NCT03684447, comparing low-dose and high-dose propofol for treatment-resistant depression
- The randomized-trial protocol used real-time processed frontal EEG to guide infusions toward predefined pharmacodynamic criteria
- A related University of Utah anesthesiology / biomedical-engineering paper clinically validated an adapted Eleveld propofol model for high-dose depression treatments
- A 2023 medRxiv report of the randomized trial found numerically larger depression-score improvements in the high-dose group, with effective blinding and tolerability, but the between-group differences did not reach statistical significance in the small sample

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed publication of the randomized trial with full safety, durability, and adverse-event data
- A larger controlled trial showing statistically and clinically meaningful benefit over credible anesthetic, psychiatric, or ECT/ketamine comparators
- Clear evidence that EEG burst suppression or another measurable propofol-induced brain state predicts response
- Cognitive, safety, anesthesia-risk, and relapse data strong enough to justify repeated treatments in real-world psychiatry settings
- Independent replication outside the University of Utah team

## Caveats

- The strongest controlled evidence so far is a small randomized trial reported as a preprint; it showed a favorable signal but not a statistically significant primary between-group result.
- Propofol is a general anesthetic with real airway, hemodynamic, staffing, monitoring, and facility requirements.
- Treatment-resistant depression trials are highly placebo-sensitive, and expectation, clinical contact, and procedural intensity can influence outcomes.
- The protocol excludes many patients at higher anesthetic or suicide risk, which may limit generalizability.
- Even if effective, the method would have to compete with ECT, ketamine/esketamine, transcranial magnetic stimulation, focused ultrasound, psychedelics, and other emerging approaches.
