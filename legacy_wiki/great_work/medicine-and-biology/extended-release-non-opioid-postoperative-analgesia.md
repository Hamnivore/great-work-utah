# Extended-Release Non-Opioid Postoperative Analgesia

**Speculative tier:** P-B - clinically relevant opioid-sparing drug-delivery work with peer-reviewed preclinical data and an IND path, but no human efficacy data yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Drug delivery / postoperative pain therapeutic
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Extended-release non-opioid postoperative analgesia is Rebel Medicine's attempt to turn a familiar local anesthetic, bupivacaine, into a longer-acting injectable gel for surgical pain control. Its lead candidate, Alevatrix, uses an injectable matrix or oleogel-style delivery system intended to release bupivacaine near the surgical site or nerve block over the first several days after surgery.

The Utah work is not the discovery of bupivacaine. It is the formulation and translation effort: making a depot that is injectable, stable, locally active, and potentially useful in ordinary postoperative care without relying on systemic opioids.

## Why this might become great work

Postoperative opioid exposure is one of the places where medicine still creates avoidable risk while trying to solve a real pain problem. A practical, affordable, long-acting local anesthetic could reduce opioid prescriptions after common surgeries, simplify discharge pain management, and improve recovery for patients who do not need systemic opioid therapy once the worst surgical pain has passed.

The world-scale version of this work would be a broadly adopted opioid-sparing standard for common outpatient and inpatient surgeries. It would matter because the intervention sits at a high-volume clinical choke point: the first 72 to 96 hours after surgery, when pain is intense and prescribing decisions are consequential.

## Signals so far

- A 2025 Journal of Drug Delivery Science and Technology paper reported benchtop characterization and two porcine postoperative pain models for an injectable oleogel-based bupivacaine formulation
- The PubMed-listed affiliations place the Rebel Medicine authors in Salt Lake City
- The preclinical paper reported multi-day analgesia in both local instillation and ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve block settings, with pharmacokinetic assessment and no clinical signs of local anesthetic systemic toxicity at tested doses
- Rebel Medicine announced a $6 million Series A in 2025 tied to opening the Alevatrix IND application with FDA
- The announced trial plan is a first-in-human, randomized, dose-escalation, active-controlled Phase 2 study in bunionectomy patients
- The company pipeline describes Alevatrix as an extended-release bupivacaine gel intended for both regional anesthesia and local instillation

## What would upgrade this

- Human Phase 2 data shows meaningful pain reduction or opioid-use reduction versus active control, without safety tradeoffs
- A larger pivotal trial confirms efficacy in one or more common surgeries
- FDA approval or another regulator-cleared path for postoperative pain
- Real-world evidence shows lower opioid prescribing, lower rescue-medication use, fewer adverse events, or improved recovery metrics
- Hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers adopt it because the clinical benefit and cost profile are compelling

## Caveats

- The strongest data so far are preclinical and company-led
- IND clearance or opening is not evidence of human efficacy
- Existing long-acting local anesthetic products make this a competitive clinical and reimbursement category
- Bupivacaine delivery has a narrow safety envelope; local anesthetic systemic toxicity remains a key risk to watch
- Pain trials can fail because endpoints, rescue medication, surgical variability, and patient expectations are hard to control
