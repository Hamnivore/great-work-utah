# Golden Broccoli Glycine Biosensor

**Speculative tier:** P-C - a clever Utah-made chemical-biology tool with peer-reviewed proof, but its broader impact depends on adoption by other labs
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Molecular imaging tool / RNA biosensor
**Era:** 2025-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The Hammond lab at the University of Utah created RNA-based biosensors that let researchers image glycine dynamics in living cells. The standout component is Golden Broccoli, a fluorescent RNA aptamer designed to work with Red Broccoli as a single-dye ratiometric sensor.

That sounds cute, but the technical point is real. Glycine is a small amino acid, neurotransmitter, metabolic signal, and possible bacterial-virulence biomarker. Before this work, there were not good tools for imaging glycine both inside and outside living cells at single-cell resolution.

## Why this might become great work

Biology often advances when a hidden variable becomes visible. Calcium imaging, fluorescent proteins, and metabolite sensors changed what researchers could ask because they let cells report internal states in real time.

If Golden Broccoli or related RNA sensors become broadly used, they could help researchers study neurotransmission, bacterial metabolism, virulence, amino-acid transport, and drug-induced metabolic changes without destroying the cells being measured.

## Signals so far

- The work was published in *Nucleic Acids Research* in 2025
- The paper reports glycine detection in vitro and in live *E. coli*
- The authors deposited RNA construct sequences and numerical data
- University of Utah described the work as the first single-dye ratiometric biosensor for glycine imaging
- The method builds on the Hammond lab's broader program engineering nucleic acids as programmable imaging and gene-control tools

## What would upgrade this

- Independent use by outside labs in bacteria, neurons, organoids, or disease models
- Sensor variants with improved brightness, specificity, dynamic range, or mammalian-cell performance
- Citations showing the tool enabled discoveries that were hard to make with older assays
- Integration into broader RNA biosensor libraries or live-cell screening workflows

## Caveats

- This is an enabling research tool, not a therapy or clinical platform
- Early proof in *E. coli* does not guarantee broad performance across cell types
- Many beautiful biosensors remain niche if they are difficult to express, calibrate, image, or interpret
- The name is memorable enough to overstate the maturity; adoption is the real test
