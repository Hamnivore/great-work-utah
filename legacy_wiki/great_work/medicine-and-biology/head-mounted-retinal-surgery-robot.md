# Head-Mounted Retinal Surgery Robot

**Speculative tier:** P-B - promising Utah-built surgical robotics platform with peer-reviewed early testing, but still preclinical
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Surgical robot / ophthalmic microsurgery
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The head-mounted retinal surgery robot is a University of Utah and Moran Eye Center device designed to help surgeons operate on the retina at scales smaller than ordinary human steadiness allows. It mounts to the patient's head, uses robotic control to compensate for motion, scales down surgeon movements, and can execute extremely small movements for tasks such as subretinal injections.

The specific work is precision retinal microsurgery: making it safer and more repeatable to deliver therapies into tiny spaces beneath the retina.

## Why this might become great work

Many future eye therapies, including gene therapies and cell-based treatments, require delivery to fragile retinal tissue. If surgeons cannot reliably place those treatments where they need to go, the therapeutic pipeline is physically bottlenecked by the hand.

A robot that gives retinal surgeons effectively steadier, smaller-scale hands could expand who can perform delicate procedures, reduce complications, and make advanced retinal therapies easier to deliver.

## Signals so far

- The system was developed by the University of Utah's John A. Moran Eye Center and Price College of Engineering
- Early results were published in *Science Robotics*
- NIH/NEI highlighted the device as a promising retinal-surgery robot
- The robot is described as executing movements as small as 1 micrometer
- Early tests used enucleated pig eyes for subretinal injection tasks and showed improved success while avoiding ophthalmic complications

## What would upgrade this

- Successful live-animal and then human clinical studies
- Demonstrated reduction in complications or improvement in delivery accuracy versus expert manual surgery
- Use in gene therapy, cell therapy, or other retinal procedures where robotic precision changes what can be delivered
- Commercial device pathway or hospital adoption

## Caveats

- The work is still experimental and preclinical
- Retinal surgery robots have to fit into real operating-room workflows, sterilization constraints, surgeon training, and regulatory pathways
- Technical precision alone does not guarantee better patient outcomes
