# HELP Clinical Decision Support System

**Tier:** A - one of the pioneering hospital information systems that showed real-time clinical decision support could work inside routine care
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Hospital information system / clinical decision support / medical informatics
**Era:** 1967-present in successor forms; major HELP development 1970s-1990s
**Location:** LDS Hospital and University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

HELP, short for Health Evaluation through Logical Processing, was a hospital information system built at LDS Hospital in Salt Lake City with deep ties to the University of Utah medical informatics community. Led by people such as Homer R. Warner, T. Allan Pryor, and Reed M. Gardner, HELP began in specialized cardiac and intensive-care settings and grew into an integrated hospital system for patient data, orders, laboratory results, pharmacy, radiology, nursing documentation, research, and teaching.

The distinctive thing was not just digitizing hospital records. HELP was designed around real-time medical decision-making. It stored clinical data in a structured database, connected those data to a medical knowledge base and inference logic, and used the result to generate alerts, reminders, protocols, antibiotic guidance, ventilator support, blood-product guidance, and other bedside decision-support functions.

## Why it mattered

Modern medicine depends on electronic health records and clinical decision support, but in the 1970s and 1980s that future was not obvious. Many hospital systems were mainly administrative or billing tools. HELP made the stronger claim that a hospital computer could participate in patient care while care was happening.

That changed the imagination of medical informatics. HELP became one of the reference examples, alongside systems such as Regenstrief and the VA's VistA/CPRS lineage, for how computerized patient data and executable medical knowledge could be woven into clinical workflow. It also gave Intermountain's later quality-improvement work a data-rich foundation rather than leaving guidelines and measurement as paper exercises.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was not simply storing patient facts. HELP had to ingest messy hospital data quickly enough to matter, represent medical rules in executable form, route advice to clinicians without breaking workflow, and remain trusted in a high-stakes environment where bad alerts could be ignored or dangerous.

The system also had to be operationally reliable. A clinical system that touches orders, pharmacy, lab data, monitors, and bedside protocols has to behave like infrastructure, not like a research demo. HELP's longevity is part of the accomplishment.

## Lasting impact

- HELP became one of the longest-running and most studied clinical information systems
- It demonstrated that computerized clinical decision support could improve care processes and cost-effectiveness in real hospital operations
- LDS Hospital and University of Utah informatics work helped train and influence generations of biomedical informaticists
- HELP's medical logic modules and knowledge-base ideas fed into broader standards and decision-support architecture discussions, including Arden Syntax work
- The system helped make Intermountain a national reference site for data-driven healthcare delivery and clinical quality improvement

## Key people

- **Homer R. Warner** - University of Utah cardiologist and medical informatics pioneer; foundational leader in the Utah medical informatics program
- **T. Allan Pryor** - HELP co-developer and University of Utah/LDS Hospital informaticist
- **Reed M. Gardner** - HELP co-developer and major clinical informatics figure at LDS Hospital, Intermountain, and the University of Utah
- **Gilad J. Kuperman, R. Scott Evans, Peter Haug, Beatriz Rocha, Roberto Rocha, and LDS Hospital clinical teams** - later evaluators, implementers, and developers of HELP/HELP2 decision-support applications

## Caveats

- HELP was a pioneering system, but it was part of a broader national movement in medical informatics; Regenstrief, the VA, Brigham, and others were also crucial.
- Many HELP results came from operational evaluations in one unusually informatics-friendly health system, so not every intervention generalized cleanly to other hospitals.
- The system's architecture eventually aged, and Intermountain had to migrate toward newer platforms and standards.
