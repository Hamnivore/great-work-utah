# HIV Capsid Biology and the Lenacapavir Foundation

**Tier:** A - University of Utah basic science helped create the target biology for a first-in-class HIV capsid inhibitor and twice-yearly HIV prevention drug; the drug itself was developed by Gilead
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Basic research program / structural virology / drug-discovery foundation
**Era:** 1990s-present; lenacapavir PrEP approved 2025
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Wesley Sundquist's University of Utah lab spent decades studying how HIV builds, packages, and releases itself from human cells. Two parts of that work became especially important: the lab's structural and biochemical work on the HIV capsid, the protein shell around the viral genome, and its work showing how HIV hijacks the ESCRT cellular machinery to bud from infected cells.

The capsid work helped reveal that HIV's shell is not just packaging. It is a delicate, druggable molecular machine. Small disruptions to capsid assembly and stability can block replication. Those insights helped motivate and guide Gilead Sciences' development of lenacapavir, a first-in-class HIV-1 capsid inhibitor.

## Why it mattered

Lenacapavir changed the prevention landscape because it lasts. Daily PrEP can be extremely effective, but adherence is hard, especially for people with unstable access to care. A twice-yearly injection changes the practical burden of prevention.

In 2024, *Science* named lenacapavir its Breakthrough of the Year after major clinical trial results. In June 2025, the FDA approved lenacapavir, marketed as Yeztugo, as the first twice-yearly PrEP option for reducing sexually acquired HIV risk. The clinical development and approval belong to Gilead, but the basic target biology has a strong Utah root.

## The hard problem they solved

HIV's capsid is structurally unusual, dynamic, and assembled from many copies of the same protein. Understanding it required purifying capsid proteins, reconstituting assemblies, identifying how small changes affected viral replication, and connecting molecular structure to the viral life cycle.

The conceptual difficulty was recognizing the capsid as a vulnerable target rather than a passive shell. The Utah work helped make the capsid legible enough for medicinal chemists to attack it.

## Lasting impact

- Lenacapavir became the first approved HIV capsid inhibitor for treatment of multidrug-resistant HIV and then the first twice-yearly PrEP medicine
- Large PrEP trials showed extremely high protection, including zero infections among more than 2,000 women in one major trial and very high efficacy in other studied populations
- Sundquist's capsid and ESCRT work has been recognized by the Horwitz Prize, the Warren Alpert Foundation Prize, TIME100 recognition, and the World Laureates Association Prize
- The work is a clean example of curiosity-driven structural virology becoming a practical public-health tool decades later
- Janet Iwasa's University of Utah scientific visualizations also helped make HIV capsid dynamics legible to researchers and the public

## Key people

- **Wesley Sundquist** - University of Utah biochemist; led the HIV capsid and ESCRT work that helped establish the target biology
- **Sundquist lab researchers** - purified, reconstituted, imaged, mutated, and modeled HIV assembly and capsid systems over many years
- **Janet Iwasa** - University of Utah biochemist and molecular animator; created widely used visualizations of HIV capsid and lenacapavir mechanisms
- **Tomas Cihlar and Gilead Sciences teams** - translated capsid biology into lenacapavir and ran the drug-development program
- **Scott Emr** - Cornell scientist whose ESCRT discoveries were central to the broader pathway; shared major recognition with Sundquist

## Caveats

- Lenacapavir was developed by Gilead Sciences, not by a Utah company, and the decisive clinical trials were global
- The Utah contribution is target biology and mechanistic foundation, not drug ownership or manufacturing
- Access and pricing will determine whether the drug's scientific promise becomes a full public-health transformation
- HIV prevention still depends on testing, delivery systems, trust, insurance or public funding, and access in low- and middle-income countries
