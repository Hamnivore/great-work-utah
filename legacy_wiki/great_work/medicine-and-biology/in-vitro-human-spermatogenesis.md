# In-Vitro Human Spermatogenesis

**Speculative tier:** P-B - potentially field-changing fertility biology, but the central claim is not yet peer-reviewed or independently replicated
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Reproductive biotechnology / stem-cell-derived gametes
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Paterna Biosciences is trying to produce mature human sperm outside the body by taking sperm-forming stem cells from testicular tissue and guiding them through spermatogenesis in culture. The company says it has grown functional human sperm in vitro and used that sperm to create test embryos as a proof-of-viability step.

The Utah-specific work is the Salt Lake City company and its connection to University of Utah reproductive-medicine leadership. CEO and co-founder Alexander Pastuszak is an associate professor of surgery at the University of Utah School of Medicine and a male reproductive medicine specialist.

## Why this might become great work

If validated, in-vitro human spermatogenesis could create a new path for some men with non-obstructive azoospermia or other severe male-factor infertility to have genetically related children without relying on donor sperm. It would also be an unusually consequential platform for reproductive biology: a controllable model of human sperm development, not only a fertility procedure.

The deeper claim is that some infertility may be caused by a failing testicular microenvironment rather than irreversibly broken sperm stem cells. If a lab can recreate the missing developmental signals safely, it may turn a biopsy into many usable sperm cells for IVF.

## Signals so far

- Paterna closed a $6 million seed round in 2024 to advance in-vitro spermatogenesis
- WIRED reported in April 2026 that Paterna claims it grew human sperm from testicular stem cells and used them to create visibly healthy-looking embryos
- The reported method used computational biology to predict developmental molecular signals and then test culture conditions stage by stage
- Paterna says it is focusing first on men who do not produce sperm but still appear to retain sperm-forming stem cells
- The company publicly states that the work uses adult patient stem cells and rejects heritable social-trait modification

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed publication with morphology, motility, meiosis, imprinting, epigenetic, and genetic-safety data
- Independent replication by a fertility or developmental-biology lab
- Embryology data showing normal fertilization and early development without chromosomal or imprinting abnormalities
- Regulator-approved clinical trials leading to healthy births and long-term follow-up
- A clear clinical niche where the approach outperforms surgical sperm retrieval, donor sperm, or current IVF workflows

## Caveats

- The key 2026 claim has not yet been peer-reviewed or independently verified
- Creating embryos proves less than creating safe pregnancies and healthy children
- Human gamete biology has unusually high ethical, regulatory, and safety stakes
- Access may be limited if the procedure becomes another expensive fertility add-on rather than a broadly available therapy
