# Injectable Disc Cell Therapy

**Speculative tier:** P-A - could become a major regenerative-medicine advance if Phase 3 confirms durable benefit and the product reaches approval
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Cell therapy / regenerative medicine / spine care
**Era:** 2007-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - DiscGenics is headquartered in Salt Lake City, built its in-house cGMP allogeneic cell-manufacturing facility there, and is sponsoring the IDCT clinical program from Utah; the clinical trials themselves are multicenter and include non-Utah investigators and sites.

## What it is

Injectable disc cell therapy is a minimally invasive, allogeneic cell therapy intended to treat symptomatic lumbar degenerative disc disease from inside the disc, rather than managing pain indefinitely or moving directly to destructive procedures and fusion surgery. The Utah case is DiscGenics' IDCT, also called rebonuputemcel.

IDCT uses a manufactured progenitor cell population derived from donated adult human intervertebral disc tissue, mixed with a viscous carrier and injected into the affected disc. The aim is to slow or reverse disc degeneration, reduce pain, and restore function through a single outpatient injection.

## Why this might become great work

Chronic low back pain is one of the world's largest disability burdens. For many patients with disc degeneration, the practical choices are conservative care, injections that mainly manage symptoms, ablation, opioids, or surgery. A regenerative, off-the-shelf disc therapy that works durably would change the spine-care ladder.

The world-scale version is not just another pain product. It would be a new category: a manufactured cell therapy for a common musculoskeletal condition, with the manufacturing discipline to reach far more patients than bespoke autologous approaches.

## Signals so far

- DiscGenics is headquartered in Salt Lake City and has built a cGMP allogeneic cell-manufacturing facility there
- IDCT received FDA Fast Track designation in 2019 and Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy designation in 2023
- The completed U.S. Phase 1/2 study on ClinicalTrials.gov enrolled 60 subjects and followed them for 104 weeks
- The peer-reviewed Phase 1/2 paper reported that the high-dose group met the 52-week pain endpoint, with two-year maintenance of pain improvement and significant disc-volume increases; the study was small and sponsor-led
- In July 2024, DiscGenics announced FDA acceptance of Phase 3 protocols and Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Controls plans for parallel pivotal and confirmatory studies
- As of the May 2026 editorial check, the U.S. Phase 3 DGX-A02 trial is listed as recruiting / active-recruiting in trial listings, with about 162 subjects and 104 weeks of follow-up; no Phase 3 results or FDA approval have surfaced

## What would upgrade this

- Positive Phase 3 results on pain, disability, function, imaging, safety, and durability
- FDA approval and a manufacturing process that reliably supplies commercial doses
- Real-world evidence that the therapy reduces surgery, opioid use, repeat procedures, or long-term disability
- Payer coverage and adoption by spine specialists without inappropriate overuse

## Caveats

- Regenerative spine therapies have a history of overclaiming; the sham-controlled Phase 3 data is the key test
- Disc degeneration and back pain are heterogeneous, so patient selection may decide whether the therapy looks transformative or marginal
- Cell therapies face manufacturing, potency, storage, delivery, and cost challenges
- Even if approved, misuse in weakly indicated patients could damage the field
- The Phase 1/2 signal is promising but not yet practice-changing: the randomized study was small, the sponsor had financial relationships with several authors, and the pivotal test is still ahead
