# Intermountain Clinical Quality Improvement

**Tier:** A — a major health-system example of using data, process control, and clinical standards to improve outcomes while lowering cost
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Healthcare delivery research / clinical quality program
**Era:** 1988–present
**Location:** Intermountain Healthcare / Intermountain Health, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Beginning in the late 1980s, Intermountain Healthcare became one of the best-known health systems applying industrial quality-improvement methods to clinical care. Under Brent James and colleagues, Intermountain built data systems, clinical programs, practice guidelines, variation measurement, and training programs that treated healthcare delivery itself as an object of research and engineering.

The work was not a single drug or device. It was a system for making care more reliable: measure how care is actually delivered, identify variation that does not improve outcomes, design evidence-based protocols with clinicians, monitor adherence and results, and keep updating the process as new evidence arrives.

## Why it mattered

Medicine often knows what should happen but fails to deliver it reliably. Intermountain's contribution was showing that a health system could manage care processes with the same seriousness that manufacturers manage production quality, while still involving physicians as partners rather than treating them as replaceable parts.

That made Intermountain a reference case for the "learning health system" idea: routine care generates data, the data improves practice, and improved practice generates better evidence. The work influenced patient-safety, quality, and value-based-care conversations far beyond Utah.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was changing care without flattening clinical judgment. Guidelines are easy to write and hard to make useful. Intermountain had to build enough trust, measurement infrastructure, and feedback loops that clinicians could see when a protocol improved outcomes and when exceptions were warranted.

It also had to connect quality and cost without letting cost-cutting become the real goal. The central claim was Deming-style: waste and harm are often consequences of poorly understood process variation, so better quality can reduce unnecessary cost.

## Lasting impact

- Intermountain became a national case study in clinical quality improvement and healthcare delivery science
- Brent James and the Advanced Training Program in Clinical Practice Improvement trained thousands of health-system leaders from around the world
- Intermountain's protocols and data systems influenced work on elective induction, intensive-care ventilation, stroke care, integrated behavioral health, and other clinical processes
- The program helped make "learning health system" thinking more concrete: not just research papers, but operational systems that change bedside care

## Key people

- **Brent C. James** — physician, statistician, and longtime leader of Intermountain's quality-improvement work
- **Lucy A. Savitz** — health-services researcher and coauthor on influential Intermountain quality and cost work
- **Intermountain Healthcare / Intermountain Health clinical teams** — the work depended on frontline clinicians, informaticists, analysts, and administrators implementing protocols across real care settings

## Caveats

- The work is influential, but the evidence base is uneven because many interventions were implemented operationally rather than as randomized trials
- Intermountain is an integrated regional system; not every lesson transfers cleanly to fragmented markets or hospitals without comparable data infrastructure
- Quality improvement can be oversold when results depend on local culture, leadership, payer structure, and long-term institutional patience
