# Low-Dose Intraoperative 3D Surgical Imaging

**Speculative tier:** P-B - FDA-cleared Utah surgical-imaging platform with NSF backing and early pediatric/adult surgical use, but needs independent outcome and dose evidence at scale
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Surgical imaging system / navigation platform / orthopedic and spine guidance
**Era:** 2014-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - nView medical is based in Salt Lake City, FDA records list Utah applicant addresses, and NSF SBIR records list Salt Lake City project contact information.

## What it is

Low-dose intraoperative 3D surgical imaging is nView medical's effort to bring fast, low-radiation 3D imaging and navigation into the operating room without requiring a rotating CT-style scanner. The nView s1 system reconstructs tomographic and fluoroscopic views during surgery, then uses integrated navigation and AI-assisted visualization to help surgeons understand anatomy and implant placement.

The specific Utah work is the imaging-and-navigation platform: instant or near-instant 3D reconstruction, virtual fluoroscopy, intraoperative image guidance, and spine-oriented planning/quality-control tools aimed especially at pediatric orthopedic and spine surgery.

## Why this might become great work

Many orthopedic and spine procedures are still guided by 2D fluoroscopy, even when the anatomy and implant-placement problem is three-dimensional. More complete intraoperative imaging can improve accuracy, but conventional 3D systems may add cost, radiation, setup complexity, or workflow friction.

If nView-like systems make 3D guidance routine at lower dose and smaller operating-room footprint, they could reduce implant malposition, revision surgery, radiation exposure, and uncertainty during complex pediatric and adult spine procedures. The world-scale version would be a practical bridge between ordinary fluoroscopy and heavy intraoperative CT/navigation suites.

## Signals so far

- NSF SBIR records describe nView's Phase I and Phase II work on a low-dose 4D cone-beam tomosynthesis reconstruction algorithm and scanner for image-guided interventions
- FDA 510(k) K190064 cleared the nView system 1 in July 2019 as an interventional fluoroscopic X-ray system, with nView Medical listed at a Salt Lake City address
- nView announced first pediatric spine use at Children's National Hospital in 2020
- FDA 510(k) K221344 cleared the nView s1 with navigation option in December 2022, again listing a Salt Lake City applicant address
- nView reported 25 pediatric cases in 2021, 250 surgical cases across nine sites by October 2023, and more than 500 surgeries by March 2025
- nView announced first adult spine surgery use in 2024, extending beyond its initial pediatric emphasis
- Product materials describe insta-3D imaging, quantum-dose operation, true-map navigation, virtual fluoroscopy, spine AI, and 3D stitching for intraoperative planning and implant verification

## What would upgrade this

- Independent studies compare dose, image quality, screw or implant accuracy, operative time, revisions, and complications against fluoroscopy, O-arm, CT navigation, or robotic-navigation workflows
- Pediatric spine centers publish multicenter evidence showing lower radiation or better implant-placement confidence without workflow penalties
- Real-world registry data links use to fewer returns to the operating room, fewer malpositioned implants, or better deformity-correction quality control
- Hospitals adopt the system beyond a small set of early sites because economics and workflow are compelling
- Guidelines, payer policies, or surgical society practice patterns recognize a defined role for low-dose intraoperative 3D imaging

## Caveats

- FDA 510(k) clearance does not prove better surgical outcomes
- Case-count milestones are company-reported and need independent context
- Surgical imaging adoption depends heavily on workflow, training, reimbursement, service reliability, and hospital capital budgets
- Competing systems from larger imaging, navigation, robotics, and orthopedic companies may limit market impact
- Radiation-dose claims need careful independent comparison by procedure, patient size, and imaging protocol
