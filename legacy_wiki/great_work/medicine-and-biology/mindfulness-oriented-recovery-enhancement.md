# Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement

**Speculative tier:** P-B - Utah-developed behavioral therapy with NIH-funded randomized trials and mechanistic evidence, but still needs broad independent adoption and real-world clinical-outcome proof
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Behavioral treatment / pain and addiction intervention / clinical research program
**Era:** 2006-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement, usually shortened to MORE, is a structured behavioral therapy for addiction, chronic pain, and opioid misuse. It combines mindfulness training, cognitive reappraisal, and savoring practice: patients learn to notice craving, pain, and stress without reflexively reacting; reinterpret stressful events; and rebuild attention to natural rewards.

The Utah work is the development and clinical testing of this specific treatment model. Eric Garland developed MORE and, while at the University of Utah College of Social Work, led the Center on Mindfulness and Integrative Health Intervention Development and a sequence of Utah-based randomized trials and mechanistic studies.

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the treatment program and major opioid/chronic-pain trials were developed and run through the University of Utah, Salt Lake City VA, and Utah clinical sites.

## Why this might become great work

The opioid crisis exposed a hard clinical gap: many people live with chronic pain, long-term opioid exposure, craving, negative affect, and diminished responsiveness to ordinary sources of reward. Medication-assisted treatment and safer prescribing matter, but they do not by themselves solve pain, stress, anhedonia, and relapse risk.

If MORE proves scalable beyond specialized research teams, it could become a clinically useful non-drug intervention for a large patient group that sits between pain medicine, addiction treatment, behavioral health, and primary care. The world-scale claim would be strongest if it reduces opioid misuse, pain burden, craving, relapse, or dose escalation in ordinary clinics, not just in tightly controlled trials.

## Signals so far

- A 2013 treatment manual from University of Utah-linked work presented MORE as a clinician-ready program for addiction, stress, and pain
- Early randomized studies from the University of Utah group tested MORE for chronic pain patients receiving opioid analgesics and reported reductions in pain-related attentional bias, opioid misuse risk, craving, and pain burden
- NIH/NIDA R01DA042033 supported a full-scale University of Utah trial comparing MORE with supportive therapy for chronic pain and prescription opioid misuse in primary care
- The completed ClinicalTrials.gov record for NCT02602535 lists the University of Utah as sponsor and Eric Garland as principal investigator for a full-scale MORE trial in primary care
- The 2022 JAMA Internal Medicine randomized clinical trial recruited patients from six University of Utah primary care clinics and found MORE superior to supportive group therapy for co-occurring chronic pain and opioid misuse outcomes
- Mechanistic work in Science Advances and JAMA Psychiatry reported evidence that MORE can shift reward processing away from opioid cues and toward natural reward / positive-emotion regulation signals
- NIH NCCIH and HEAL materials have treated MORE as a notable non-drug treatment strategy for pain and opioid-use-disorder research
- A completed University of Utah ClinicalTrials.gov pilot, NCT04902339, tested near-infrared spectroscopy neurofeedback as an adjunct to MORE for chronic pain

## What would upgrade this

- Multisite, independent randomized trials led outside the founding Utah / Garland network with durable effects on opioid misuse, pain, quality of life, relapse, and healthcare utilization
- Pragmatic implementation data from primary care, VA, pain, addiction, or community behavioral-health settings showing that ordinary clinicians can deliver MORE well
- Evidence that MORE reduces opioid dose escalation, overdose-risk markers, emergency visits, relapse, or disability compared with credible active controls
- Training, fidelity, reimbursement, and workforce models that make the intervention scalable without diluting the active ingredients
- Guideline inclusion or health-system adoption based on replicated clinical utility, not only mechanistic plausibility

## Caveats

- Behavioral interventions often show strong results in expert-led trials but lose effect size when moved into routine practice.
- MORE is not a substitute for medication-assisted treatment, harm reduction, safer prescribing, or structural responses to addiction and pain.
- The evidence base is still heavily associated with the founding investigator network, even when collaborators and sites vary.
- Blinding is inherently difficult in psychotherapy trials, and expectancy, group support, therapist skill, and participant selection can influence outcomes.
- The intervention may require trained facilitators and patient engagement that are hard to sustain in overloaded clinics.
