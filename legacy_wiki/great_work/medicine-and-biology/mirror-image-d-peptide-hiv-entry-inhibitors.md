# Mirror-Image D-Peptide HIV Entry Inhibitors

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a University of Utah / Navigen antiviral-design program with strong peer-reviewed and early clinical signals, but no approved drug or proven patient benefit yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Antiviral drug-discovery method / therapeutic candidate
**Era:** 2000s-present
**Location:** University of Utah and Navigen, Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the core D-peptide design work came from Michael Kay's University of Utah biochemistry lab, with Navigen in Salt Lake City translating the HIV and Ebola/filovirus programs.

## What it is

The University of Utah's Kay lab developed mirror-image D-peptide inhibitors that bind conserved viral-entry intermediates. D-peptides are made from D-amino acids, the mirror-image form of ordinary biological L-amino acids, which makes them resistant to proteases and potentially useful for long-lasting drugs.

The clearest Utah candidate is CPT31, also called cholesterol-PIE12-trimer: a cholesterol-conjugated D-peptide HIV entry inhibitor designed to bind the conserved hydrophobic pocket in HIV gp41 as the virus attempts membrane fusion. The same lab/company approach also generated Ebola/filovirus target and D-peptide inhibitor work, but the HIV entry-inhibitor line has the strongest public evidence.

## Why this might become great work

HIV therapy is one of medicine's success stories, but it still depends on lifelong combinations, adherence, resistance management, toxicity tradeoffs, and access. A long-acting entry inhibitor with a distinct target could be useful for prevention, treatment, salvage therapy, or resistance scenarios.

The broader claim is methodological. If mirror-image phage display and D-peptide optimization can reliably produce protease-resistant inhibitors against conserved viral or bacterial interfaces, Utah's work could help establish a new therapeutic class for targets that are hard for small molecules and ordinary biologics.

## Signals so far

- A 2010 *Journal of Virology* paper from University of Utah and collaborators described PIE12-trimer as a potent D-peptide HIV entry inhibitor with broad activity and a strong resistance barrier
- A 2018 *Molecular Pharmaceutics* paper from University of Utah and Navigen optimized pharmacokinetics and synthesis for extended-release delivery of the D-peptide HIV entry-inhibitor candidate
- A 2019 *Retrovirology* paper characterized resistance to PIE12-trimer and found that viral resistance required prolonged passaging compared with other entry-inhibitor settings
- A 2020 *PNAS* nonhuman-primate study reported that CPT31 prevented SHIV acquisition in rhesus macaques, reduced viral load by about 2 logs in chronic infection before resistance emerged, and prevented rebound after cART discontinuation in a controlled setting
- ClinicalTrials.gov mirrors list NCT04672083 as a completed Phase 1 single-ascending-dose study of CPT31 in healthy adults, sponsored by Navigen with NIAID collaboration
- University of Utah's Kay lab describes cholesterol-conjugated PIE12-trimer as its most advanced HIV D-peptide entry inhibitor and says it completed a Phase 1 clinical trial
- NIH/SBIR records show Navigen Phase II support for protease-resistant D-peptide Ebola entry inhibitors, tying the platform to broader viral-entry work

## What would upgrade this

- Published Phase 1 CPT31 safety, pharmacokinetic, immunogenicity, and tolerability results
- Phase 2 patient or PrEP data showing safe antiviral activity, practical dosing, and a useful role beside existing long-acting HIV medicines
- Evidence that resistance, manufacturing cost, injection burden, and formulation can be managed in real-world use
- Continued active development, licensing, or partnership that moves CPT31 or a successor beyond a completed first-in-human trial
- A second D-peptide therapeutic from the Utah platform reaching animal efficacy or human testing in another disease area

## Caveats

- HIV already has highly effective treatment and prevention options, including long-acting agents; CPT31 must show a distinct advantage, not just a clever mechanism
- The macaque study showed monotherapy resistance in chronic uncontrolled infection, so clinical positioning would matter
- Public sources suggest CPT31 completed Phase 1, but no widely visible later-stage trial or approval has surfaced
- D-peptide manufacturing, formulation, immunogenicity, cost, and delivery could limit adoption even if the biology works
- Ebola/filovirus D-peptide work is promising but appears earlier and should not be treated as a proven therapy
