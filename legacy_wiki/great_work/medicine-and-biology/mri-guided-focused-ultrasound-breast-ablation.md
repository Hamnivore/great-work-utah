# MRI-Guided Focused Ultrasound Breast Ablation

**Speculative tier:** P-B - could matter if it safely replaces surgery for selected breast cancers, but it is still in early feasibility testing
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / image-guided cancer therapy
**Era:** 2012-present
**Location:** University of Utah Health / Huntsman Cancer Institute, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The MUSE Magnetic Resonance Guided Focused Ultrasound system is a breast-specific device for noninvasive tumor ablation. It combines MRI localization and temperature monitoring with focused ultrasound energy that heats and kills targeted tissue without an incision.

The system was developed at the University of Utah by Allison Payne and colleagues in radiology, imaging sciences, and engineering. The first prototype was developed in 2012, and the first U.S. patient was treated with the system at University of Utah Health on February 13, 2023. The current clinical study is an early feasibility trial: patients undergo partial ablation of up to half of a tumor, then receive standard surgical resection so pathologists can verify what the device did without compromising ordinary care.

## Why this might become great work

Breast cancer treatment has already moved toward less invasive care: lumpectomy instead of mastectomy where possible, sentinel-node biopsy instead of full dissection, and more precise radiation and systemic therapy. If MRI-guided focused ultrasound can reliably eliminate selected early breast tumors without surgery, it would be a major shift in patient experience: outpatient treatment, no incision, less scarring, and potentially fewer surgical complications.

The broader prize is a reusable platform for image-guided noninvasive oncology. Focused ultrasound is already important in other settings, but breast tumors pose specific targeting, motion, thermal-dose, and workflow problems. A Utah-built system that solves those problems could become part of the next stage of organ-preserving cancer care.

## Signals so far

- University of Utah researchers developed a breast-specific MR-guided focused-ultrasound system and reported a first prototype in 2012
- The first U.S. patient treatment with MUSE occurred at University of Utah Health in February 2023
- The active Phase 1 / feasibility trial is registered as NCT05291507 and sponsored by the University of Utah
- The National Cancer Institute lists the Huntsman Cancer Institute / University of Utah trial as active, with objectives including safety, tolerability, ablation efficacy, and longer-term follow-up
- The study design preserves standard care by ablating only part of the tumor before surgical removal

## What would upgrade this

- Published feasibility results showing accurate ablation, acceptable pain, low device-related adverse events, and strong pathology correlation
- A larger trial demonstrating complete tumor ablation in selected patients
- A pivotal trial showing noninferiority or clear patient-centered benefit versus surgery for an appropriate breast-cancer subgroup
- FDA clearance or approval with real-world adoption by breast oncology programs
- Evidence that the procedure reduces surgical burden without increasing recurrence, missed residual disease, or inequitable access

## Caveats

- This is early. The current study is partial ablation followed by surgery, not surgery replacement
- Breast cancer is biologically heterogeneous; a device that works for some small localized tumors may not generalize
- Focused ultrasound must prove not only that it can heat tissue, but that it can reliably treat the full clinically relevant tumor volume with adequate margins
- MRI-guided procedures can be expensive and operationally complex, which may limit access even if the technology works
