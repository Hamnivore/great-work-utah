# Multi-Antigen T-Cell Hybridizers

**Speculative tier:** P-B - strong preclinical Utah science with a clean problem statement; still needs manufacturing, toxicology, and human clinical proof
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Cancer immunotherapy platform / modular T-cell engager
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Multi-Antigen T-Cell Hybridizers, or MATCH, are a University of Utah cancer-immunotherapy platform that splits a bispecific T-cell engager into two modular pieces. One piece recognizes cancer-cell antigens; the other engages T cells. Complementary morpholino oligonucleotides make the pieces self-assemble, so different cancer-targeting fragments can be swapped or combined without rebuilding a new permanent multispecific antibody each time.

The work grew out of the Kopecek/Yang lab, Huntsman Cancer Institute collaborators, and University of Utah molecular pharmaceutics and biomedical engineering. Thera-T Pharmaceutics is the startup vehicle for translating the platform.

## Why this might become great work

Modern T-cell engagers and CAR-T therapies can work dramatically, but they struggle with antigen escape, cytokine release, exhaustion, and patient-to-patient tumor heterogeneity. A modular two-component system could make T-cell recruitment more tunable: target a patient's actual antigen profile, adjust dosing of the tumor-binding and T-cell-binding pieces separately, and potentially reduce toxicity without losing efficacy.

If MATCH can do that in humans, it would matter beyond one blood cancer. It would be a programmable architecture for safer, more personalized immune engagement.

## Signals so far

- A 2024 ACS Nano paper introduced MATCH across CD20, CD38, BCMA, and SLAMF7 targets and showed cancer-specific T-cell activation in blood-cancer models
- A 2025 Advanced Healthcare Materials paper extended the approach for multiple myeloma and included patient-derived bone marrow sample validation
- A 2025 Nanomedicine paper reported that two-step antigen pre-targeting could reduce cytokine release without forfeiting efficacy in preclinical models
- University of Utah's Technology Licensing Office named Tommy Gambles' MATCH work a 2025 Breakthrough of the Year
- The platform has support from the University of Utah Therapeutics Accelerator and Huntsman Cancer Institute collaborators

## What would upgrade this

- IND-enabling toxicology and manufacturability data
- First-in-human trial showing controllable cytokine release and meaningful tumor response
- Evidence that patient-specific antigen matching improves outcomes versus fixed bispecific antibodies
- Durable responses in multiple myeloma, lymphoma, leukemia, or another hard hematologic malignancy
- A repeatable process for rapid, reliable assembly of personalized MATCH component sets

## Caveats

- The current evidence is preclinical
- Manufacturing and quality control may be harder for a modular multi-component therapy than for a conventional antibody
- Bispecific antibodies, CAR-T, and other cell therapies are moving quickly, so MATCH must prove a real clinical advantage
- Tumor antigen expression changes over time, which could complicate personalization
