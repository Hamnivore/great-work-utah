# Multiplexed Medical Radioisotope Production

**Speculative tier:** P-A - could become nationally important supply-chain infrastructure for radiopharmaceuticals if commercial production performs at claimed scale
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Radiopharmaceutical supply-chain infrastructure / accelerator physics
**Era:** 2021-present
**Location:** West Valley City, UT

## What it is

This is the work of producing many medically useful radioisotopes from a Utah-based accelerator platform, rather than relying on a small number of aging reactors and fragile international supply chains. The technical idea is multiplexing: using a heavy-ion accelerator, fast-pulse modulators, switching magnets, and target systems to produce multiple isotopes in a single run.

The immediate target is the radiopharmaceutical bottleneck. Modern cancer diagnostics and targeted radiotherapies increasingly need isotopes such as lutetium-177, actinium-225, astatine-211, copper-64, and copper-67. Supply shortages can delay clinical trials, constrain treatment, and make otherwise promising drugs hard to scale.

## Why this might become great work

If the West Valley City facility becomes a reliable domestic source of scarce isotopes, it could unlock a larger U.S. radiopharmaceutical ecosystem. The work would matter not because a factory opened, but because radioisotope scarcity is one of the hidden physical limits on next-generation cancer care.

The especially interesting claim is flexibility. A platform that can switch among dozens of isotopes and produce several at once would be more resilient than single-isotope supply chains tied to reactor schedules, target availability, and international logistics.

## Signals so far

- A 190,000-square-foot Utah production facility opened in August 2025
- The platform is reported to be capable of producing more than 40 radioisotopes, with up to 12 in a single run
- Nusano announced successful testing of klystrons and fast-pulse modulators for steering a heavy-ion beam
- Commercial supply agreements and reservations have been announced for isotopes used in oncology diagnostics and therapeutics
- The company announced a stable-isotope milestone enriching gadolinium-160 to 99.2% purity, relevant because stable isotopes are target materials for radioisotope production

## What would upgrade this

- Routine commercial delivery of multiple clinically important isotopes
- Demonstrated reliability through shortages that affect reactor-based suppliers
- Radiopharmaceutical developers citing the Utah supply as enabling trials or launches that otherwise would have been delayed
- Independent confirmation of yield, purity, cost, and uptime

## Caveats

- As of this entry, the strongest signals are facility opening, equipment milestones, and supply agreements; sustained commercial throughput is the real test
- Nuclear and radiopharmaceutical supply chains are regulated and operationally unforgiving
- The work is supply-chain infrastructure, so its impact may be invisible if it succeeds: patients simply get treatments on time
