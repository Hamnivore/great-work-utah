# Myriad Genetics — BRCA Gene Sequencing and Testing

**Tier:** A — first to sequence BRCA1/2; created the clinical market for genetic cancer risk testing; triggered a landmark Supreme Court ruling on gene patents
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Company / scientific discovery
**Era:** 1991–present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Myriad Genetics was founded in 1991 by Mark Skolnick, then a geneticist at the University of Utah's Center for Genetic Epidemiology. The company's founding mission was to sequence the genes associated with hereditary breast and ovarian cancer and develop diagnostic tests based on them.

In October 1994, Myriad's team published the sequence of BRCA1 in *Science* and immediately applied for a patent. In December 1995, Myriad announced the sequencing of BRCA2, filed a patent, and deposited the sequence in GenBank. Myriad then commercialized the only clinically available tests for BRCA1/2 mutations in the U.S., holding what amounted to a monopoly on hereditary breast cancer genetic testing for nearly two decades.

The monopoly ended with *Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, Inc.*, a 2013 U.S. Supreme Court case decided 9-0: naturally occurring gene sequences cannot be patented. The ruling immediately opened the BRCA testing market to competitors, dramatically reducing test costs.

## Why it mattered

Knowing whether you carry a BRCA1/2 mutation is among the most actionable pieces of genetic information a person can have — carriers have a 50–70% lifetime risk of breast cancer and up to 44% risk of ovarian cancer. Myriad's tests enabled preventive surgeries and surveillance programs that have saved thousands of lives. The company also demonstrated that genetic risk testing was a viable clinical and commercial category, opening the path to the broader consumer genomics industry.

The Supreme Court ruling was equally significant: it established that raw biological information — genes as they exist in nature — is not patentable, which opened genetic medicine to competition and accelerated research that had been blocked by patent licenses.

## The hard problem they solved

Locating, sequencing, and validating a disease-linked gene across a genome of 3 billion base pairs, building a clinical lab capable of reliably sequencing patient samples, getting FDA and insurance coverage, and turning it into a scalable commercial operation — all before genomics was an established industry. In 1991, no one had done this end-to-end.

## Lasting impact

- Myriad's BRACAnalysis test has been administered to millions of patients worldwide; it remains the reference standard for BRCA testing
- The 2013 Supreme Court ruling reshaped gene patent law globally and immediately produced price competition in genetic testing (BRCA test costs dropped from ~$3,000 to under $300 within years of the ruling)
- The BRCA story — including Angelina Jolie's 2013 New York Times op-ed disclosing her BRCA1 mutation and preventive mastectomy — elevated public awareness of genetic cancer risk more than any scientific paper could
- Myriad's work on Utah's multigenerational genealogical records (the LDS community's extensive family histories) was instrumental in identifying BRCA2 — the Utah genealogical database was used to trace hereditary cancer patterns across families

## Key people

- **Mark Skolnick** — founder; U of U geneticist who developed the cancer genealogy approach using Utah's family history records; co-discoverer of BRCA1/2
- **Alexander Kamb** — early researcher who contributed to BRCA1 sequencing
- **Donna Shattuck-Eidens** — key researcher in BRCA1 isolation

## Utah-specific note

Utah's large, multigenerational, well-documented LDS families were essential to the BRCA research. The Utah Population Database — a genealogical and medical records linkage project at the U of U — enabled researchers to trace hereditary cancer patterns across thousands of family members in ways impossible anywhere else. The BRCA discovery is partly a product of Utah's unique combination of deep genealogical records and a university willing to use them.

## Caveats

- Myriad's patent strategy — enforcing monopoly control over BRCA testing for nearly two decades — prevented competition, kept prices high, and blocked research by other labs; this is a significant ethical mark on the company's legacy
- The BRCA1 race was competitive; researchers at UC Berkeley and NIH were close behind; Myriad won by months, not years
- Post-Supreme Court, Myriad has faced class action suits and continued controversy over data hoarding (it holds the world's largest proprietary database of BRCA variants and has resisted sharing it publicly)
