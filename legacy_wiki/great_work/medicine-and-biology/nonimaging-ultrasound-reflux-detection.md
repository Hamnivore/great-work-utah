# Nonimaging Ultrasound Reflux Detection

**Speculative tier:** P-C - Utah-origin noninvasive diagnostic-device work with NSF Phase II and feasibility papers, but no FDA clearance or clinical-utility proof yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Diagnostic device / noninvasive ultrasound monitoring
**Era:** 2015-present
**Location:** Clearfield / Kaysville and University of Utah, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - RefloDx is a Utah company spun out of the University of Utah Center for Medical Innovation, NSF records list a Utah award location, and early papers list Utah company and University of Utah/Primary Children's affiliations.

## What it is

RefloDx is developing SondeFlux, a nonimaging pulse-echo ultrasound device meant to detect and characterize reflux-related bolus movement in the upper esophagus from sensors placed externally on the neck. Instead of producing diagnostic images, the system looks for acoustic signatures created when the esophagus expands around liquid, food, or refluxed material.

The specific Utah work is a noninvasive laryngopharyngeal reflux and aspiration-risk monitoring method: portable ultrasound hardware, custom transducers, signal-processing algorithms, and a proposed home-monitoring workflow that could avoid nasal wire pH and impedance probes in some patients.

## Why this might become great work

Reflux-related throat, voice, cough, aspiration, and airway symptoms are common and often hard to pin down. Current objective tests can be invasive, uncomfortable, expensive, difficult in children, and weakly matched to intermittent symptoms.

If a wearable or externally placed ultrasound system can reliably track reflux events over 24 hours at home, clinicians could get objective event timing, frequency, direction, duration, and symptom correlation without putting probes through a patient's nose. The world-scale version would be a cheap, comfortable diagnostic layer for a messy and overtreated symptom category.

## Signals so far

- A 2020 *Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery* feasibility study reported nonimaging pulse-echo ultrasound signatures for liquid and food boluses in the proximal esophagus
- A 2024 *Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery* paper further reported noninvasive bolus-transit detection in the cervical esophagus using ultrasound characteristics
- NSF Award 2208084 provided a $1 million SBIR Phase II award to RefloDx for a noninvasive device to detect and characterize bolus presence in the upper esophagus
- NSF describes the project as pulse-echo ultrasound plus proprietary algorithms to create an acoustic signature for detecting and differentiating esophageal bolus events
- RefloDx says the device is patented and intended for extended 24-hour-plus home monitoring of laryngopharyngeal reflux
- The company states that it spun out of the University of Utah Center for Medical Innovation and has support from NSF and Utah's USTAR/TAP ecosystem

## What would upgrade this

- A prospective clinical study comparing SondeFlux against pH-impedance monitoring, endoscopy-adjacent evaluation, aspiration events, or validated symptom outcomes
- FDA clearance or a documented regulatory pathway with intended-use claims, sensitivity, specificity, false-positive burden, and usability data
- Pediatric, adult, and geriatric datasets showing the system works across neck anatomy, swallowing patterns, posture, speech, sleep, and everyday movement
- Evidence that clinicians change management based on the data and patients avoid unnecessary drugs, procedures, visits, or hospital observation
- Independent studies outside RefloDx and University of Utah-connected investigators

## Caveats

- SondeFlux is not commercially available and RefloDx states it has not been FDA-cleared
- Detecting bolus movement is not automatically the same as diagnosing clinically meaningful reflux disease or aspiration risk
- Neck-worn or externally placed sensors may struggle with motion, anatomy, placement, calibration, speech, sleep, and mixed airway/esophageal signals
- Reflux is clinically heterogeneous; objective detection must still improve decisions, not merely produce more data
- The current case is promising but early, so P-C is the right holding tier until larger clinical validation appears
