# Noninvasive Deep-Brain Ultrasound Neuromodulation

**Speculative tier:** P-A - a University of Utah-origin neuromodulation platform with peer-reviewed human pilot data and a device-company path, but no pivotal evidence or regulatory clearance yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / neuromodulation / focused ultrasound
**Era:** 2018-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the DIADEM device, skull/hair attenuation-correction method, and early chronic-pain/depression clinical studies came from University of Utah biomedical engineering and clinical collaborators in Salt Lake City.

## What it is

Noninvasive deep-brain ultrasound neuromodulation is the University of Utah and SPIRE Therapeutics effort to use low-intensity focused ultrasound to modulate deep brain circuits without implants or ablative surgery. The specific Utah work is not therapeutic ultrasound in general. It is the DIADEM platform and its relative through-transmit correction method: measuring how a person's head, skull, hair, and acoustic coupling attenuate ultrasound, then compensating so a specified deep brain target receives a controlled low-intensity dose.

The first disease targets are chronic pain and treatment-resistant depression. In both cases, the bet is that a short, non-ablative stimulation session can change activity in deep circuit nodes such as the anterior cingulate cortex or subcallosal/subgenual cingulate.

## Why this might become great work

Deep brain stimulation can help some patients with severe neurologic or psychiatric disease, but it requires implanted hardware. Existing noninvasive stimulation methods are easier to use but struggle to reach small deep targets with precision. A safe, repeatable, noninvasive way to modulate deep circuits would create a new middle path between external brain stimulation and neurosurgery.

If the Utah approach survives larger trials, it could matter across chronic pain, depression, addiction, tremor, and other circuit disorders. The world-scale version is a therapeutic platform: targeted deep-brain neuromodulation delivered without implants, lesions, or operating-room infrastructure.

## Signals so far

- A 2024 Communications Engineering paper reported the relative through-transmit method and showed that it could restore intended ultrasound intensity through ex-vivo human skulls and enable fMRI-visible modulation of deep brain regions in humans
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists the University of Utah-sponsored chronic-pain study of low-intensity transcranial focused ultrasound as completed, with 47 actual participants, a single University of Utah site, and no results posted on the registry as of its May 2025 update
- A 2024 Pain paper from University of Utah authors reported a double-blind randomized sham-controlled pilot study for generalized chronic pain
- A 2025 Biological Psychiatry paper reported a randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled pilot study in 22 participants targeting the subcallosal cingulate in treatment-resistant depression
- The related depression trial registry record is active-not-recruiting in secondary trial listings, with a University of Utah sponsor and no sign yet of a pivotal or multicenter study
- Published and indexed Utah work also includes essential tremor modulation and methods for controlled ultrasonic interventions through the skull
- SPIRE Therapeutics is headquartered in Salt Lake City and describes DIADEM as a device designed and built from the Utah research program

## What would upgrade this

- A larger multicenter randomized trial shows durable benefit for chronic pain, depression, or another indication with credible sham control
- FDA clearance, approval, breakthrough-device progress with clinical data, or another regulator-visible path establishes safety and effectiveness
- Independent groups reproduce target engagement and clinical benefit using the same or comparable correction method
- Treatment effects last long enough, and can be repeated safely enough, to matter in real-world care
- The device becomes practical outside a small expert lab, including targeting, workflow, training, reimbursement, and adverse-event monitoring

## Caveats

- Current human evidence is still early and small, even where sham-controlled
- The best clinical signals are single-center pilot studies; they show target engagement and short-term symptom effects, not yet durable clinical utility
- Neuromodulation trials are vulnerable to placebo effects, regression to the mean, expectancy, and endpoint selection
- The mechanism of low-intensity ultrasound neuromodulation is still an active research area
- Commercial claims about broad indications need to be separated from indication-specific clinical data
- The chronic-pain ClinicalTrials.gov record states that the study does not involve a U.S. FDA-regulated device product, so registry status should not be mistaken for FDA clearance or approval
- Focused ultrasound already has strong clinical use in ablation; this entry is about reversible low-intensity neuromodulation, which has a higher proof burden
