# Novel Ribosomal Antibiotics for Drug-Resistant Bacteria

**Speculative tier:** P-B - University of Utah-origin antibiotic-discovery work with NIH, CARB-X, patent, and translational signals, but still preclinical
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Antibiotic discovery / medicinal chemistry / infectious-disease therapeutic platform
**Era:** 2013-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City / Provo, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the technology originated in University of Utah labs, Curza is Utah-based, and federal award records list Utah performance and recipient addresses.

## What it is

Novel ribosomal antibiotics are Curza's attempt to build new small-molecule antibacterial classes for multidrug-resistant pathogens by targeting an underused binding site on the bacterial ribosome. The lead CZ-02 program is described as a broad-spectrum antibiotic effort focused on MDR Gram-negative pathogens, inspired by a natural-product scaffold and re-engineered through medicinal chemistry to improve activity, selectivity, and drug-like properties.

The Utah work is the discovery and translation package: University of Utah chemistry and microbiology labs generated the underlying antimicrobial programs, Curza licensed and developed them, and federal awards supported preclinical optimization toward resistant Gram-negative and Gram-positive infections.

## Why this might become great work

Antibiotic resistance is one of the places where scientific failure shows up as ordinary clinical catastrophe: infections that used to be treatable become dangerous again. New antibiotic classes are especially valuable because resistance to old mechanisms can make incremental variants less useful.

If Curza's ribosomal compounds reach the clinic and work against MDR Gram-negative infections with a manageable safety profile, the Utah contribution could matter globally. A genuinely new class with low cross-resistance would be useful even if reserved for severe infections, because hospitals need last-line options for pathogens where the existing pipeline is thin.

## Signals so far

- University of Utah described Curza as a spin-off based on technology from the labs of chemistry professor Ryan Looper and microbiology professor Dustin Williams
- SBIR.gov lists a 2016 NIH Phase I award to Curza for "Natural product inspired antibacterials with unique ribosomal binding," with a Salt Lake City address and a project focused on broad-spectrum antibiotics for Gram-negative bacteria
- The SBIR abstract says the scaffold binds a highly conserved, clinically undrugged region of the ribosome's peptidyl transferase center and was structurally studied with the Steitz lab at Yale
- HHS TAGGS records list multiple Curza NIH awards from Utah addresses, including R01AI132304 for broad-spectrum antibacterials selectively targeting an undrugged ribosomal site and later R44 awards for MDR Gram-negative antibiotics
- CARB-X funded Curza in 2018 to support a new class of antibiotics for drug-resistant Gram-negative bacteria, with milestone-based funding
- Patent records list Curza Global and the University of Utah Research Foundation as assignees on related antimicrobial and antibiofilm compound inventions from Ryan Looper, Dustin Williams, and collaborators
- Curza's current materials describe CZ-02 as a new class of broad-spectrum antibiotics aimed at ESKAPE pathogens and an unexploited bacterial-ribosome binding site

## What would upgrade this

- Published preclinical studies disclose structures, spectra, resistance frequency, pharmacokinetics, toxicity, and efficacy in serious infection models
- An IND opens and Phase 1 data show human safety and exposure consistent with antibacterial activity
- Phase 2 infection data show efficacy against clinically important resistant pathogens
- Independent microbiology labs reproduce the activity and resistance-barrier claims
- The compounds advance through a partner, public-private antibiotic fund, BARDA/CARB-X-style pipeline, or regulator-recognized antimicrobial pathway into real clinical use

## Caveats

- This is still preclinical, and most antibiotic discovery programs fail before approval
- The antibiotic market is commercially difficult; even technically good antibiotics can stall without a viable development and reimbursement path
- The strongest current claims come from grants, company materials, and translational funders rather than peer-reviewed clinical evidence
- Ribosomal antibiotics can face toxicity and resistance surprises after promising early chemistry
- The work is distributed: University of Utah, Curza, Yale structural biology, CARB-X, NIH, and investors all appear in the trail
