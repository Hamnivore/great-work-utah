# Percutaneous Bone Bolt Fracture Fixation

**Speculative tier:** P-B - FDA-cleared University of Utah fracture-fixation system with first clinical use, but still needs peer-reviewed outcomes, commercialization, and adoption evidence
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Orthopedic implant system / translational medical device
**Era:** 2023-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the Bone Bolt System was designed and developed by the University of Utah Orthopaedic Innovation Center, and FDA records list the University of Utah Department of Orthopaedics as the applicant.

## What it is

The Bone Bolt System is a University of Utah-developed implant and instrument platform for percutaneous fixation of difficult bone fractures. It is aimed especially at complex pelvic fractures and long-bone fractures where surgeons need strong stabilization through small incisions.

The system includes implants in multiple sizes plus dedicated instruments and sterilization trays. The specific Utah work is not generic screw fixation; it is a university-built orthopedic device package that moved from clinical/engineering design through FDA 510(k) clearance and early patient use at the University Orthopaedic Center.

## Why this might become great work

Pelvic and complex long-bone fractures are hard cases: fixation must be stable enough for recovery and mobilization, but the surgical corridor may be narrow, deep, and unforgiving. If a percutaneous system can make these cases more stable, efficient, and reproducible, it could reduce surgical morbidity, pain, immobility, hospital time, or revision risk.

The bigger signal is translational infrastructure. Bone Bolt is one of the first visible products from the University of Utah's L. S. Peery, M.D. Orthopaedic Innovation Center pipeline, showing that a Utah academic orthopedic group can design, regulate, supply, and clinically deploy its own implant systems.

## Signals so far

- FDA 510(k) K230867 lists the Bone Bolt System as a bone-fixation screw device, with the University of Utah Department of Orthopaedics as applicant and a June 26, 2023 substantially equivalent decision
- University of Utah Technology Licensing Office reporting says Bone Bolt was designed and developed by the Orthopaedic Innovation Center for percutaneous bone fracture fixation
- The same University of Utah source says Bone Bolt was the first novel medical device in the university's history to receive FDA 510(k) clearance
- The university reported first patient cases in November 2025 at the University Orthopaedic Center for complex pelvic fractures
- University reporting says a biomechanical study found pelvic fractures treated with Bone Bolt were more than 75% more stable than those treated with standard screws after simulated walking cycles
- The system is under a multicenter observational clinical study led by Lucas Marchand, tracking pain, mobility, recovery-at-home days, and hospital stay

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed biomechanical data with anatomy-specific comparisons against cannulated screws and other fixation options
- Published clinical outcomes from the multicenter observational study, including pain, mobility, operative time, complications, revisions, and return-to-function measures
- Commercial licensing, acquisition, or scaled manufacturing that puts the system into trauma centers beyond the originating institution
- Independent surgeon adoption showing the device solves a recurring fixation problem rather than remaining a local innovation-center success
- Evidence of fewer reoperations, better early mobilization, shorter stays, or lower complication rates in well-matched fracture cohorts

## Caveats

- FDA 510(k) clearance allows marketing based on substantial equivalence; it does not prove superior outcomes
- First clinical cases are encouraging but far too early to judge durability, complication rates, or broad surgeon acceptance
- The >75% stability claim appears to come from a study presentation or institutional reporting; peer-reviewed details matter
- Orthopedic trauma outcomes depend on fracture pattern, patient biology, surgical technique, rehabilitation, and hospital systems, not just implant design
- If industry partners do not commercialize and distribute the system, the practical impact may remain bounded
