# Proteomic Preterm-Birth Risk Testing

**Speculative tier:** P-A - validated Utah pregnancy biomarker work with intervention-trial signals, but still awaiting broad clinical adoption, guideline uptake, and replicated real-world outcome impact
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Diagnostic test / maternal-fetal medicine / proteomics
**Era:** 2010s-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - Sera Prognostics is headquartered in Salt Lake City, and multiple analytical-validation and test-development publications list Sera's Salt Lake City team.

## What it is

Proteomic preterm-birth risk testing is Sera Prognostics' attempt to predict spontaneous preterm birth risk from maternal blood proteins measured in mid-pregnancy. Its PreTRM test reports individualized risk for asymptomatic singleton pregnancies, aiming to identify patients whose elevated risk is not obvious from ordinary clinical history.

The Utah work is the biomarker-test platform and clinical translation strategy: measure specific maternal serum proteins, combine them into a risk model, and pair high-risk results with earlier surveillance and established interventions.

## Why this might become great work

Preterm birth is a major cause of neonatal illness, death, NICU use, long-term disability, family trauma, and medical spending. Obstetrics has historically had limited tools for predicting spontaneous preterm birth in otherwise asymptomatic pregnancies. A blood test that reliably finds hidden high-risk pregnancies early enough to change care would be a serious maternal-child-health advance.

The world-scale version would not be prediction alone. It would be a test-and-treat pathway that reduces very early preterm births, neonatal morbidity, NICU admissions, and health inequities in routine prenatal care.

## Signals so far

- A 2017 Clinical Mass Spectrometry paper analytically validated protein biomarkers for spontaneous preterm birth risk, with Sera Prognostics Salt Lake City authors
- Published clinical-validation work includes the PAPR and TREETOP study lines, including a large prospective U.S. cohort registered as NCT02787213
- A 2021 American Journal of Perinatology publication reported results from the PREVENT-PTB randomized clinical utility study of PreTRM-guided intervention
- A 2024 Diagnostics paper reported AVERT PRETERM Trial outcomes after maternal biomarker-guided intervention
- In January 2026, Sera announced publication of the PRIME randomized controlled trial of 5,018 women, reporting reductions in very early preterm birth, NICU admissions, and neonatal morbidity
- The test is commercially available, but the public evidence base is still moving from validation and trials toward payer, guideline, and health-system adoption

## What would upgrade this

- Independent replication of the PRIME-scale clinical-utility result in diverse health systems
- Broad payer coverage and routine prenatal-care adoption without worsening disparities
- Professional-society guideline support that defines who should be tested and how high-risk results should be managed
- Real-world evidence showing fewer very early births, fewer NICU admissions, better neonatal outcomes, and acceptable false-positive burden
- Transparent evidence that test-guided interventions, not selection effects or care-intensity artifacts, drive the outcome gains

## Caveats

- Prediction is only valuable if the downstream interventions reliably improve outcomes
- Some evidence is company-sponsored or company-reported, so independent replication matters
- False positives could create anxiety, extra monitoring, cost, or interventions without benefit
- Preterm birth is biologically heterogeneous; one proteomic signature may not capture all pathways equally well
- Commercial adoption can lag even when clinical evidence is promising, especially in prenatal screening and Medicaid-heavy populations
