# Refillable Local Antibiotic Delivery / Purgo Pouch

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a University of Utah-origin medical-device/drug-delivery platform with FDA Breakthrough Device designation and substantial DoD support, but still preclinical for human use
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / local drug delivery / infection control
**Era:** 2016-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City and South Jordan, UT

## What it is

The Purgo Pouch is a refillable local drug-delivery device intended to sustain high-dose therapy at injury or surgical sites. The initial human target is open fracture-related infection, where contaminated traumatic wounds, implants, dead space, poor vascularity, and bacterial biofilms can make systemic antibiotics and one-time local antibiotic carriers inadequate.

The Utah work began in the University of Utah Department of Orthopaedics and Bone and Biofilm Research Lab. Dustin Williams conceived the pouch, Nicholas Ashton helped co-develop and refine the device toward manufacturability, and David Rothberg contributed trauma-surgery design and clinical input. The technology was licensed from the University of Utah into Purgo Scientific.

## Why this might become great work

Open-fracture infection is a stubborn clinical problem. Infection can drive repeat surgeries, nonunion, amputation, chronic disability, opioid exposure, and high costs. A device that can keep local antimicrobial concentrations high, be refilled after implantation, and work against biofilm-associated infections could change orthopedic trauma, military medicine, and possibly other surgical-site infections.

The bigger claim is not just "a better antibiotic spacer." If refillable local delivery lets clinicians tune therapy over time without repeatedly reopening the wound, it could create a new category between systemic antibiotics, temporary antibiotic beads/spacers, and implanted pumps.

## Signals so far

- University of Utah reporting says the Purgo Pouch received FDA Breakthrough Device designation for treating open fracture-related infections
- The Department of Defense is supporting development with more than $9 million across Congressionally Directed Medical Research Program and Army Medical Research and Development Command funding
- Purgo says the technology was co-invented at the University of Utah in 2016-2017 and exclusively licensed from the University of Utah Research Foundation
- The company reports preclinical sheep studies, including a 138-sheep efficacy study and later safety/pharmacokinetic programs
- Purgo reports a veterinary early-access / alpha program for the related Vetlen Pouch, with hundreds of devices distributed to veterinary hospitals and universities
- The company says it completed FDA sprint discussions and is working through a CDER-led 505(b)(2) pathway for first-in-human studies

## What would upgrade this

- Published preclinical data with transparent infection model, comparator, pharmacokinetics, biofilm outcomes, safety, and device-failure rates
- FDA-cleared or IND-authorized first-in-human trial with meaningful open-fracture infection endpoints
- Human evidence showing lower infection recurrence, fewer debridements, better union, fewer amputations, or lower treatment burden than standard care
- Clear manufacturing and refill protocols that surgeons can use safely in messy trauma settings
- Independent replication or military/trauma-center adoption beyond the founding team

## Caveats

- As of the current public record, the human product is still preclinical; Purgo's own site describes clinical testing as planned rather than completed.
- FDA Breakthrough Device designation is a development and review pathway signal, not proof of safety or effectiveness.
- Local antibiotic delivery has a long history, so the Utah contribution must prove that refillability, dosing control, or biofilm performance changes outcomes rather than merely adding device complexity.
- Open fracture infection trials are difficult because wounds, organisms, implants, surgical timing, and host factors vary widely.
- Company milestone pages are useful signals but need peer-reviewed and regulator-visible confirmation.
