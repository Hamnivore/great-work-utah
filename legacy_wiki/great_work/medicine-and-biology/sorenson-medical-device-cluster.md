# Sorenson Medical Device Cluster

**Tier:** B - a Salt Lake City medical-device cluster whose products became routine hospital infrastructure, though some "first" claims are hard to isolate from broader industry evolution
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Company / medical device innovation / manufacturing cluster
**Era:** 1957-1980s
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

James LeVoy Sorenson built a Utah medical-device company cluster around practical hospital tools: disposable surgical masks, modern disposable venous catheters, blood infusion and recycling systems, hemodynamic monitoring devices, and real-time computerized heart monitoring. The work began after Sorenson left pharmaceutical sales and co-founded Deseret Pharmaceutical in Salt Lake City in 1957, then continued through Sorenson Research, founded in 1962 and later sold to Abbott Laboratories in 1980.

The important contribution was not one glamorous breakthrough. It was the repeated conversion of bedside annoyances and infection risks into disposable, manufacturable, clinician-friendly products that could spread through hospitals.

## Why it mattered

Modern hospital care depends on boring devices that work every time: masks, catheters, tubing, pumps, monitors, anti-clotting systems, and sterile disposables. Sorenson's companies helped push that world forward from reusable, improvised, or labor-intensive tools toward standardized disposable products and electronic monitoring.

The Utah significance is partly industrial. The work helped establish Salt Lake City as a serious medical-device center before the better-known modern Utah medtech ecosystem existed. It also seeded later institutional memory: Utah's medtech identity is not only Jarvik-7 and university research, but a practical manufacturing lineage built around clinician workflow and high-volume hospital products.

## The hard problem they solved

Sorenson's edge was recognizing small clinical frictions that could become large device categories. A good disposable mask had to filter effectively, be cheap enough to throw away, and be manufacturable at high speed. A plastic venous catheter had to be sterile, flexible, reliable, and easy for clinicians to insert. Monitoring systems had to turn messy physiologic signals into timely bedside information.

None of those problems was conceptually exotic, but each required getting materials, manufacturing, sales, and clinical trust right enough for hospitals to change routine practice.

## Lasting impact

- Sorenson and his companies are credited with more than forty medical patents and a long list of hospital devices
- The disposable surgical mask and disposable venous catheter became part of ordinary clinical practice worldwide
- Sorenson Research became valuable enough to be acquired by Abbott, tying Utah device work into a major global healthcare company
- The Sorenson name remains embedded in Utah's medical innovation infrastructure, including the University of Utah's James LeVoy Sorenson Center for Medical Innovation

## Key people

- **James LeVoy Sorenson** - inventor and entrepreneur; co-founded Deseret Pharmaceutical, founded Sorenson Research, and built the broader Sorenson Companies group
- **Deseret Pharmaceutical** - early Salt Lake City company where the disposable mask and catheter work is commonly traced
- **Sorenson Research** - medical-device company founded in 1962 and sold to Abbott Laboratories in 1980
- **Abbott Laboratories** - acquired Sorenson's medical-device business, helping scale the products through a global healthcare company

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. Sorenson moved to Salt Lake City in 1946, began selling pharmaceuticals to local physicians, co-founded Deseret Pharmaceutical there in 1957, and built Sorenson Research in the Salt Lake area. The inventions came from contact with Utah clinicians and were commercialized through Utah companies.

## Caveats

- Sorenson was not a university scientist, and much of the evidence is company history, obituary, and business-press reporting rather than a clean academic record
- "First disposable surgical mask" and "first modern intravenous catheter" claims are repeated by Utah institutions and press accounts, but medical-device categories usually evolve through many incremental contributors
- Some products may have been co-developed with partners or incorporated prior art; Sorenson's role is best understood as inventor-entrepreneur and scaler, not lone inventor in the mythic sense
- The entry is B tier because the impact is real, but the exact boundaries of originality are fuzzier than for entries like BRCA testing or the Jarvik-7
