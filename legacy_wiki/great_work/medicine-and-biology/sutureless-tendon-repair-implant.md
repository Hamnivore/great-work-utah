# Sutureless Tendon-Repair Implant

**Speculative tier:** P-B - FDA-cleared Utah soft-tissue repair device backed by a randomized clinical trial, but adoption and independent outcome evidence remain thin
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / orthopedic and hand-surgery implant
**Era:** 2011-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - CoNextions Medical is a Salt Lake City company, and FDA's 510(k) record lists the CoNextions TR Tendon Repair System applicant at a Salt Lake City address.

## What it is

The CoNextions TR Tendon Repair System is an implant-based alternative to conventional suturing for tendon-to-tendon repair. The device uses two stainless-steel anchors placed into the injured tendon ends and connected by ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene loops spanning the repair site.

The first cleared target is lacerated flexor tendons in the hand, wrist, and forearm, including difficult Zone 2 flexor digitorum profundus injuries. The specific work is not hand surgery in general; it is an attempt to turn tendon approximation from surgeon-tied suture construction into a faster, standardized mechanical coupling system.

## Why this might become great work

Tendon repair is old, demanding, and frustratingly dependent on surgical technique and rehabilitation. Repairs can rupture, form adhesions, restrict motion, require repeat operations, and leave patients with long-term hand disability. Zone 2 flexor tendon injuries are especially hard because the tendons must glide through a tight pulley system after repair.

If a mechanical coupler can make tendon repair faster, stronger, smoother-gliding, and more reproducible without worse infection, stiffness, or foreign-body complications, it could change a basic surgical workflow. That would matter in trauma, hand surgery, occupational injury, military medicine, and global settings where consistent repair quality is hard to maintain.

## Signals so far

- FDA cleared the CoNextions TR Tendon Repair System through 510(k) K203855 on April 22, 2022, with the official record listing ClinicalTrials.gov NCT03622372 as the associated clinical trial
- ClinicalTrials.gov describes NCT03622372 as a pivotal randomized controlled trial comparing the CoNextions TR Implant System with a four-strand locked cruciate repair for Zone 2 FDP tendon lacerations
- CoNextions' clinical trial summary says the study enrolled 90 participants at four South African sites and followed patients for 24 weeks after surgery
- In that company-posted summary, rupture occurred in 1 of 39 CoNextions TR participants versus 5 of 48 suture participants; surgical-site infection occurred in 1 of 37 CoNextions TR participants versus 5 of 45 suture participants
- The same summary reports comparable mobility, pinch strength, grip strength, pain, and DASH disability outcomes between groups, with differences generally not statistically significant
- CoNextions announced first commercial patient use shortly after FDA clearance in May 2022
- FDA's record classifies the device under nonabsorbable steel sterile suture and lists the Salt Lake City applicant address

## What would upgrade this

- Peer-reviewed publication of the pivotal randomized trial with full statistical methods, adverse-event adjudication, rehabilitation adherence, missing-data handling, and subgroup details
- Post-market registry or claims evidence showing lower rupture, infection, adhesions, reoperation, therapy burden, disability, or time-to-function versus conventional repair
- Independent hand-surgery adoption beyond early enthusiasts and distributor-led launch signals
- Evidence that the device works across multiple tendon zones, tendon sizes, and real-world injury patterns without introducing difficult revision problems
- Clear cost-effectiveness evidence if the implant is more expensive than suture

## Caveats

- The most detailed clinical results found in this sweep are company-posted rather than peer-reviewed
- FDA 510(k) clearance shows substantial equivalence for marketing, not proof that the device is clinically superior to sutures
- The pivotal trial's functional outcomes were broadly comparable rather than clearly better, and the lower rupture and infection rates were not reported as statistically significant
- Hand outcomes depend heavily on rehabilitation quality, patient factors, injury mechanism, and surgeon judgment, so device effects may be hard to isolate
- A tendon coupler could be valuable without becoming world-changing if adoption remains narrow or if surgeons prefer familiar suture techniques
