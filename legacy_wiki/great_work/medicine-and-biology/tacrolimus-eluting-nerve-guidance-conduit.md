# Tacrolimus-Eluting Nerve Guidance Conduit

**Speculative tier:** P-B - peer-reviewed University of Utah regenerative-device work with NIH/STTR commercialization support, but no human clinical evidence yet
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Regenerative medical device / drug-eluting biomaterial / peripheral nerve repair
**Era:** 2018-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the conduit work and preclinical publications are University of Utah-affiliated, and NIH/STTR award records list a Salt Lake City company commercializing the device.

## What it is

The tacrolimus-eluting nerve guidance conduit is a biodegradable peripheral-nerve repair device that combines a physical scaffold with localized release of tacrolimus, also known as FK506. Tacrolimus is already used clinically as an immunosuppressant, but in this setting it is being repurposed as a local drug-delivery tool to encourage axonal regeneration and reduce scarring or inflammation across critical nerve gaps.

The Utah work is the conduit and release system: University of Utah researchers fabricated and tested drug-releasing biodegradable materials for peripheral nerve repair, then Microsurgical Innovations pursued commercialization through NIH/STTR funding.

## Why this might become great work

Peripheral nerve injuries can leave patients with chronic pain, sensory loss, weakness, and disability. The best repair for many nerve gaps remains an autograft, which requires sacrificing a patient's own nerve tissue and still may not restore full function. Synthetic conduits are useful for some small gaps but often underperform when the injury is large.

If a biodegradable, drug-eluting conduit can make longer-gap repairs work reliably, it could reduce donor-site morbidity, improve functional recovery, and expand the toolkit for trauma, reconstructive, and hand surgery. The world-scale version would be a nerve-repair product that closes part of the gap between simple conduits and autografts.

## Signals so far

- A 2018 University of Utah paper reported controlled release of FK506 from micropatterned PLGA films as a potential peripheral-nerve repair approach
- A 2024 Biomedical Microdevices paper by University of Utah-affiliated authors reported a biodegradable tacrolimus-eluting nerve guidance conduit tested in 15-mm critical-sized rat sciatic nerve gaps
- The 2024 study reported controlled tacrolimus release, neurite outgrowth support, and improved regeneration versus placebo conduits in the rat model
- SBIR.gov lists a 2025 Phase II NIH/STTR award to Microsurgical Innovations in Salt Lake City for "Tacrolimus-eluting nerve guidance conduit for enhanced regeneration in critical-sized peripheral nerve injuries"
- The STTR abstract frames the device as an FK506-eluting biodegradable conduit intended to address limitations of autografts and synthetic conduits
- Microsurgical Innovations describes its flagship product as an FK506-eluting nerve guidance conduit that combines mechanical support with localized immunosuppressive therapy

## What would upgrade this

- Larger-animal studies show functional recovery, safety, degradation behavior, and dose control over clinically relevant nerve gaps
- Manufacturing data show reproducible drug loading, release kinetics, sterility, shelf life, and mechanical performance
- FDA IDE, 510(k), De Novo, PMA, or other regulator-facing milestones clarify the clinical path
- Human feasibility or pivotal data show better recovery than standard conduits and a credible alternative to autograft in defined injuries
- Surgeons adopt the device because it improves sensory/motor outcomes, reduces donor-site morbidity, or broadens repair options without unacceptable complications

## Caveats

- Rat sciatic nerve gaps are useful but far from proof in human traumatic nerve repair
- Autograft remains a high bar, especially for larger or complex injuries
- Tacrolimus has known systemic risks; local delivery has to prove exposure stays controlled and useful
- Nerve regeneration is slow and outcome measurement is difficult, so clinical trials may be long and expensive
- This may become a niche specialty device rather than a broad change in reconstructive surgery
