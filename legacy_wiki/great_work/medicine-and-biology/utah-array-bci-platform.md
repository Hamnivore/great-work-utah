# Utah Array BCI Platform

**Speculative tier:** P-B - historically important research tool with a plausible path to medical product impact, but commercialization has been slower than early expectations
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Neural interface / brain-computer interface platform
**Era:** 1990s-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The Utah Array is a high-channel-count intracortical microelectrode array originally developed at the University of Utah and later commercialized through Blackrock Neurotech's Utah Array and NeuroPort systems. The recent speculative work is the attempt to turn decades of BCI research into medical systems that restore communication, computer control, prosthetic control, or other function for people with paralysis and neurological disorders.

This entry is framed around the platform, not the company. The important thing is that a Utah-originated neural interface became one of the standard tools for human intracortical BCI research and remains part of the most ambitious path from neural signal to useful action.

## Why this might become great work

If implantable BCIs become practical medical devices, the Utah Array lineage may be one of the field's foundational platforms. It has already powered research in cursor control, robotic arms, sensory restoration, speech decoding, and long-duration human neural recording.

The future impact would be obvious: people who cannot move or speak gaining direct control over computers, communication systems, wheelchairs, prosthetics, or other assistive devices.

## Signals so far

- The NeuroPort Electrode received FDA 510(k) clearance and has been used in human research since 2004
- Blackrock reports more than 30,000 aggregate days of human data collection with no reported serious adverse events related to the NeuroPort Electrode
- Blackrock's MoveAgain BCI system received FDA Breakthrough Device designation in 2021
- The Utah Array / NeuroPort platform has been used across many leading BCI research programs
- A 2024 majority investment by Tether was explicitly framed as funding commercialization and continued R&D

## What would upgrade this

- FDA approval or clearance of a take-home BCI system for paralysis or communication
- Durable patient use outside carefully controlled lab settings
- Insurance reimbursement or hospital adoption that makes the system available beyond a small research cohort
- Clear comparison showing that intracortical arrays provide meaningful function that less invasive approaches cannot match

## Caveats

- Commercial timelines have slipped; Breakthrough Device designation is not approval
- Intracortical implants face surgical, durability, reimbursement, and patient-selection challenges
- The field may shift toward speech BCIs, less invasive approaches, or different electrode architectures
- Blackrock's product claims should be treated separately from peer-reviewed clinical outcomes
