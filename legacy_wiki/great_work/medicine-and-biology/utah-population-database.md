# Utah Population Database (UPDB)

**Tier:** A — the only resource of its kind in the United States; has contributed to identifying genetic factors in 50+ diseases; a foundational instrument of genomic epidemiology
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Research infrastructure / database
**Era:** 1970s–present
**Location:** Huntsman Cancer Institute, University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

The Utah Population Database is a linked genealogical and medical records database maintained at the Huntsman Cancer Institute at the University of Utah. It contains information on more than 11 million individuals, built by linking:
- Extensive LDS church genealogical records (detailed multi-generational family histories for 185,000+ Utah families)
- Utah vital records (births, deaths, marriages)
- Utah Cancer Registry
- Utah hospital discharge records and other medical databases

The result is an unparalleled resource: family trees spanning 4–6 generations, linked to medical outcomes, for a large, relatively homogeneous, well-documented population. No comparable linked resource exists elsewhere in the United States. Researchers at the U of U and worldwide use it to study hereditary disease patterns, identify high-risk families, and locate genetic variants associated with cancer, heart disease, and dozens of other conditions.

## Why it matters

Most genetic epidemiology struggles with small sample sizes and incomplete family histories. The UPDB solves both problems at once. The LDS community's tradition of extensive genealogical record-keeping — motivated by religious practice (proxy baptism for ancestors) — created a documentary foundation that no other U.S. population has. Combined with medical records, it becomes a scientific instrument of extraordinary power.

The BRCA2 discovery (Myriad Genetics, 1995) relied directly on UPDB: researchers traced hereditary breast cancer patterns through multigenerational Utah families, narrowing the chromosomal search space enough to make sequencing tractable. Without the UPDB, the BRCA2 discovery would have been harder and slower.

## The hard problem they solved

Linking genealogical records to medical records across millions of individuals — matching names, dates, and relationships across record types that were created by different institutions for different purposes over 150 years — and maintaining the privacy protections required to make the data accessible to researchers. The UPDB developed record linkage methods that were later adopted by population health researchers worldwide.

## Lasting impact

- Over 400 active research projects use UPDB data at any given time
- Researchers using UPDB have identified genetic contributors to more than 50 diseases including breast cancer, colon cancer, melanoma, heart disease, and diabetes
- The UPDB is the primary reason the University of Utah punches above its weight in cancer genetics globally — it is a resource advantage no other institution can easily replicate
- The record linkage methodology developed for UPDB influenced population health informatics as a discipline

## Key people

- **Mark Skolnick** — founder; the same U of U geneticist who later co-founded Myriad Genetics; recognized early that Utah's genealogical records were a scientific asset
- **Ken Smith** — longtime UPDB director; developed the data linkage infrastructure and made the resource accessible to the wider research community

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct: the database exists because of Utah's unique combination of (1) the LDS church's genealogical record-keeping tradition, (2) the U of U's willingness to treat those records as scientific infrastructure, and (3) a relatively stable, multigenerational, well-documented population. This could not have been built anywhere else in the same way.

## Caveats

- The population is not representative of global human genetic diversity — it is primarily descended from 19th-century Northern European Mormon settlers; findings may not generalize across all ancestries
- Privacy management is an ongoing challenge; the UPDB operates under strict IRB oversight and data access is controlled, but the combination of genealogical and medical data creates sensitivity
- The LDS church's genealogical records were created for religious purposes; their use as a scientific instrument raises questions about informed consent that were not asked when the records were originally created
