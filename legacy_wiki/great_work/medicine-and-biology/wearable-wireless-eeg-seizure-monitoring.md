# Wearable Wireless EEG Seizure Monitoring

**Speculative tier:** P-B - FDA-cleared Utah medical-device work with NIH, clinical-trial, and peer-reviewed validation signals, but still needs broad clinical adoption and outcome evidence
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / EEG monitoring platform / seizure-detection software
**Era:** 2016-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - Epitel is a Salt Lake City company, FDA records list Salt Lake City applicant addresses, and the validation work includes Epitel and University of Utah investigators.

## What it is

Wearable wireless EEG seizure monitoring is Epitel's attempt to make electroencephalography easier to start, easier to tolerate, and usable outside the traditional wired EEG lab. Its REMI platform uses small disposable scalp sensors that amplify and wirelessly transmit single-channel EEG signals; multiple sensors can be combined into a reduced-channel montage for remote review. Later REMI clearances added ambulatory use and AI modules that mark or notify clinicians about potential electrographic seizure events.

The specific Utah work is not EEG itself. It is the miniaturized, wireless, patch-style sensor system, cloud workflow, and seizure-detection software package meant to bring useful EEG monitoring to emergency departments, hospital units, and home or ambulatory settings where conventional full wired EEG is hard to deploy.

## Why this might become great work

Many patients with suspected seizures do not get timely EEG monitoring, and seizure diaries can miss events because patients may not know a seizure occurred. Conventional EEG also requires trained setup, wires, equipment, and specialist review capacity that are unevenly distributed across hospitals and clinics.

If REMI-like systems become routine, they could make longer-duration brain monitoring more available for epilepsy care, acute neurologic evaluation, and pediatric or ambulatory monitoring. The world-scale version would not replace full EEG; it would make EEG-like evidence available in more places, earlier, and for longer periods, changing how suspected seizures are detected and managed.

## Signals so far

- NIH SBIR records describe Epitel's EEG Patch as a wearable, two-electrode, patch-like device for long-term EEG recording in people with epilepsy in everyday environments
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists an Epitel-sponsored study, "A Wireless EEG Patch for Continuous Electrographic Monitoring," comparing patch EEG with standard wired EEG and ambulatory EEG while developing seizure-detection algorithms
- A 2021 Frontiers in Neurology paper by Epitel, University of Utah, and University of Colorado authors evaluated remote review of Epilog/REMI reduced-channel EEG recordings collected alongside traditional video EEG
- FDA 510(k) K203827 cleared REMI in March 2021 as a reduced-montage standard electroencephalograph, with Epitel listed at a Salt Lake City address
- FDA 510(k) K240408 cleared REMI-AI Rapid Detection Module in October 2024 as automatic event-detection software for electroencephalograph data
- FDA 510(k) K243185 cleared an expanded REMI Remote EEG Monitoring System in March 2025 for healthcare and ambulatory settings, with wearable sensors that can transmit EEG for up to 30 days and an indicated age range down to pediatric patients age 1+
- Epitel's product materials describe REMI as intended for healthcare-facility and ambulatory monitoring, with REMI Vigilenz AI marking potential electrographic seizure activity for clinician review

## What would upgrade this

- Independent multicenter studies show that REMI-style monitoring detects clinically important seizures sooner or more often than usual care in emergency, inpatient, pediatric, or ambulatory settings
- Real-world deployments show reduced time to EEG, better treatment decisions, fewer missed seizures, shorter admissions, or lower transfer burden for hospitals without conventional EEG access
- Peer-reviewed evidence shows AI detection helps clinicians without causing unacceptable false alarms, missed events, or workflow burden
- Broad adoption by epilepsy centers, community hospitals, tele-neurology programs, or ambulatory neurology practices
- Reimbursement and clinical guidelines treat reduced-channel wireless EEG as a validated complement to conventional EEG for defined use cases

## Caveats

- REMI is a physiological signal monitor and does not make a diagnosis by itself
- Reduced-channel EEG is not the same as full conventional EEG, so clinical use has to be narrow and evidence-based
- FDA 510(k) clearance establishes substantial equivalence for specified indications, not proof of improved outcomes
- The strongest adoption claims remain early and company-reported
- AI seizure detection can fail if false alarms, missed seizures, artifacts, or review burden overwhelm the practical benefit
