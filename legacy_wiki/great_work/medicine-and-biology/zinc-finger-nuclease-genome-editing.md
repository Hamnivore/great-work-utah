# Zinc-Finger Nuclease Genome Editing

**Tier:** A — foundational pre-CRISPR genome-editing platform work; helped establish targetable nucleases as practical tools
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Genome engineering / molecular biology tool
**Era:** 1990s–2010s
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Dana Carroll's University of Utah lab helped pioneer zinc-finger nucleases (ZFNs) as practical genome-editing tools. ZFNs combine engineered zinc-finger DNA-binding domains with a DNA-cutting nuclease domain, allowing researchers to make targeted double-strand breaks at chosen genomic sites.

Before CRISPR made genome editing broadly accessible, ZFNs were one of the first programmable systems that could cut DNA at a designed location and use the cell's own repair machinery to create targeted mutations or gene replacements. Carroll's lab clarified how ZFN-induced breaks could drive mutagenesis and homologous-recombination repair in living organisms.

## Why it mattered

The key idea of modern genome editing is simple but powerful: if you can cut DNA at a chosen place, the cell's repair systems can be used to rewrite that location. ZFNs proved that this was not just theory. They enabled targeted genome changes in animals, plants, cells, and eventually clinical programs.

CRISPR later made the toolset cheaper and easier, but it arrived into a field that ZFNs and TALENs had already made real. Carroll's Utah work helped establish the conceptual and experimental foundation: targetable nucleases plus DNA repair equals genome engineering.

## The hard problem they solved

Before targetable nucleases, gene targeting in many organisms was slow, inefficient, or unavailable. The hard problem was making a programmable cut that was specific enough to be useful and then learning how cells repaired that cut.

ZFNs were difficult to design and validate compared with CRISPR, but that difficulty was part of the achievement. Researchers had to engineer DNA-binding proteins, manage nuclease dimerization, measure off-target effects, distinguish repair pathways, and show that the approach worked across biological systems.

## Lasting impact

- ZFNs became the first widely recognized programmable genome-editing platform
- Carroll's work helped define the repair logic used by later ZFN, TALEN, and CRISPR systems
- Targetable nuclease methods have been applied to hundreds of organisms
- ZFN-based clinical programs helped open the path toward in vivo and ex vivo gene-editing therapies
- Carroll became a major voice on the science and societal implications of genome editing

## Key people

- **Dana Carroll** — University of Utah biochemist; pioneer of ZFN genome engineering
- **University of Utah Carroll lab members** — developed and tested ZFN-mediated gene targeting in model systems
- **Broader ZFN field, including Sangamo and other groups** — engineered reagents and translated the platform into therapeutic and agricultural applications

## Caveats

- ZFNs were not invented by one person or one lab; the platform emerged from multiple groups combining zinc-finger engineering, FokI nuclease domains, and DNA-repair biology
- CRISPR displaced ZFNs for many research uses because it is easier to design and multiplex
- The strongest Utah claim is Carroll's role in recognizing, proving, and explaining ZFNs as genome-engineering tools, not sole invention of every component
