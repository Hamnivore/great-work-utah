# Jarvik-7 Artificial Heart ⭐

**Tier:** S — first sustained artificial heart implant; redefined what medicine could attempt
**Domain:** Medicine & Biology
**Type:** Medical device / clinical first
**Era:** 1967–1990 (development); December 2, 1982 (landmark implant)
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT (University of Utah)

## What it was

The Jarvik-7 was a pneumatically driven artificial heart developed by Dr. Robert Jarvik under the supervision of Dr. Willem Kolff at the University of Utah's Division of Artificial Organs. On December 2, 1982, surgeon Dr. William DeVries implanted it in Barney Clark, a Seattle dentist with terminal cardiomyopathy. Clark survived 112 days — the first human to live with a permanent mechanical heart. The device was made of polyurethane and aluminum and driven by a 375-pound external air compressor.

## Why it mattered

Before the Jarvik-7, total artificial heart replacement was theoretical. The Clark implant proved that a human could survive with a completely mechanical pump replacing a biological heart — even briefly. It opened the clinical, regulatory, and engineering path for all subsequent mechanical circulatory support: ventricular assist devices (VADs), the CardioWest TAH (a direct descendant), and eventually bridge-to-transplant devices that are now standard of care for end-stage heart failure.

## The hard problem they solved

The heart must pump ~5 liters of blood per minute, 24/7, without clotting, hemolysis, or infection, using materials that the immune system tolerates indefinitely. The Utah team solved enough of these problems to achieve sustained survival — longer than anyone had before — despite working with pneumatic hardware that required a tethered external drive unit.

## Lasting impact

- The CardioWest Total Artificial Heart (now SynCardia) is the direct clinical descendant of the Jarvik-7 and is FDA-approved as a bridge to transplant; it has been implanted in over 1,000 patients
- The clinical trial framework the Utah team developed influenced how the FDA evaluates implantable devices
- Willem Kolff's artificial organs program at Utah also pioneered the artificial kidney (hemodialysis) before the heart work; Salt Lake City was the center of artificial organ research for decades
- Robert Jarvik's later work on smaller, implantable designs (Jarvik 2000) influenced ventricular assist device miniaturization
- Barney Clark's surgery was the first time artificial organ implantation was covered as a live national news story — it permanently changed public expectations of medicine

## Key people

- **Willem Kolff** — Dutch physician who invented hemodialysis and led the U of U artificial organs program from 1967 onward; recruited Jarvik
- **Robert Jarvik** — device designer; developed the series of artificial hearts (Jarvik-3, -5, -7) culminating in the implanted device
- **William DeVries** — surgeon who performed the Clark implant; worked at U of U and later Humana Hospital in Louisville
- **Barney Clark** — first recipient; a retired Seattle dentist; survived 112 days

## Caveats

- The 112-day survival was medically significant but also grueling — Clark suffered multiple strokes and was largely bedridden; the ethics of the trial were debated at the time and remain a bioethics case study
- The external drive unit was never practical for long-term quality of life; the Jarvik-7 was always more proof-of-concept than product
- Willem Kolff had already invented hemodialysis in the Netherlands before coming to Utah; his Utah contribution was the artificial heart program, not his entire career
