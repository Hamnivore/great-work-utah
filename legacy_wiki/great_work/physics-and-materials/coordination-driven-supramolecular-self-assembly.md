# Coordination-Driven Supramolecular Self-Assembly

**Tier:** A - a major University of Utah physical chemistry program that helped define how chemists build complex nanoscale structures from designed molecular parts
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Research program / chemistry method
**Era:** 1990s-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Peter J. Stang's University of Utah group helped develop coordination-driven self-assembly: a strategy for building supramolecular polygons, cages, polyhedra, dendrimers, and other nanoscale architectures by designing metal acceptors and organic donor ligands that assemble through coordination chemistry.

The central idea is powerful because the chemist does not hand-build every bond in a large structure. Instead, molecular components are designed so geometry, symmetry, and coordination preferences cause them to organize into predictable larger forms. The work sits at the boundary of organic chemistry, inorganic chemistry, materials science, and nanoscience.

## Why it mattered

Chemists have long wanted to build complex structures with the reliability of biology: viral capsids, enzymes, membranes, and protein complexes all emerge from local interactions that produce larger order. Coordination-driven self-assembly gave synthetic chemists a practical route toward that kind of designed complexity.

The impact is not a consumer product. It is methodological: a general way to think about making nanoscale containers, hosts, catalysts, sensors, photonic assemblies, and functional materials. Stang's recognition with the 2010 National Medal of Science specifically cited his contributions to organic supramolecular chemistry, and the Utah group became one of the field's central references.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was controlling complexity. If many molecular pieces are mixed together, chemistry usually produces mixtures, side products, and hard-to-separate messes. The Stang group's work showed how geometry-directed design, metal-ligand coordination, and self-selection could produce well-defined structures with predetermined shapes.

That required not just synthesis, but characterization: NMR, mass spectrometry, crystallography, isotope labeling, and kinetic/thermodynamic analysis to prove what had assembled and how dynamic the assemblies were.

## Lasting impact

- Stang's group produced widely cited examples of supramolecular polygons, cages, cuboctahedra, dendrimers, and dynamic supramolecular transformations
- Coordination-driven self-assembly became a standard concept in supramolecular chemistry and materials design
- The work trained chemists who carried self-assembly methods into other institutions and application areas
- Stang's broader scientific role, including service as editor of the *Journal of the American Chemical Society*, helped make Utah a visible node in global chemistry

## Key people

- **Peter J. Stang** - University of Utah chemist; central figure in coordination-driven self-assembly and 2010 National Medal of Science recipient
- **Brian H. Northrop, Yao-Rong Zheng, Hai-Bo Yang, Liang Zhao, Bogdan Olenyuk, Jeffery A. Whiteford, and many collaborators** - Utah-group researchers and collaborators on major self-assembly papers

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. Stang joined the University of Utah faculty in 1969 and built the supramolecular chemistry program there. Many key papers list the University of Utah Department of Chemistry as the lead affiliation, and Stang's National Medal of Science affiliation was the University of Utah.

## Caveats

- Supramolecular chemistry is a broad international field; Jean-Marie Lehn, Donald Cram, Charles Pedersen, Fraser Stoddart, Ben Feringa, Makoto Fujita, and many others shaped adjacent or overlapping ideas
- The entry is about a major research program and method, not a single invention with an easy before/after adoption curve
- Applications in catalysis, sensing, nanomedicine, and materials are real but uneven; the strongest claim is field-shaping science, not direct product impact
