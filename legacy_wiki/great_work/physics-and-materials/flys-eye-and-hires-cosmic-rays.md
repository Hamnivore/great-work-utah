# Fly's Eye and HiRes Ultra-High-Energy Cosmic Rays

**Tier:** A - a University of Utah-led experimental program that detected the highest-energy cosmic ray ever observed and helped establish the observed high-energy suppression in the cosmic-ray spectrum
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Scientific observatory / astroparticle physics research program
**Era:** 1981-2006; key results in 1991, 1995, and 2008
**Location:** Dugway Proving Ground / West Desert, UT; University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Fly's Eye and its successor, the High Resolution Fly's Eye (HiRes), were University of Utah-led cosmic-ray observatories in Utah's West Desert. Instead of catching cosmic rays directly, they watched the atmosphere for faint ultraviolet fluorescence from giant air showers produced when ultra-high-energy particles struck the upper atmosphere.

The original Fly's Eye detector became famous for the October 15, 1991 event later nicknamed the Oh-My-God particle: a cosmic ray with an estimated energy of about 3.2 x 10^20 electron volts, or roughly 51 joules in a single subatomic particle. HiRes then extended the Utah program with improved optics, stereo observing, and years of energy-spectrum measurements.

## Why it mattered

The Oh-My-God particle was not just a record. Its energy was above the level where interactions with the cosmic microwave background should strongly limit how far ordinary protons can travel. That made it a clean, shocking problem for astrophysics: either a nearby source could accelerate particles to absurd energies, the particle was not what scientists assumed, or some piece of propagation or source physics was missing.

HiRes mattered for the complementary reason. After the spectacular outlier, the field needed enough carefully calibrated observations to know whether the predicted Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin suppression was visible in the spectrum. The HiRes collaboration reported the first observation of that suppression at five-standard-deviation significance, turning a famous theoretical expectation into an observed feature of the highest-energy cosmic-ray sky.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was measuring rare, indirect, transient events with enough precision to make particle astrophysics out of them. The interesting particles arrive at vanishingly low rates. They do not land neatly in a lab detector. They create moving, kilometer-scale cascades through the atmosphere, and the experiment has to reconstruct the original particle's energy, direction, and shower development from faint light collected over desert distances.

Fly's Eye solved the proof-of-concept at record energy. HiRes solved the statistical and calibration problem well enough to measure spectrum features: the ankle and the high-energy suppression around 6 x 10^19 eV.

## Lasting impact

- The Oh-My-God particle remains the highest-energy cosmic ray ever observed and a standard reference point in ultra-high-energy cosmic-ray physics
- The 1995 Fly's Eye Astrophysical Journal paper became one of the defining observations behind modern discussions of extreme-energy cosmic-ray origins
- HiRes provided early strong evidence for the GZK suppression, later reinforced by the Pierre Auger Observatory and Telescope Array
- The program created the Utah experimental lineage that led into the Telescope Array west of Delta
- It helped make Utah's West Desert one of the world's important landscapes for astroparticle physics

## Key people

- **Pierre Sokolsky** - University of Utah physicist and central leader in Fly's Eye, HiRes, and later Telescope Array work
- **Eugene Loh, David Kieda, Jerome Elbert, John Belz, Gordon Thomson, John Matthews, Douglas Bergman, and University of Utah collaborators** - Utah faculty, researchers, and students across the Fly's Eye/HiRes lineage
- **Fly's Eye and HiRes collaborations** - multi-institution teams responsible for detector operations, reconstruction, spectrum analysis, and publications

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct. The detectors operated at Dugway Proving Ground and nearby West Desert sites, the program was led from the University of Utah, and the most famous event was detected by the Fly's Eye air-shower detector in Utah. The later HiRes work also used Utah desert detector sites and University of Utah leadership, even as the collaboration included researchers from other institutions.

## Caveats

- The experiments did not identify the sources of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays; they made the mystery sharper and measured key spectrum features
- The GZK suppression result is a collaboration result and part of an international experimental story, with later confirmation and refinement from Auger and Telescope Array
- "Highest-energy cosmic ray ever observed" is a measurement claim with uncertainty, not a claim that no higher-energy particle has ever reached Earth
- The work depends on access to military test-range land, so the geographic conditions that made the science possible are tied to morally complex infrastructure
