# H. Tracy Hall's Diamond Presses

**Tier:** B — important Utah continuation of synthetic-diamond and high-pressure research; the first diamond synthesis happened at GE, but the BYU press work had lasting industrial influence
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Materials research / high-pressure apparatus / company formation
**Era:** 1955–1970s
**Location:** Brigham Young University and Provo, UT

## What it was

H. Tracy Hall was an Ogden-born physical chemist, University of Utah Ph.D., and one of the central figures in the first reproducible synthetic-diamond process at General Electric in 1954. After leaving GE in 1955, Hall returned to Utah as a BYU professor of chemistry and director of research.

At BYU, Hall built new high-pressure apparatuses for diamond and materials research, including tetrahedral and cubic press systems. The tetrahedral press used four anvils converging on a small volume; the cubic press used six. These presses were part of the next chapter of high-pressure/high-temperature diamond synthesis after the original GE belt apparatus.

Hall and BYU colleagues later helped found MegaDiamond in Provo, which manufactured industrial diamonds and high-pressure equipment. The local ecosystem around Hall connected Utah academic chemistry to synthetic diamond production, polycrystalline diamond, and superhard materials.

## Why it mattered

Synthetic diamonds changed cutting, grinding, polishing, drilling, heat spreading, and many forms of industrial manufacturing. The first GE breakthrough did not happen in Utah, so this entry should not steal that credit. The Utah work matters because Hall continued the apparatus innovation here, trained people, started companies, and contributed designs that spread into high-pressure research and industrial diamond production.

The broader significance is that diamond synthesis is not only a chemistry result. It is an equipment problem: how to make extreme pressure and temperature reliably, repeatedly, and economically. Hall's Utah-era press designs helped advance that practical machinery.

## The hard problem they solved

The hard problem was building compact machines that could reach and survive extraordinary pressures without destroying themselves or contaminating the sample. Diamond formation requires high pressure, high temperature, catalysts, precise containment, and repeatability. Small changes in geometry, stress distribution, heating, and sample assembly can make the difference between a useful run and a failed one.

Hall's BYU work attacked that apparatus problem directly by changing the geometry of pressure delivery. The tetrahedral and cubic presses were attempts to make high-pressure research more flexible and productive than the original belt-style system.

## Lasting impact

- Hall's Utah-era work helped keep him central in high-pressure research after the GE diamond breakthrough
- BYU became a site for diamond-press experimentation and high-pressure apparatus development
- MegaDiamond and related Provo-area companies commercialized industrial diamond and high-pressure equipment
- Cubic press concepts became important in global industrial diamond production, especially for diamond powder
- Hall's career helped establish Utah as a small but real node in superhard materials and high-pressure research

## Key people

- **H. Tracy Hall** — synthetic-diamond pioneer; BYU professor; inventor of high-pressure press systems
- **M. Duane Horton and Bill J. Pope** — BYU colleagues associated with the early company effort that became MegaDiamond
- **MegaDiamond / Novatek ecosystem** — Provo-area companies tied to industrial diamond and high-pressure technology

## Caveats

- The S-tier synthetic-diamond breakthrough happened at General Electric in Schenectady, New York, not in Utah
- The Utah entry is therefore about subsequent press innovation and industrial follow-through, not the first synthesis itself
- Accounts of Hall's role and credit at GE are historically charged; this entry should avoid overstating what can be cleanly attributed
- Synthetic diamond had many contributors worldwide, including GE colleagues and later Soviet, Chinese, Japanese, European, and commercial programs
