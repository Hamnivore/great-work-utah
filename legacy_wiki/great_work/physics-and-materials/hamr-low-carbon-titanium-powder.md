# HAMR Low-Carbon Titanium Powder

**Speculative tier:** P-A - University of Utah materials process with ARPA-E support, peer-reviewed validation, R&D 100 recognition, and pilot commercialization, but not yet proven at broad industrial scale
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Materials process / extractive metallurgy / titanium powder production
**Era:** 2013-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - the HAMR and related GSD titanium-powder processes were invented and developed by Zak Fang's University of Utah team, with ARPA-E-funded work and later Utah pilot production.

## What it is

HAMR, hydrogen assisted magnesiothermic reduction, is a thermochemical process for producing low-oxygen titanium powder from titanium dioxide or recycled titanium feedstocks. The key idea is to use hydrogen to destabilize titanium-oxygen solid solutions, making it possible to reduce and deoxygenate titanium oxide with magnesium at lower temperature and avoid parts of the conventional Kroll-process route.

The broader Utah titanium-powder package also includes GSD, granulation-sintering-deoxygenation, for producing spherical titanium alloy powders for additive manufacturing, metal injection molding, hot-isostatic pressing, and other powder-metallurgy routes. Together, these processes aim to make titanium cheaper, lower-carbon, and more recyclable.

## Why this might become great work

Titanium is strong, light, corrosion-resistant, and biocompatible, but its cost and supply-chain complexity keep it out of many applications where it could replace heavier metals. The conventional Kroll process is energy-intensive, capital-intensive, and poorly suited to circular recycling of titanium scrap.

If HAMR/GSD production scales, it could lower titanium cost, reduce energy and emissions, enable more domestic titanium supply, and make high-quality titanium powder more available for aerospace, defense, medical implants, additive manufacturing, and transportation. The world-scale version would not just be a better powder; it would change where titanium can be economically used.

## Signals so far

- University of Utah's Powder Metallurgy Research Lab describes HAMR as a novel process that can produce titanium powder at pilot scale and reports modeling estimates of more than 50% lower energy use and more than 30% lower emissions than the Kroll process
- The University of Utah lab page says the low-cost titanium production project was funded by ARPA-E through a $6.8 million cooperative agreement
- OSTI records list ARPA-E-supported University of Utah papers on HAMR and titanium powder metallurgy under contract AR0000420
- A 2017 Chemical Engineering Journal paper established the hydrogen assisted magnesiothermic reduction route for titanium dioxide, with ARPA-E support acknowledged
- A 2017 Materials Transactions paper reported HAMR of commercial TiO2 to produce titanium powder meeting ASTM B299 general-purpose titanium sponge requirements
- GSD work produced spherical Ti-6Al-4V powder meeting aerospace material specifications in published powder-technology work
- The HAMR process won a 2023 R&D 100 award, with the University of Utah listed as developer and IperionX as co-developer
- IperionX says its titanium metal technologies were invented at the University of Utah, developed with DOE ARPA-E support, and proven at pilot scale for high-quality titanium powders

## What would upgrade this

- Sustained commercial production reaches meaningful tonnage with independent quality, cost, energy, and emissions data
- Aerospace, defense, biomedical, or additive-manufacturing customers qualify HAMR/GSD titanium powders or parts for demanding applications
- Recycled titanium feedstock is used routinely at high yield while maintaining oxygen, morphology, and mechanical-property specifications
- Lifecycle analysis and audited plant data confirm the claimed energy, emissions, and cost advantages versus Kroll and atomization routes
- Multiple plants, licensees, or supply-chain partners adopt the process beyond a single pilot or early commercial site

## Caveats

- Pilot-scale success is not the same as commodity-scale titanium production
- Commercial claims about cost, energy, emissions, and customer qualification need independent verification
- Titanium qualification cycles are slow, especially in aerospace, defense, and medical applications
- Kroll-process economics, scrap availability, feedstock purity, magnesium handling, and deoxygenation control may all complicate scale-up
- IperionX and other partners are now central to commercialization, so the Utah claim should stay focused on the invented process and Utah-origin technology rather than all downstream company execution
