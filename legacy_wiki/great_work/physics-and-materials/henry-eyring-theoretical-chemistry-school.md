# Henry Eyring and the Utah Theoretical Chemistry School

**Tier:** B - a major Utah theoretical-chemistry institution built around one of twentieth-century physical chemistry's central figures, with the most famous Eyring equation predating Utah
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Research program / scientific school / institution building
**Era:** 1946-1981 and after
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

## What it was

Henry Eyring moved from Princeton to the University of Utah in 1946 as professor of chemistry and dean of the Graduate School. He was already famous for absolute reaction-rate theory and the activated-complex picture of chemical reactions. In Utah, he spent the rest of his career building graduate research capacity, teaching theoretical chemistry, publishing widely across statistical mechanics, liquid-state theory, rheology, optical rotation, biological rate processes, aging, cancer, and anesthesiology, and anchoring a local school of theoretical chemistry.

One Utah-specific thread was the significant-structure theory of liquids, developed with Taikyue Ree, Teresa Ree, R. P. Marchi, N. Hirai, and other Utah collaborators. The program treated liquids as having both solid-like and gas-like structural features and used statistical mechanics to calculate thermodynamic and transport properties for dense gases, molten salts, liquid metals, and molecular liquids.

## Why it mattered

Eyring helped make theoretical chemistry a normal part of American chemistry: quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and reaction dynamics were not just mathematical decoration, but tools for explaining real chemical behavior. His Utah years mattered in two ways.

First, the University of Utah gained a nationally visible scientific leader at the moment it was becoming a modern research university. Eyring arrived as dean of the Graduate School, helped strengthen graduate education, and made Utah a credible home for high-level physical and theoretical chemistry.

Second, the Utah liquids and rate-process work extended Eyring's larger program: explaining matter and chemical change from molecular structure, energy barriers, and partition functions. Significant-structure theory was not the final theory of liquids, but it was a serious midcentury attempt to make liquid thermodynamics and transport calculable across difficult systems.

## The hard problem they solved

Liquids are notoriously hard to theorize. Gases can often be approximated as weakly interacting particles; crystalline solids have order. Liquids are dense, disordered, mobile, and strongly interacting. A practical theory had to account for energy, entropy, free volume, molecular association, viscosity, conductivity, and phase behavior without the simple symmetry of a crystal.

The Utah work attacked that middle ground by building statistical-mechanical models that mixed solid-like and gas-like contributions. It also applied the broader Eyring rate-process style to messy systems where chemical kinetics, transport, and structure are coupled.

## Lasting impact

- Eyring's presence helped establish the University of Utah as a serious physical-chemistry and theoretical-chemistry institution
- His Utah group produced a large body of liquid-state, dense-gas, molten-salt, rheology, and rate-process work
- The Henry Eyring Center for Theoretical Chemistry continues as a University of Utah research center spanning chemical physics, materials chemistry, biomolecular simulation, and computational chemistry
- Eyring's textbooks and broad theoretical style trained generations of chemists to treat molecular theory as a practical explanatory tool
- His honors while affiliated with Utah included the 1966 National Medal of Science and the 1980 Wolf Prize in Chemistry

## Key people

- **Henry Eyring** - University of Utah professor of chemistry and metallurgy, dean of the Graduate School, theoretical chemist, National Medal of Science recipient, and Wolf Prize laureate
- **Taikyue Ree and Teresa S. Ree** - major collaborators on significant-structure theory and liquid-state papers at Utah
- **R. P. Marchi, N. Hirai, Kai Liang, Wei-Chen Lu, Chen C. Hsu, and other Utah students and collaborators** - contributors to Utah-era liquid-state and statistical-mechanics work
- **Frank Harris, Jack Simons, and later Henry Eyring Center faculty** - part of the continuing Utah theoretical-chemistry lineage

## Why Utah specifically

The Utah claim is direct for the school and for much of the later research. Eyring joined the University of Utah in 1946 and remained there until his death in 1981. Many significant-structure papers list the University of Utah Department of Chemistry as the institutional home, and Utah's chemistry history treats Eyring's arrival as a major turning point in the university's graduate research program.

## Caveats

- Eyring's most famous contribution, absolute reaction-rate theory and the Eyring equation, was developed before he came to Utah
- The entry should not imply that Utah originated transition-state theory; Utah was the long second act of Eyring's career and the home of a broader theoretical-chemistry school
- Significant-structure theory was influential and productive, but liquid-state theory later evolved through many other approaches, including computer simulation and modern statistical-mechanical methods
- Some of the impact is institutional and pedagogical rather than a single world-changing invention
