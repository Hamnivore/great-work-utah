# Hydrogen-Reduction Melt-Less Steelmaking

**Speculative tier:** P-B - high-leverage industrial decarbonization work with federal validation, but still at research scale
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Powder metallurgy / low-emissions steelmaking process
**Era:** 2024-present
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT

## What it is

Hydrogen-reduction melt-less steelmaking is a University of Utah powder-metallurgy approach for making steel products directly from iron ore concentrate without the usual melting step. The work is led by Pei Sun in Zak Fang's Powder Metallurgy Research Lab and is funded through ARPA-E's ROSIE program, which targets zero-process-emission ironmaking and ultra-low life-cycle-emissions steelmaking.

The specific Utah bet is that steel can move through a powder route: reduce and alloy iron ore concentrate, shape the material, then sinter and hot-work it into useful steel products. If that route works, it could skip several high-temperature, high-energy stages that dominate conventional iron and steel production.

## Why this might become great work

Steel is one of the hardest industrial materials to decarbonize because the conventional blast-furnace route uses carbon both as fuel and as chemistry. A credible Utah-originated route that produces qualified steel products from ore concentrate with far lower energy use and emissions would matter well beyond Utah.

The world-scale version of this work is not a nicer lab process; it is a new path from ore to mill product or near-net-shape component that can compete on cost, product quality, and emissions. That would give cars, buildings, appliances, energy systems, and defense manufacturing a lower-carbon materials base.

## Signals so far

- ARPA-E selected the University of Utah for a roughly $3.5 million ROSIE award, the largest award in that funding round according to the University of Utah College of Science
- The project is explicitly aimed at hydrogen-reduction melt-less steelmaking rather than incremental blast-furnace efficiency
- The University of Utah reports projected energy reductions of at least 50% for steel mill products and up to 90% for near-net-shape steel components if the approach works as planned
- ARPA-E meeting materials list the project as "Producing Clean Steel Directly from Iron Ore Concentrate"
- A 2025 PowderMet abstract describes HRMLES as a powder-metallurgy pathway supported by DOE/ARPA-E and focused on hydrogen reduction from concentrated iron ore
- The work builds on Fang Lab powder-metallurgy experience in titanium, hydrogen-assisted reduction, sintering, and hot-working of powder-derived metals

## What would upgrade this

- Lab-scale samples meet relevant mechanical-property and chemistry specifications for real steel products
- Independent life-cycle and technoeconomic analysis confirms large emissions and energy gains under realistic hydrogen, electricity, ore, and process assumptions
- A pilot line produces repeatable mill products or near-net-shape components, not just coupons
- Industrial partners qualify the material for automotive, structural, tooling, energy, or defense use
- The route demonstrates cost parity or a credible cost premium with low-carbon procurement demand

## Caveats

- The process is still early-stage and federally funded research, not operating industrial production
- Steel qualification is unforgiving: strength, ductility, fatigue, impurity control, microstructure, scale, and cost all have to work at once
- Hydrogen-based processes are only as clean as the hydrogen and electricity supply behind them
- ARPA-E awards are good signals of technical ambition, but many ARPA-E projects never survive scale-up
- The Utah contribution should be tracked as the specific HRMLES powder route, not as a broad claim to all green steelmaking
