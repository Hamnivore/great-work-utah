# ML-Guided Niobium Alloys for High-Temperature Turbines

**Speculative tier:** P-C - University of Utah ARPA-E materials-design work with a peer-reviewed method paper, but still far from qualified turbine hardware
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Materials informatics / refractory alloy design
**Era:** 2021-present
**Location:** University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT

**Utah attribution:** Strong - ARPA-E and OSTI records list the University of Utah materials-science team and Salt Lake City project location.

## What it is

University of Utah researchers are using physical metallurgy, machine learning, and rapid experimental validation to search for multicomponent niobium-based refractory alloys for very high-temperature turbine environments. The ARPA-E ULTIMATE project aimed to identify alloy chemistries and processing conditions that could operate above the limits of current nickel- and cobalt-based superalloys without becoming too brittle at lower temperatures.

The Utah-specific work is the alloy-design workflow: domain-knowledge descriptors, Bayesian optimization, and candidate screening for quaternary and quinary niobium alloys in a sparse-data regime.

## Why this might become great work

Gas turbines in aviation and power generation are constrained by materials. Hotter turbine sections can improve efficiency, but materials must survive heat, oxidation, creep, thermal cycling, manufacturing, and mechanical loads. Nickel superalloys are extraordinary, but their temperature ceiling pushes researchers toward refractory alloys.

If Utah-designed niobium alloys or design workflows help produce turbine materials that operate meaningfully hotter while remaining tough, manufacturable, and protectable, the impact could show up as better aircraft engines, lower fuel use, or more efficient power generation.

## Signals so far

- ARPA-E's ULTIMATE program selected the University of Utah project "Designing Novel Multicomponent Niobium Alloys for High Temperature: Integrated Design, Rapid Processing & Validation Approach" with $800,000 in funding
- ARPA-E project descriptions say the team would use physical metallurgy and artificial intelligence to down-select alloy compositions and processing conditions from millions of possibilities
- A 2023 *APL Machine Learning* paper by Trupti Mohanty, K. S. Ravi Chandran, and Taylor Sparks presented a machine-learning and Bayesian-optimization strategy for niobium alloy composition selection
- OSTI indexes the 2023 paper and records the University of Utah author affiliations
- A 2025 materials paper on spark plasma sintering of C103 niobium alloy acknowledges ARPA-E support through project DE-AR0001426, suggesting experimental follow-through around niobium alloy processing
- Refractory-metals conference material lists University of Utah work on new niobium alloys with high strength and toughness for high-temperature applications

## What would upgrade this

- Experimental alloy coupons matching or beating predicted strength, toughness, creep, oxidation, and thermal-cycling targets
- Peer-reviewed comparisons against C103, other niobium systems, molybdenum systems, and state-of-the-art nickel superalloys under relevant turbine conditions
- Coating compatibility and oxidation-protection evidence, since refractory alloys usually need environmental protection
- Demonstration in representative turbine components, combustor/rig tests, or aerospace/power-industry qualification programs
- Adoption of the Utah design workflow by national labs or manufacturers for real alloy-development decisions

## Caveats

- Machine-learning alloy screening can look impressive before experimental synthesis, processing, and environmental testing narrow the candidates
- Niobium alloys face oxidation, brittleness, density, joining, coating, and cost barriers
- A better design workflow is not the same as a deployable turbine blade
- The project appears to be early-stage ARPA-E Phase I-style work; no public qualified component has surfaced
- Other national labs, universities, and companies are pursuing the same high-temperature materials problem
