# Synthetic Organic Electrochemistry Center

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a Utah-anchored chemistry center helped turn electrosynthesis into a more usable synthetic-chemistry toolkit, but the industrial transformation is still emerging
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Research center / sustainable chemistry methods / electrochemical synthesis toolkit
**Era:** 2020-present; Utah-led Phase II from 2020-2025
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT; continuing center leadership moved to Rolla, MO

**Utah attribution:** Medium - the NSF Center for Synthetic Organic Electrochemistry was anchored at the University of Utah during its 2020-2025 Phase II award, with Shelley Minteer and Utah collaborators central to the center, but the renewed 2025-2030 center is led by Missouri S&T after Minteer's move.

## What it is

The NSF Center for Synthetic Organic Electrochemistry, or CSOE, is a multi-institution research center built to make electrons practical reagents for organic synthesis. Instead of relying on stoichiometric oxidants, reductants, high heat, pressure, or hazardous reagent packages, the center develops electrochemical reactions, mediators, electrodes, cells, mechanistic tools, and training materials that synthetic chemists can actually use.

The Utah-centered phase began when NSF awarded the University of Utah-led CSOE a five-year Phase II Center for Chemical Innovation grant in 2020. The work joined synthetic organic chemistry, electroanalytical chemistry, surface chemistry, materials science, theory, chemical engineering, and industry-facing translation around a simple bottleneck: electrosynthesis is attractive in principle, but many chemists still lack predictable reactions, standard hardware, and mechanistic intuition.

## Why this might become great work

Most pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, polymers, fine chemicals, and research molecules require oxidation or reduction somewhere in their synthesis. When those steps use large chemical reagents, the cost shows up as waste, safety risk, purification burden, and scale-up difficulty.

If CSOE's methods, cells, and training help make electrochemical synthesis routine, then part of chemical manufacturing can become safer, more selective, and less wasteful. The counterfactual is not one miracle reaction; it is a slower adoption curve for electrified chemistry across academic labs and industrial process development.

## Signals so far

- NSF announced the Center for Synthetic Organic Electrochemistry as a 2020 Center for Chemical Innovation anchored at the University of Utah, with nine research partners and relevance to academia, pharmaceuticals, personal-care products, and fine-chemical manufacturing
- The University of Utah reported the Phase II award as a $20 million, five-year center grant to improve the sustainability of synthetic chemistry
- The center's stated mission is to make synthetic organic electrochemistry mainstream through new green, safe, economic reactions, demystified electrochemical reactivity, industry partnerships, training, and outreach
- The CSOE publication list includes a continuing stream of electrosynthesis methods and mechanistic papers, including alternating-current dehalogenation, electrochemical oxalate chemistry, reductive arylation, electro-oxidative deoxyfluorination, catalyst-discovery workflows, and peptide decarboxylation mechanisms
- Peer-reviewed CSOE-affiliated work has shown practical reaction patterns such as electroreductive olefin-ketone coupling that is scalable, chemoselective, and tolerant of air and water
- NSF's current Centers for Chemical Innovation list still includes CSOE as a Phase II center, now led by Missouri S&T, indicating the center survived beyond the Utah-led phase

## What would upgrade this

- Broad independent adoption of CSOE-developed cells, reaction standards, training modules, or protocols in synthetic labs and process-chemistry groups
- Industrial process examples where CSOE-derived electrosynthesis replaces hazardous or waste-heavy redox chemistry at pilot or manufacturing scale
- Citation and methods evidence showing CSOE reactions became standard tools rather than isolated high-profile papers
- Open or commercial hardware that measurably lowers the friction of preparative electrochemistry for non-specialist organic chemists
- Life-cycle, waste, safety, or cost comparisons for real chemical products showing a practical advantage over conventional redox routes

## Caveats

- Synthetic organic electrochemistry is an international revival, not a Utah invention; Utah's claim is the CSOE institutional push, specific methods, and training infrastructure
- Many electrosynthetic reactions look promising in papers but fail to become routine because of substrate scope, electrode fouling, mass transport, current efficiency, cell design, or process economics
- The renewed NSF center is now led by Missouri S&T, so the Utah attribution is strongest for the 2020-2025 Phase II center period
- Industrial adoption in chemistry is often quiet and slow; the public trail currently shows strong research activity more clearly than field-changing manufacturing deployment
