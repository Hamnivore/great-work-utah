# Synthetic Spider Silk Biomanufacturing

**Speculative tier:** P-B - a visible Utah materials-biology program with real technical advances, but commercial-scale adoption remains unproven
**Domain:** Physics & Materials
**Type:** Biomaterials research / synthetic biology / manufacturing process
**Era:** 2011-present in Utah
**Location:** Utah State University, Logan, UT

## What it is

Randy Lewis's Utah State University lab develops ways to make recombinant spider-silk proteins without farming spiders. The program uses transgenic goats, bacteria, alfalfa, and silkworms as biological production systems, then processes the proteins into fibers, films, gels, coatings, sponges, adhesives, and other material forms.

The Utah-era work built on Lewis's earlier spider-silk career, but USU became the home for several production and processing advances: a USTAR bioproducts scale-up facility, aqueous solvation work that replaced harsh organic-solvent assumptions, and CRISPR-assisted silkworm work aimed at more stable spider-protein inheritance and stronger fibers.

## Why this might become great work

Spider silk is attractive because it combines light weight, strength, elasticity, and biocompatibility. A scalable way to manufacture designed spider-silk proteins could matter in medical implants, ligament and tendon repair, coatings, adhesives, high-performance textiles, parachutes, airbags, vehicle parts, and body armor.

The hard part is not imagining uses. It is producing enough consistent, affordable protein and spinning or forming it into useful materials without destroying the properties that made spider silk interesting in the first place. If the Utah program's biological hosts and water-based processing methods become part of a real manufacturing route, the work could become an important biomaterials platform.

## Signals so far

- USU recruited Randy Lewis as a USTAR Professor of Biology in 2011
- USU reports that the Lewis Lab has led recombinant spider-silk protein work using transgenic goats, bacteria, alfalfa, and silkworms
- In 2015, USU researchers reported an aqueous solvation method that enabled full recovery of synthetic spider-silk protein without relying on costly and hazardous organic solvents
- The same USU team reported forming fibers, gels, coatings, absorbent sponges, adhesives, and transparent films from the proteins
- In 2019, USU researchers reported CRISPR-assisted transgenic silkworm work intended to improve spider-protein inheritance and fiber strength
- USU notes that Lewis's broader research record includes more than 140 papers, 16 book chapters, nine issued patents, and more than $33 million in grants

## What would upgrade this

- Commercial production of spider-silk proteins or fibers at meaningful volume with public cost and quality data
- Medical-device, textile, aerospace, defense, or industrial customers using the material in real products
- Peer-reviewed comparisons showing performance, durability, biocompatibility, and manufacturability versus aramids, UHMWPE, collagen, nylon, silk, and other incumbent materials
- FDA-cleared or otherwise regulated medical uses, especially in implants, sutures, coatings, or tissue repair
- Evidence that the Utah-developed processing steps, hosts, or scale-up methods are necessary to adoption rather than just one research path among many

## Caveats

- The global synthetic-spider-silk field includes major non-Utah contributors and companies; the first recombinant cloning and many silkworm advances are not solely Utah stories
- "Spider silk" has attracted decades of overpromising because the material properties are spectacular but manufacturing is stubborn
- The Utah-era work is best framed as a strong platform and process contribution, not yet a transformed materials industry
- Transgenic animal and insect production raises regulatory, containment, animal-welfare, and public-acceptance questions
