## The tier convention

Each entry carries an explicit `**Tier:**` line:

- **S** — category-defining; arguably the best place in the world to do this work. ⭐ marker on filename and H1.
- **A** — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent. If you're already in Utah and want this kind of work, these are great places.
- **B** — real, with significant caveats. Outclassed, mid-tier, commoditizing, or very early. Worth knowing about; honest about what's compromised.
- **C** — plausible. Some signal, not enough to be confident. Read before applying.
- **D** — weak but in. Mission integrity questionable, or dominated by larger players. Real organizations, but the world-changing claim is weak or contested.
- **F** — "why is this in the wiki?" tier. Minimal one- or two-line entry. Placeholder for things spotted in research that might be worth revisiting when more is known.

The tier system is the visibility control — not exclusion. The wiki includes generously; the tier helps you decide where to spend your reading time. Most S/A entries have a "Why this is not ⭐" section explaining what would have to change for the tier to move up.

## Inclusion bar

The future is not obvious. Ford was dismissed for betting on combustion engines. The Wright brothers were ridiculed before they flew. It is difficult — maybe impossible — to say with confidence that a given company *won't* change the world. Because of that, the wiki is inclusive rather than selective. Companies that seem mediocre, execution-focused, or mid-tier are included *and* their tier is named explicitly inside the entry, rather than excluded. F-tier entries are minimal stubs for things spotted in research that haven't been validated enough for a full write-up.

The S/A/B/C/D/F scheme makes it easy to scan for the handful of category-defining options while still surfacing the broader landscape — genuinely good places to work that readers can weigh on their own terms.
