# Cosm

**Tier:** B — real technological depth, genuine novelty in shared-reality venue design, but the world-changing claim depends on whether immersive dome experiences become a durable medium or an upscale novelty
**Domain:** Art & meaning — shared reality / immersive entertainment venues
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Well-funded growth (raised $250M in 2024 from Steve Wynn, Marc Lasry, Dan Gilbert, others; total funding undisclosed)
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT (E&S / Digistar division); venues in Los Angeles, Dallas, Atlanta (2026), global expansion underway
**Website:** [cosm.com](https://cosm.com) / [tech.cosm.com](https://tech.cosm.com)

## Mission

Build a global network of shared-reality venues — large-format LED dome environments that let audiences watch live sports, concerts, and original content together in full immersion, without putting on a headset.

## Why it matters

VR promised immersive experiences and mostly retreated to solo headset use. Cosm is betting on the inverse: a physically present, socially shared version of immersion where the dome itself is the display and the audience watches together. The technology stack is serious — nearly 100 NVIDIA GPUs per full venue, Digistar 7 (the world's most deployed planetarium system, with 70+ years of dome-rendering heritage from Evans & Sutherland), and Unreal Engine integration for live rendering. The E&S acquisition in 2020 wasn't branding; it brought one of the oldest and deepest dome-display engineering lineages in the world. Cosm has cut venue setup time from a year to four months by standardizing the CX System platform, and it's targeting up to 100 venues globally.

## The hard problem

Convincing people to leave home for a premium venue experience is hard enough. Doing it repeatedly — not just for novelty — requires content, comfort, and a social contract around shared viewing that most entertainment venues haven't solved. On the engineering side, rendering live sports at dome scale with minimal latency and color-accurate 8K-ish imagery across a curved LED surface is genuinely non-trivial. The Digistar 7 lineage gives Cosm an unusual head start.

## Mechanism of impact

Physical deployment of novel venues. If the format works, it becomes a new category of entertainment infrastructure — like IMAX, but social and real-time. The stakes are whether audiences will pay a premium to watch live sports or concerts in a dome rather than at home, and whether that premium sustains long enough for the content ecosystem to form around it.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

- Display engineers, real-time rendering engineers (Unreal Engine, GPU cluster management, LED calibration)
- Venue systems engineers — the integration of 100 GPUs running simultaneously is a serious distributed-systems problem
- Content producers and creative technologists who want to make things for a 360-degree canvas
- Software engineers interested in building the CX System platform, which ships with every new venue
- Anyone interested in the E&S / Digistar / Spitz side: planetarium software and science education still run on this infrastructure

## Ownership

Private. CEO: Jeb Terry. Notable investors: Steve Wynn, Marc Lasry, Dan Gilbert. Previously acquired Evans & Sutherland (2020, $14.5M) and Spitz Inc. (2020), absorbing their combined 70+ years of dome engineering, Digistar software, and institutional customer relationships.

## Caveats

- The business model depends on premium venue attendance, which has historically been difficult to sustain outside of IMAX-scale content pipelines
- Cosm venues cost significant capital to build and run; 100 GPUs per venue is infrastructure, not SaaS margins
- "Shared reality" as a category has not yet proven it creates habitual return visits the way movie theaters did at their peak
- The E&S acquisition was a bargain buy ($14.5M for a 70-year-old simulation company) — the tech is deep, but planetarium and simulation software are legacy markets
- Global expansion from 4 to 100 venues is an ambitious logistics and operations problem, not just a technology one
- The world-changing case is weak if this remains a premium sports-bar-adjacent category rather than becoming the default medium for shared live experience
