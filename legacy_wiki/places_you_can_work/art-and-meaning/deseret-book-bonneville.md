# Deseret Book / Bonneville International

**Tier:** B — real with significant caveats; outclassed, mid-tier, or commoditizing
**Domain:** Art & meaning — Religious publishing, broadcasting, and media
**Also relevant to:** [Other](../other/) (LDS Church institutional infrastructure)
**Type:** Private media conglomerate, wholly owned by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
**Stage:** Established (Deseret Book founded 1866; Bonneville founded 1964)
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Website:** [deseretbook.com](https://deseretbook.com), [bonneville.com](https://www.bonneville.com)

## Mission

Publish, distribute, and broadcast media aligned with the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints' values — books, music, radio, television, and digital content — both for the ~17 million members of the LDS Church globally and for a broader audience interested in faith-centered content.

## Why it matters

This is a wiki about meaning-making, not just about frontier-tech startups. Any honest account of Utah's art-and-meaning infrastructure has to include the LDS Church's media operations, because at scale they are the largest cultural-production institutions in the state and among the most durable religious media organizations in the US.

- **Deseret Book** is the country's largest dedicated religious-book publisher, with decades of Shadow Mountain titles (Mormon literary fiction, devotional, children's), music imprints, and a retail chain of ~40 stores.
- **Bonneville International** operates ~20 radio stations across major US markets (Seattle, San Francisco, Phoenix, Denver, Salt Lake City) plus KSL-TV (NBC affiliate, Salt Lake City) and the KSL news operation — which is, by Utah standards, a significant regional news organization with genuine investigative work.
- **Deseret Management Corporation** is the umbrella that owns these plus the Deseret News (state's oldest paper, founded 1850).

The interesting question for the wiki: does an institution that produces art and meaning *for a specific faith tradition* count as world-changing? The honest answer is yes — religious institutions are among the most persistent meaning-making systems humans have built, and the LDS Church's media apparatus has been a major engine of a specific kind of literature, music, and public discourse for over 150 years. It is not "frontier" in the startup sense, but it shapes the cultural water that millions of people swim in, and has for generations.

## The hard problem

Producing media at the intersection of religious commitment and contemporary literary/journalistic standards is genuinely difficult. Shadow Mountain has published books that reached broad secular audiences (Brandon Sanderson early career, Jason F. Wright); KSL has won regional journalism awards on investigations including sexual abuse in polygamist communities. Holding "aligned with Church values" and "genuinely good" in the same brief is harder than either alone.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct via audience: millions of LDS members globally and the broader market for faith-centered media. Second-order: the cultural imagination of Utah and the LDS diaspora is substantially shaped by what these institutions publish, broadcast, and amplify. Third-order: KSL-TV and the Deseret News are the local news baseline for much of Utah, meaning what gets reported as "news" in the state is largely filtered through Church-owned media.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Editors, journalists, radio producers, TV producers, book designers, music producers, and digital content strategists — all aligned (explicitly or in practice) with the Church's values. Internal politics matter; working here without being a member is possible but rare. Good fit for Latter-day Saint creatives who want to do serious work at a major media institution without leaving Utah.

## Ownership

Wholly owned by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints through Deseret Management Corporation. Mission autonomy from external shareholders is complete; mission autonomy from the Church's hierarchy is, by design, not something the Church would want.

## Why this is not ⭐

Serving a specific faith community is a meaningful mission but makes "world-changing" a different kind of claim than what the wiki usually rewards. The institutions are durable, large, and consequential within their audience, but they are not the frontier of creative-medium innovation. Included in the wiki under the revised inclusion bar because cultural and meaning-making infrastructure is part of how worlds change, even when it's not packaged as a Silicon Valley startup.

## Caveats

- Wholly Church-owned — editorial latitude is bounded in ways that secular media is not
- Mission alignment ("aligned with the Church's values") is an explicit hiring consideration
- Radio and newspaper economics are declining industries broadly; Bonneville and Deseret News face the same headwinds as all legacy media
- KSL's mix of serious journalism and Church-sympathetic framing is a known-to-insiders tension
- Not a place to work if you're uncomfortable with faith-integrated institutional culture
