# 3Helix

**Tier:** B — validated by BASF investment, multiple application areas, but cosmetics-first is a limited entry point
**Domain:** Biological engineering — collagen hybridizing peptides
**Also relevant to:** [Health & longevity](../health-and-longevity/)
**Type:** Company (private)

**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT (U of U Biomedical Engineering spinout)
**Website:** [3helix.com](https://www.3helix.com)

## Mission

Use Collagen Hybridizing Peptides (CHP) — short single-stranded peptides that bind specifically to damaged/denatured collagen — as a platform for diagnostics, therapeutics, and personal care across any disease involving collagen degradation.

## Why it matters

Damaged collagen is the structural signature of injury and disease: heart attack (collagen breakdown in infarct tissue), cancer invasion (tumor microenvironment collagen remodeling), arthritis, fibrosis, wound healing, and aging. CHP is the only known molecular tool that binds *specifically* to denatured collagen triple-helix sites, not intact collagen — which means it can target disease tissue without hitting normal tissue. That's a targeting mechanism with diagnostic (imaging damaged tissue), therapeutic (drug delivery to lesion), and cosmetic (anti-aging in skin) applications. BASF's equity investment and exclusive license for the personal care market validates the commercial viability; the therapeutic pipeline is potentially more impactful.

## The hard problem

Designing short peptides that hybridize specifically to denatured collagen (not native triple-helix) required understanding the thermodynamics of collagen assembly at atomic resolution — a decade of structural biochemistry work at U of U by Dr. Michael Yu and Dr. Yang Li. Translating this into clinical diagnostics and therapeutics requires pharmacokinetic optimization, biodistribution control, and payload attachment chemistry.

## Mechanism of impact

Dual tracks: (1) platform licensing — BASF commercializes CHP in personal care; other pharma companies license for diagnostics/therapeutics; and (2) internal drug development — if 3Helix pursues CHP-based targeted drug delivery or imaging agents directly.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Biochemists, materials scientists, drug delivery engineers. People interested in translational research from molecular biomechanics to clinical applications. The BASF partnership means the company has stable near-term revenue while pursuing the larger therapeutic opportunity.

## Ownership

Private. Founders: Dr. Michael Yu and Dr. Yang Li (U of U BME, co-founders). BASF equity investment + exclusive license for personal care (2023). Utah Innovation Fund investment.

## Caveats

- BASF's exclusive license is for personal care only — BASF commercializing CHP as an anti-aging cosmetic ingredient is a very different trajectory than the cancer/heart disease vision
- Therapeutic CHP applications require clinical trials; no IND filed as of known information
- The diagnostic imaging application is scientifically exciting but MRI/PET contrast agent development is slow and expensive
- Founded 2015, still pre-clinical for therapeutic applications after 10 years — suggests the translation timeline is longer than originally projected

## Why this is not ⭐

The technology is genuinely distinctive (no competing chemistry for damaged-collagen-specific targeting), but the commercial entry point (cosmetics) doesn't reflect the maximum impact potential, and the therapeutic program is moving slowly. A star-tier entry would need clinical validation for a disease indication.
