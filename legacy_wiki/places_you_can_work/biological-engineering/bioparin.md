## Mission

Produce heparin — the most-used anticoagulant drug in the world — synthetically in cell culture rather than extracting it from pig intestines.

## Why it matters

Heparin is used in roughly 12 million patients per year globally. Every dialysis session, most cardiac surgeries, many ICU stays, and most DVT/PE treatments use heparin or a heparin derivative. The supply chain is one of the most fragile in all of medicine: approximately 80% of the world's heparin is extracted from the small intestines of Chinese-raised pigs. In 2008, contaminated heparin from Chinese suppliers killed at least 81 people in the US and caused thousands of adverse reactions. Swine disease outbreaks (African swine fever, most recently) periodically threaten global supply.

A synthetic alternative — heparin produced by engineered cells or via cell-free enzymatic synthesis — would eliminate the pig-dependency, remove contamination risk, enable precise control of the heparin fragment mix (not possible with animal-sourced product), and in principle scale with demand. Academic groups have been working toward this for two decades. Bioparin is one of the handful of companies trying to commercialize.

This is a high-difficulty, high-value target. If it works, it addresses a real global health vulnerability that has already killed people when the existing supply chain failed. If it does not work, it joins a long list of ambitious synthetic-biology programs that could not hit price parity with animal extraction.

## The hard problem

Heparin is not a single molecule — it is a polysaccharide mixture with a specific sulfation pattern that determines anticoagulant activity. Producing it synthetically requires either:
1. **Cell-based biosynthesis**: engineering cells to express the full biosynthetic enzyme cascade and produce correctly sulfated heparin. Hard because the enzymes are not all well-characterized and the product mix must match the clinical specification.
2. **Cell-free enzymatic synthesis**: running the enzymatic reactions outside cells, with purified enzymes and starting substrates. More controllable but expensive to scale.

Either way, the product has to clear FDA as equivalent-or-better to USP heparin, which is a significant regulatory bar, and hit a price point that can compete with $/kg animal extraction.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment via drug substitution. If Bioparin reaches FDA approval at a viable price, every heparin-treated patient is a potential impact footprint. Second-order: de-risks the heparin supply chain for all subsequent global health crises.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Synthetic biologists, carbohydrate chemists, process engineers with biomanufacturing scale-up experience, regulatory affairs specialists with biologic/drug experience. Very early stage; roles are broad and leverage is high.

## Ownership

Private, University of Utah spinout. IP licensed from U of U. STTR funding (early federal non-dilutive). No major VC round identified publicly. Ownership is founder/university/SBIR-government.

## Why this is not ⭐

Very early. The synthetic-heparin goal has been pursued by multiple academic groups and companies for 20+ years without a commercial product reaching market. Bioparin needs to demonstrate scalable production, match the clinical heparin specification, and reach FDA approval before earning S-tier. The target is worth aiming at; success is unproven.

## Caveats

- Pre-clinical; no commercial product yet
- Competing with Chinese animal-extraction at $/kg is a very hard economic target
- Multiple prior academic programs on synthetic heparin have not reached market
- FDA path for a biosimilar to USP heparin is regulatorily complex
- Small team; single-point-of-failure risk on academic founders
