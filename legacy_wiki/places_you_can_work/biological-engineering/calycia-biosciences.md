## Mission

Use engineered macrophages as CAR cell therapy for glioblastoma (GBM) and other solid tumors where CAR-T has failed.

## Why it matters

CAR-T therapy has transformed liquid cancers (leukemia, lymphoma) but mostly failed at solid tumors. Two reasons: (1) T cells can't penetrate solid tumor microenvironments, and (2) solid tumors actively suppress T cell function. Macrophages are different — they're natural infiltrators of solid tumors and key modulators of the tumor immune microenvironment. If engineered macrophages can be directed at GBM (median survival 15 months, no major therapeutic advances in decades), the impact is enormous.

## The hard problem

Engineering macrophages for CAR therapy is technically harder than T cells — macrophages are harder to transfect and shorter-lived ex vivo. The Roh-Johnson lab's specific insight on macrophage trafficking and solid tumor penetration needs to be validated in clinical setting. No macrophage CAR therapy has reached clinical approval as of 2025.

## Ownership

U of U Altitude Lab spinout. Cumming Foundation seed investment. Very early; founder/lab-controlled at this stage.

## Caveats

- Preclinical only — no IND filed as of 2025
- GBM is a notoriously difficult tumor; many promising therapies have failed in clinical trials
- Small team, early funding: execution risk is very high
- The macrophage CAR field is nascent; if it works here, competition will accelerate fast
