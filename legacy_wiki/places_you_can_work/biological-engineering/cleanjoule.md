# CleanJoule

**Also relevant to:** [Transportation](../../transportation/), [Energy](../../energy/), [Space](../../space/)
**Type:** Company (private)

**Founded:** ~2020
**Website:** [cleanjoule.com](https://cleanjoule.com)

## Mission

Eliminate aerospace's dependence on fossil fuels by producing biomanufactured fuels that outperform petroleum — not just match it.

## Why it matters

Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) is one of the hardest decarbonization problems: aviation can't easily electrify, and most SAF today is either too expensive or requires blending with conventional jet fuel. CleanJoule's CycloSAF has 10% higher energy density than standard Jet-A, which means it doesn't just substitute for petroleum — it's thermodynamically better. Airlines can carry more payload or fly farther on the same weight of fuel. That flips the usual economics of sustainable fuel: instead of asking operators to accept a penalty for going green, CleanJoule offers a performance benefit.

The SpaceSAF product extends this logic to rocket propellant: a drop-in replacement for RP-1 (the kerosene used in SpaceX's Merlin engines, among others) with 4%+ better energy density. In rocket science, 4% more energy per kilogram of propellant is significant — it means more payload to orbit, or the same payload on a smaller rocket. And it means sustainable space launch is achievable without redesigning existing rocket engines.

## The hard problem

Getting higher energy density from a biological feedstock than from refined petroleum requires solving the fermentation pathway and the downstream catalytic conversion simultaneously. Most biofuels sacrifice energy density to gain renewability. CleanJoule's feedstock-flexible process (they're not locked to one input) is their differentiator, and ASTM certification for CycloSAF — the hardest gate for aviation fuel approval — is underway as of late 2024.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct displacement: their fuel drops into existing aircraft and rockets without modification. If they reach cost parity (or better) at scale, there's no adoption barrier — operators switch for performance reasons, not sustainability reasons. That's a fundamentally different market entry than most climate tech.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Biochemical engineers, process engineers, and catalytic chemists who want to see their work go into actual flight hardware. The DoD partnership suggests classified as well as commercial pathways. Building their first full-scale US manufacturing plant in 2025 — this is the transition from process development to industrial scale.

## Ownership

Private. Backers include airline operators (Frontier, Wizz, Volaris) as strategic investors — unusual and positive, since it aligns customer incentives with the company's success. Indigo Partners (major airline ownership group) is lead investor. No evidence of outside-majority control that would conflict with the mission.

## Caveats

- ASTM certification for CycloSAF is still in progress — the most critical near-term gate
- Scaling fermentation and catalysis simultaneously from lab to industrial is notoriously hard; cost parity at scale is unproven
- SpaceSAF is announced but no public data on certification progress or launch customers
- Biofuel companies have a checkered history of promising economics that don't survive scale-up
- The DoD partnership is a positive signal but also creates dependency on government contracts
