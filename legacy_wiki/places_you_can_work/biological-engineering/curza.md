**Domain:** Biological engineering — novel antibiotics for drug-resistant Gram-negative bacteria
**Also relevant to:** [Health & longevity](../health-and-longevity/)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Late preclinical → IND preparation (Phase 1); $40M+ raised (NIH, DTRA, CARB-X, Novo Holdings REPAIR Fund)
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT (U of U College of Science spinout)
**Website:** [curza.com](https://curza.com)

## Mission

Develop the first new class of antibiotics for drug-resistant Gram-negative bacteria in nearly 50 years, using a novel binding site on the bacterial ribosome that current antibiotics don't touch.

## Why it matters

Antibiotic-resistant Gram-negative bacteria (E. coli, Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas — the "ESKAPE" pathogens) kill an estimated 700,000 people per year globally, a number projected to reach 10 million by 2050 if untreated. The antibiotic pipeline is nearly dry for this class: no new mechanism against Gram-negative bacteria has been clinically approved in ~50 years. The economics of antibiotic development (low margins, fast resistance, long development timelines) have driven pharma companies to exit the space, creating a massive gap between public need and commercial incentive. Curza is filling that gap with grant-based funding from DTRA, CARB-X, NIH, and impact investors — the organizations willing to fund what markets won't.

## The hard problem

Gram-negative bacteria have a double membrane that acts as a physical barrier to many antibiotics effective against Gram-positives. Getting a drug molecule across both membranes into the bacterial cytoplasm is a hard pharmacokinetics problem. Curza's CZ-02 compound targets a clinically un-drugged and highly conserved site on the bacterial ribosome — meaning bacteria can't easily develop resistance by mutating this site without disrupting core ribosome function. The binding site specificity across Gram-negative species without cross-reacting with human ribosomes is the key design challenge.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment: a new antibiotic approved for drug-resistant Gram-negative infections would save hundreds of thousands of lives per year immediately, and prevent the looming mortality surge from antimicrobial resistance. The Defense Threat Reduction Agency's involvement ($75M award with University of Florida partners) signals that drug-resistant bacteria are also a biodefense priority.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Medicinal chemists, microbiologists, biochemists focused on structural biology. Anyone motivated by the global public health / biodefense crisis of antimicrobial resistance and willing to work in a grant-heavy, mission-driven development environment. Not a typical VC-backed pharma startup — the funding model is different (government grants + impact funds), and the company is genuinely mission-aligned.

## Ownership

Private. U of U spinout; original chemistry licensed from U of U. CEO: Chad Testa. Funded primarily by government grants (NIH, DTRA) and impact investors (CARB-X, Novo Holdings REPAIR Fund). ~$40M total raised. This ownership structure aligns mission with revenue — there's no pressure to sell to a pharma company that will shelve the drug.

## Caveats

- Late preclinical — Phase 1 IND not yet submitted; attrition between preclinical and clinical is high for antibiotics
- The economics of antibiotic commercialization remain broken even after successful Phase 3 — new reimbursement models (subscription/subscription-like payments from governments) are not yet universally adopted
- $75M DTRA + UFL collaboration is large but has a 9-year timeline — impact is not imminent
- CARB-X award potential ($4M) is modest relative to full development costs

## Why this is not ⭐

The mechanism is genuinely first-in-class and the funder validation is strong, but no data in human patients yet. A star-tier entry would need clinical efficacy data showing the CZ-02 compound works in patients with resistant Gram-negative infections. If Phase 1 confirms safety and Phase 2 confirms efficacy, the tier should move up significantly.
