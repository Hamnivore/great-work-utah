## Mission

Build a new class of peptide drugs by using enzymatic modification to make peptides that would otherwise be too unstable or too permeable-to-degradation into clinically usable therapeutics.

## Why it matters

Peptides are a middle-ground between small molecules and large biologics — specific enough to target protein-protein interactions that small molecules cannot hit, small enough to potentially be delivered orally or to cross cell membranes. The reason the industry hasn't fully embraced peptides is stability: unprotected peptides are rapidly degraded by proteases in the blood and gut. Existing solutions (cyclization, N-methylation, D-amino acid substitution) are case-by-case and have limitations.

Sethera's approach uses enzymatic modification — installing stabilizing chemical groups using engineered enzymes — to create peptides that resist degradation while preserving the binding specificity that made peptides attractive in the first place. If it works broadly, it opens up drug targets (many intracellular protein-protein interactions) that are currently considered undruggable. GLP-1 agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy) are themselves stabilized peptides and are now the biggest drug category by revenue in the world; that's the size of the prize if peptides become broadly drug-like.

## The hard problem

Making the enzymatic stabilization approach work across many different peptide sequences (rather than just the one used to demonstrate proof of concept) is the core technical bet. Each new peptide target typically requires:
- Demonstrated in-vivo stability after modification
- Preserved binding affinity to the target
- Acceptable pharmacokinetics (distribution, half-life, clearance)
- No new immunogenicity introduced by the modification
- Scalable synthesis at GMP quality

The first peptide drug is the hard one; if the platform generalizes, subsequent candidates are faster. Most platform biotech companies succeed or fail on this generalization question.

## Mechanism of impact

Potentially large if the platform generalizes — every previously-undruggable intracellular target opened by stabilized peptides is a potential medicine. Direct via therapeutic deployment at scale over time. Platform plays typically take 8–15 years from founding to first approved drug.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Peptide chemists, enzymologists, medicinal chemists, in-vivo pharmacologists, regulatory affairs. Very early stage academic spinout; leverage and roles are broad. Good fit for someone who wants to build a drug-discovery platform from the ground floor in the Mountain West.

## Ownership

Private, University of Utah spinout. IP licensed from U of U. Stage-appropriate early funding.

## Why this is not ⭐

Too early. No clinical candidate yet, no IND. Platform biotech plays are particularly long-dated, and the success rate from "enzymatically-modified peptides work in a paper" to "an approved drug" is low. S-tier would require either a clinical candidate in an IND-enabling phase or evidence the platform generalizes across multiple targets.

## Caveats

- Very early stage; no clinical program yet
- Platform biotech is a long, expensive path; typical timeline 8–15 years to first approval
- Competes with other peptide-stabilization approaches (macrocyclization companies, ADCs, stapled peptides)
- Small team; academic spinout single-point-of-failure risk
