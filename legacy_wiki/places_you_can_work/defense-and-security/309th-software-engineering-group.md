# 309th Software Engineering Group (309th SWEG)

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Defense & security — Flight software sustainment for US Air Force platforms
**Also relevant to:** [Machine intelligence](../machine-intelligence/) (DevSecOps / classified software engineering)
**Type:** US Air Force organization (Hill Air Force Base, Ogden Air Logistics Complex)
**Stage:** Established — one of the largest defense software orgs in the US
**HQ:** Hill AFB, Ogden, UT
**Website:** [hill.af.mil](https://www.hill.af.mil)

## Mission

Write and maintain the flight, weapons, and mission software that keeps most of the US Air Force's combat fleet flying — F-35, F-22, F-16, A-10, ICBMs, cruise missiles, and the emerging B-21 sustainment pipeline.

## Why it matters

When most people think about software engineering at the frontier, they think Bay Area AI labs. But the 309th is the organization that keeps the US air superiority fleet operational — code that is on actual flying jets, in actual combat, with lives directly downstream of it. Roughly 2,000+ engineers. This is one of the few government-run organic software depots that has kept pace with industry on DevSecOps practices: they run the Kessel Run-style agile model, use container-based secure development, and push software updates to fielded aircraft on modern cadences rather than the old multi-year sustainment cycle.

Why it's on the frontier: the US is in a race with China to modernize defense software. The B-21 Raider, CCA (Collaborative Combat Aircraft, the loyal-wingman autonomous drones), and next-generation ICBM (Sentinel) all depend on software-first architecture. The 309th is where a lot of that sustainment responsibility lands. Also, F-35 software modernization (Block 4 / TR-3) is one of the biggest software programs in the DoD; the 309th owns significant pieces of it.

For a software engineer who wants to do impactful defense work without relocating to the Beltway, and who can pass the clearance process, the 309th is one of the most interesting places in the country. The pay is government-scale (not startup), but the mission weight and the seriousness of the codebases are unmatched outside a small number of defense primes.

## The hard problem

Safety-critical, real-time, classified software running on hardware that can kill people on both sides of the fight if it goes wrong. Legacy codebases in Ada, assembly, and C that have to interoperate with modernized C++/Rust/Python components. Running agile and DevSecOps on classified networks where you cannot pip-install, cannot pull from GitHub, and every change is audited. Cybersecurity at nation-state adversary threat level. And, at the same time, keeping 50-year-old airframes (A-10, B-52) flying while also standing up software for aircraft that don't exist yet (B-21, CCA).

## Mechanism of impact

Direct: every sortie flown by a 309th-sustained aircraft is enabled by this code. Deterrent: credible US air power reduces the probability of certain kinds of conflict. Also, the 309th has been a talent pipeline into defense tech startups — Anduril, Shield AI, and others recruit heavily from this pool, so the second-order effect on the broader defense-software ecosystem is real.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Software engineers (systems, embedded, avionics, cybersecurity, DevSecOps) willing to get a Secret or Top Secret clearance and work on classified programs. Former military is common but not required. Good fit for someone who wants their code on real combat aircraft, is comfortable with government process, and prioritizes mission weight over compensation and optionality. Also a strong fit for software engineers who want to live in Utah and don't want to commute to a Bay Area salary but also don't want to write CRUD apps.

## Ownership

US Air Force — part of the Ogden Air Logistics Complex at Hill AFB. Government civilian workforce (GS/DR-scale) supplemented by contractors (SAIC, Leidos, Northrop, etc.). Not a company; mission autonomy is institutional and mission integrity is definitionally aligned with US Air Force operational needs.

## Why this is not ⭐

Government organization, not a company. The ⭐ convention is being applied primarily to companies and independent orgs. The 309th is civilizationally important and a genuinely great engineering job for the right person, but it isn't the wiki's company-first object. A reader should absolutely consider working here if the fit is right.

## Caveats

- Clearance requirement is an absolute gate (no prior drug use beyond narrow windows, financial issues flagged, foreign contact review, etc.)
- Government pay scale — typically below private-sector software, compensation ceiling at GS-15/DR-IV
- Classified work cannot be discussed outside the program; public portfolio-building is impossible
- Institutional pace — process is heavy even on DevSecOps programs
- Mission ethics: this is weapons work. Anyone considering this path should think clearly about the ethics of working on combat aircraft software
