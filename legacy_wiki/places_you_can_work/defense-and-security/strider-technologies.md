# Strider Technologies

**Tier:** A — occupies a largely uncontested niche in a genuinely serious problem space; strong traction
**Domain:** Defense & security — AI-driven counterintelligence / state-sponsored threat detection
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Series C ($55M); $117M total raised
**HQ:** South Jordan, UT
**Founded:** ~2019
**Website:** [striderintel.com](https://www.striderintel.com)

## Mission

Turn publicly available global data into actionable counterintelligence that helps governments, universities, and corporations identify state-sponsored IP theft, talent recruitment, and supply chain infiltration before it happens.

## Why it matters

State-sponsored economic espionage — primarily by China but also Russia, Iran, and others — is estimated to cost the US hundreds of billions in IP annually. The mechanism is subtle: talent recruitment through academic partnerships, joint ventures that extract proprietary knowledge, and supply chain infiltration. Most organizations have no systematic way to detect this because the signals are in open-source data scattered across millions of documents in dozens of languages.

Strider has indexed 12+ billion publicly available documents from global sources and ingests 20 million new documents daily from 50,000+ data sources. Their platform identifies when an employee is being recruited by a state-affiliated entity, when a supplier has undisclosed ties to adversary military programs, or when research collaborations are providing technology transfer to restricted parties. The FBI has warned about exactly these vectors; Strider is building the defensive infrastructure.

## The hard problem

Open-source intelligence (OSINT) on state-sponsored activity requires cross-referencing information across languages, organizations, and time in ways that no human analyst team can do at scale. The hard part is not collecting the data — it is linking entities across documents, resolving aliases, and generating actionable risk assessments that are low false-positive enough to act on. Building this for 50,000 data sources in real time, with sufficient precision to avoid damaging false accusations, is a serious NLP and entity-resolution problem.

## Mechanism of impact

Defensive intelligence layer: Strider sells to corporations protecting R&D, universities managing research security compliance (required under federal grant rules), government agencies, and critical infrastructure operators. If Strider's signals become standard practice in technology licensing and research partnership review, the cost to state-sponsored actors of covert technology transfer rises substantially.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Data scientists, NLP engineers, knowledge graph engineers, and national security analysts. Also people with foreign language skills and area expertise. The organization has offices in SLC, Washington DC, Tokyo, and London — signaling a hybrid intelligence/technology culture uncommon in a Utah startup.

## Ownership

Private. $117M total raised ($55M Series C led by Pelion Venture Partners, with AXA Venture Partners, Valor Equity Partners, DataTribe, Cyfr Capital). Pelion-led, so VC-majority is likely. DataTribe is a cyber-intelligence-specialist investor (CIA venture arm connection) — meaningful signal for the national security legitimacy of the mission. Utah has formally partnered with Strider.

## Caveats / red flags

- Counterintelligence is a dual-use domain — the same platform that protects against foreign adversaries could be repurposed for corporate surveillance or domestic intelligence; mission integrity depends on customer scope and organizational guardrails
- Open-source intelligence has coverage gaps — state-sponsored actors who operate through non-documented channels or offline will not appear in Strider's dataset
- VC backing creates pressure toward enterprise SaaS revenue expansion, which may dilute focus on the harder national-security use cases
- The precision of state-sponsored threat attribution is genuinely uncertain; false positives could have serious reputational consequences for individuals or organizations flagged
