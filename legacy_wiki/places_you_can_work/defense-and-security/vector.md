# Vector

**Tier:** A — serious SOF customer, validated capability, genuine model innovation; very early (founded 2024)
**Domain:** Defense & security — Attritable drone systems / modern warfare as a service
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Series A; $61M raised in ~14 months; ~150 employees
**HQ:** Bluffdale, UT
**Founded:** 2024
**Website:** [tfvector.com](https://www.tfvector.com)

## Mission

Equip U.S. and allied special operations forces with drone systems that can be procured, upgraded, and replaced at the speed of modern conflict — not at the speed of traditional defense acquisition.

## Why it matters

Traditional defense procurement takes years. The drone threat environment in modern conflict changes in weeks. Vector's answer is a subscription model — "Modern Warfare as a Service" — where warfighters get continuous hardware and doctrine updates rather than a one-time purchase that ages out. Special Operations Command validated this bet with a multi-year, multi-million-dollar contract, designating Vector a "chosen capability provider for America's most elite warfighters." The Pentagon's Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering awarded Vector its Top Gun Trophy. DIU Blue and Green List approval confirms domestic supply chain compliance and cyber resilience — prerequisites for serious DoD scaling.

## Product line

Three platforms covering outdoor, urban, and indoor mission profiles:

- **Hammer** (10-inch propeller) — long-range outdoor operations; fiber-optic control link immune to RF jamming and GPS denial; detects targets up to 20km; dual-use sensing and strike
- **Mace** (5-inch propeller) — compact design for urban and forested environments
- **3.5-inch indoor drone** — building reconnaissance and close-quarters tactical operations

The fiber-optic Hammer is the technical core: fiber optic communication is impervious to electronic warfare countermeasures that defeat conventional RF-linked drones. In contested electronic environments — which is the norm in peer and near-peer conflict — this matters.

## Business model

Unlike traditional defense primes who deliver a platform and walk away, Vector sells ongoing capability: drones, tactical integration training, doctrine updates, and hardware refreshes, all under subscription. The military gets the newest generation without a new procurement cycle; Vector gets recurring revenue and a live feedback loop from operational use.

## Differentiation

The dominant attritable drone players (Anduril, AeroVironment, Shield AI) mostly focus on ISR, loitering munitions, or autonomous coordination platforms. Vector's fiber-optic FPV for SOF fills a different niche: highly maneuverable, jamming-immune, operator-directed systems for small teams operating in GPS-denied environments. The SOF customer base is also strategically valuable — SOF units are early adopters whose operational feedback shapes doctrine that scales to conventional forces.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Engineers from robotics, aerospace, embedded systems, and EW (electronic warfare) backgrounds. Special operations veterans and defense acquisition professionals who can translate SOF requirements into product. The company is ~150 people and operating at startup pace inside DoD.

## Caveats / red flags

- Founded 2024 — the SOCOM contract and funding traction are impressive for 14 months, but durability and production scaling are unproven
- Attritable drone market is crowded; Anduril, AeroVironment, Shield AI, L3Harris, and dozens of startups are competing for the same DoD budgets
- "Modern Warfare as a Service" subscription model requires DoD contracting flexibility that has historically been hard to sustain across administrations and budget cycles
- Specific contract values are undisclosed; the SOCOM relationship could be a pilot rather than a programmatic win
