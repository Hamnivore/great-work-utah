# Fortem Technologies ⭐

**Tier:** S — category-defining; arguably the best place in the world to do this work
**Domain:** Defense & security — Counter-drone / airspace defense
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Private, growth (100+ employees, battle-tested deployments in Ukraine and the Middle East)
**HQ:** Lindon, UT (51,000 sq ft advanced manufacturing HQ opened June 2025)
**Website:** [fortemtech.com](https://fortemtech.com)

## Mission

Build the radar and autonomous interceptor systems that protect airspace from hostile drones — military, critical infrastructure, and increasingly the power grid.

## Why it matters

Small drones became a strategic weapon category in the 2020s. Ukraine has demonstrated at industrial scale what everyone in defense had been writing papers about: cheap, expendable drones can destroy expensive armored vehicles, contested airspace below 1,000 feet is a new warfighting layer, and traditional air defense (built for jets and missiles) is the wrong tool against swarms of $1,000 quadcopters. The counter-UAS problem is now one of the most active areas in defense, and the U.S. DoD has stood up entire programs around it.

Fortem's differentiator is that they built an actual kinetic interceptor — the DroneHunter — that uses aircraft-grade radar (TrueView) to autonomously find hostile drones and capture them with a Dyneema net (15x stronger than steel). Net-capture is important because it preserves the drone for intelligence exploitation rather than just destroying it. As of January 2026 they began deliveries of DroneHunter 5.0, built for counter-swarm defense. They've done 70,000+ test flights and 5,500+ real captures. In December 2025 they announced a partnership with Southern States for U.S. power grid defense — this is no longer just a war zone product.

The counterfactual argument is strong. Most counter-drone systems are jammers or directed-energy weapons, both of which have serious limitations (electronic warfare spectrum conflicts; line-of-sight, weather, and power constraints). A kinetic net-capture system that works autonomously at scale is not commoditized. If drone swarms become a standard threat vector against U.S. infrastructure — grid substations, data centers, airports, stadiums — the field of companies with actual fielded solutions is small, and Fortem is one of them.

## The hard problem

Counter-swarm defense is the current frontier. A single drone is easy to detect and defeat. A coordinated swarm of 50+ is a radically harder problem: the radar has to track them all, the battle management system has to allocate interceptors efficiently, and the interceptors have to launch, engage, and reload fast enough that the defense doesn't get saturated. Fortem is working on all three layers — radar, AI-driven target allocation, and interceptor throughput. There are also RF-denied and GPS-denied environments where autonomy has to be fully onboard.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment. Fortem systems are in Ukraine, the Middle East, and at critical infrastructure sites globally as of 2025. Unlike most defense startups whose products exist mostly in prototype, Fortem has a real production line shipping real hardware. The Southern States grid partnership opens a civilian infrastructure market that could be much larger than the DoD one.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Radar engineers, guidance/navigation/control engineers, ML engineers for target recognition, manufacturing engineers, and mission software engineers. Hardware + software + AI + real deployment — this is one of the few Utah companies where all four converge on a problem that actually matters this decade. If you care about physical deterrence that is narrowly targeted at bad actors (rather than broad-effects weapons), the mission fit is strong.

## Ownership

Private. Raised Series C funding; investors include Mubadala, Maniv Mobility, Boeing HorizonX, DCVC, Signia Venture Partners, among others. VC-majority is possible — detailed cap table not fully public. The defense exit model (acquisition by a prime) is a real risk, but the new HQ build-out and the independent grid defense partnerships suggest they are building for durable independent operation at least for now. Worth verifying ownership concentration if a reader is joining with mission autonomy as a deal-breaker.

## Caveats

- Kinetic counter-UAS is a crowded field globally; competitive moat is real but not unassailable
- Defense primes and adjacent players (Anduril's Anvil, Epirus, others) will compete hard for the same DoD programs
- Ukraine-deployment case studies are powerful marketing but also expose the company to operational risk and export-control complexity
- VC-majority ownership (if confirmed) increases pressure toward an acquisition by a prime contractor
- The grid defense application is commercially exciting but legally novel — who is authorized to shoot down a drone over a power substation? Policy is still being written
