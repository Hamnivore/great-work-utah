# BYU-Pathway Worldwide

**Tier:** B — genuinely large-scale and unusual; limited by methodology and organizational constraints
**Domain:** Education — Low-cost online credentialing + workforce development
**Type:** Nonprofit (operated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)
**Stage:** Operational, scaling (80,000+ students, 40% growth projected in 2025)
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Founded:** 2017 (as BYU-Pathway Worldwide; predecessor PathwayConnect launched 2009)
**Website:** [byupathway.edu](https://byupathway.edu)

## Mission

Deliver accredited, affordable online certificates and degrees to people worldwide who otherwise lack access to higher education — and connect graduates to employment.

## Why it matters

Most of the world's population lives in countries where an accredited university degree is either unaffordable, inaccessible, or both. BYU-Pathway charges less than $100/month for most programs and delivers them fully online in 11 languages across 180+ countries. At 80,000+ students, it is one of the largest accredited online universities in the world by enrollment, with students concentrated in parts of Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia that Western edtech companies rarely serve.

The counterfactual is partial but real: for many students, the alternative is no post-secondary credential at all. The dual-mission structure — academic institution *and* employment placement engine — attempts to close the credentialing-to-income gap rather than just issuing degrees.

## The hard problem

Online education at low cost is not a technical problem; the barrier is governance, accreditation, trust, and local network effects. BYU-Pathway solves these through the LDS Church's existing global infrastructure (meetinghouses as study centers in 180 countries, local ecclesiastical networks for community, church employment resources). This is a supply-chain advantage that is very hard to replicate from scratch.

What remains unsolved: whether online-only, low-cost, faith-network-delivered education translates into durable economic mobility for graduates, or whether it produces credentials that employers in local markets do not recognize or value.

## Mechanism of impact

Access: getting credentials to people who have no path to a traditional university. Employment: BYU-Pathway partners with companies to place graduates directly. The feedback loop between enrollment, skill development, and employment is tighter than in most universities.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

People who care about educational access and are comfortable working within a faith-affiliated nonprofit structure. Roles span curriculum development, technology platform, student success, and global operations. The employment-development side resembles a workforce development organization more than a traditional university.

## Ownership

Wholly owned and operated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. This is the most unusual ownership structure in the wiki. Implications:
- No VC pressure to extract financial returns — mission can run long
- No public accountability for academic quality beyond regional accreditation (NWCCU)
- Mission is explicitly tied to LDS Church membership and community structures; ~90% of students are church members, though enrollment is open to all
- Church financial backing is significant but opaque (LDS Church does not publish financials)

## Caveats / red flags

- The educational methodology is standard online course delivery, not a frontier bet on how humans learn. The impact is access, not pedagogy
- 90%+ students are LDS Church members; the program's global reach runs primarily through church infrastructure rather than open market adoption
- Employment outcome data is not publicly available; the claim of functioning as an "economic development engine" is not independently verified
- The faith-affiliated governance structure means the organization's direction can change based on ecclesiastical priorities in ways that are not predictable to outside observers
- Accreditation (NWCCU via BYU-Idaho) is real but regionally-scoped; employer recognition in developing-world job markets is the harder question
