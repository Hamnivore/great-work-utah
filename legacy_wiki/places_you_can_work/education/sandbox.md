# Sandbox

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Education — Entrepreneurship education / practicum model
**Type:** Private program, partnered with universities (originated at BYU Marriott School of Business)
**Stage:** Expanding — originally BYU only (2020); now live at USU, Utah Tech, UVU; University of Utah begins Fall 2026
**HQ:** Provo, UT (with partner-university cohorts at each participating school)
**Founded:** 2020 (BYU)
**Website:** [web.sandbox.ing](https://web.sandbox.ing)

## Mission

Give undergraduate and graduate students one year of full-time building instead of classroom time, inside an accredited degree, and send them out having actually founded a company.

## Why it matters

The standard American undergraduate path selects for compliance and produces most of its graduates into wage employment — even among students who would thrive building things. Entrepreneurship programs at most universities are theory-heavy and extracurricular. Sandbox is the opposite: 18 credits of the senior year spent building a real venture-scale startup, with a cohort, mentors, and capital access. It is closer to a YC batch running inside an accredited degree than to a typical entrepreneurship minor.

The outcomes suggest this is working. In two years, five teams from BYU's Sandbox landed Y Combinator spots — roughly 2–3% YC acceptance rates for Sandbox alumni vs. YC's ~1% overall acceptance rate. Sandbox-tracked companies have raised against a combined $262M+ valuation. Starting salaries for Sandbox alumni run ~25% above comparable peers. For a program launched in 2020, that is a serious early track record.

The counterfactual argument is interesting. Great founders are rare, and the standard selection path into startup founding (go to a top CS program, drop out, move to San Francisco, find cofounders) is both narrow and heavily geographically concentrated. A credit-bearing practicum at a public or private state university in Utah is a fundamentally different on-ramp — it doesn't require dropping out, doesn't require moving, and can reach students who would otherwise never consider founding. If Sandbox-style programs scale, they could materially shift where the next generation of founders comes from.

## The hard problem

Entrepreneurship education has a long history of not working. Most programs produce business plans, not businesses. The unsolved question is whether you can *teach* the core of starting a company — taste, speed, resilience, talent for the specific thing — or whether you can only *select for* it. Sandbox's bet seems to be: you can't teach taste from a lectern, but you *can* create conditions (cohort, time, stakes, mentors, capital) where the students with latent founder-ability actually try and find out. Scaling that across multiple universities without losing the edge is the hard problem now. The original BYU program was small and hand-tuned; the USU/Utah Tech/UVU/U of U expansion tests whether the program's results travel.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct, at two levels. (1) Every Sandbox company that becomes real is direct impact. (2) Second-order: if the model works at scale across Utah higher ed, it becomes a replicable template for entrepreneurship practicums nationally. The founder, Chris Crittenden, is explicitly pitching Sandbox as an expandable program rather than a BYU-only experiment.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Students: ambitious undergraduate or graduate students with a venture idea and the willingness to work full-time on it through their senior year. Nothing is stopping you if you're at a partner institution. Operators/staff: people who have founded or early-employee'd at a real startup and want to shape how the next generation of founders gets built. Teaching-as-coaching, not teaching-as-lecturing.

## Ownership

The program is private (Sandbox, LLC, led by Chris Crittenden), partnered with universities that grant credit. Not a nonprofit; not a university program. Ownership structure is not fully public — a reader evaluating long-term mission autonomy should ask whether Sandbox takes equity in student companies, how revenue is split with universities, and whether there is outside investment. These details are not disclosed on the public site.

## Caveats

- The 2020 founding makes this program new; the long-term track record is only 5 years. Early results are promising but not yet replicated at national scale.
- The expansion from BYU (a selective, highly networked Mormon-affiliated university) to state schools is an open test. It is possible Sandbox's early outcomes depended on BYU's specific student population.
- Program is explicitly venture-scale-focused — the model selects for startup founders, not for small-business operators or nonprofit/mission founders. Readers who care about non-VC entrepreneurship should know this.
- Ownership opacity. Sandbox is a private company operating inside public universities; worth asking what the cap table and equity arrangement looks like.
- Joining Sandbox is joining a one-year pressure cooker; it is not the right fit for everyone even among ambitious students.
