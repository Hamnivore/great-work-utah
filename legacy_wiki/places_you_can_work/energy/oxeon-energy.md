# OxEon Energy

**Tier:** B — real with significant caveats; outclassed, mid-tier, or commoditizing
**Domain:** Energy — Solid Oxide Electrolysis & Fuel Cells
**Stage:** Private (DOE-funded, $36M+ in federal grants)
**HQ:** North Salt Lake, UT
**Founded:** 2017
**Website:** [oxeonenergy.com](https://oxeonenergy.com)

## Mission

Produce clean fuels and generate power at industrial scale using high-temperature solid oxide electrochemistry.

## Why it matters

OxEon built the MOXIE device aboard NASA's Perseverance rover — the first instrument to produce oxygen on Mars, extracting it from CO2 at 99.96% purity. That is not a metaphor. Their solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) work in some of the most extreme conditions imaginable and are now being scaled for industrial hydrogen production on Earth. The same electrochemistry that could one day support human life on Mars is also one of the most efficient pathways to green hydrogen, synthetic fuels, and long-duration energy storage at grid scale.

## The hard problem

Solid oxide electrolyzers operate at very high temperatures (700–900°C), which makes them more efficient than low-temperature alternatives (PEM, alkaline) but harder to build durably. The core challenges are materials degradation over thousands of hours, manufacturing at scale without losing performance, and cost reduction. OxEon has 30+ years of accumulated solid oxide R&D behind it and is now working on high-volume manufacturing with DOE backing.

## Mechanism of impact

Dual: (1) a tool/component that other industries use — their SOEC stacks go into hydrogen plants, industrial fuel synthesis, and potentially space systems; (2) direct deployment through their own systems. The Mars work also creates a unique feedback loop — extreme environment validation that feeds back into Earth-side improvements.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Electrochemists, materials scientists, ceramics engineers, and thermal systems engineers. People who are comfortable with manufacturing process development alongside fundamental R&D. Likely a small, tight team with deep domain expertise — the kind of place where one skilled engineer materially changes outcomes.

## Ownership

Primarily government-funded (DOE grants, SBIR/STTR), which is a meaningful autonomy signal — federal grants don't come with board seats or exit expectations. Private equity stake unknown, but the funding profile suggests founders likely retain substantial control. Government-backed R&D shops tend to operate on longer time horizons than VC-backed ones. Flag to verify if they take significant private capital.

## Caveats

- Small company; less name recognition than coastal startups, but the underlying tech is world-class
- Hydrogen economics are still uncertain — SOEC systems need cheap, abundant renewable power to produce green hydrogen cost-competitively
- Manufacturing scale-up is a hard problem in its own right and they are early in that journey
