# Torus

**Tier:** B — real technology, strong funding, but flywheel storage's role vs. lithium is still being proven at utility scale
**Domain:** Energy — Non-lithium energy storage (flywheel kinetic storage)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Growth ($267M+ total; $200M raise, September 2025)
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Founded:** ~2018 (Nate Walkingshaw, Gilbert Lee)
**Website:** [torus.co](https://www.torus.co)

## Mission

Deploy modular, non-lithium kinetic energy storage for utilities, data centers, and industrial applications — without the cycle degradation, chemical hazard, or supply chain dependence of lithium batteries.

## Why it matters

Energy storage is the bottleneck for renewable penetration and grid resilience. Lithium-ion dominates today, but it has structural weaknesses: finite cycle life (typically 2,000–5,000 deep cycles before significant degradation), supply chains dependent on Chinese lithium processing, and temperature sensitivity that creates performance cliffs in hot or cold climates. Torus's flywheel approach stores energy kinetically in a spinning mass — unlimited cycle life (mechanical wear is the only limit), no chemical degradation, no temperature cliff, built with domestic materials.

The near-term forcing function: data centers are facing power infrastructure crises from AI workload growth. Uninterruptible power and short-duration storage for grid frequency regulation are established markets where flywheels compete on cost of ownership, not just capital cost.

## The hard problem

Flywheels excel at short-duration, high-cycle applications (frequency regulation, UPS, bridging renewable intermittency at the minute scale) but have lower energy density than chemical batteries. The engineering challenge is making flywheel systems cost-competitive at modular scale and building the power electronics that let them integrate cleanly with utility and industrial systems at lower installed cost than incumbent UPS and short-duration storage alternatives.

## Mechanism of impact

Grid infrastructure: deployed modular power plants that provide firm, cycled-unlimited backup and balancing power for utilities, data centers, and industrial sites. Domestic supply chain: US-made with no lithium/cobalt dependency reduces exposure to critical mineral supply chain disruption.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Mechanical engineers with rotating machinery or energy storage backgrounds, power electronics engineers, and grid systems engineers. Founders come from energy industry backgrounds, not consumer tech. Growing fast — $200M raise in 2025 signals a transition from demonstration to deployment scale.

## Ownership

Private, VC-backed. Investors include Magnetar Capital (led $200M), Origin Ventures, Epic Ventures, Larry H. Miller Company, Zions Bank, Pelion, ICONIQ, Cumming Capital. VC-majority at this funding level. Mission alignment seems intact (non-lithium energy storage for grid resilience is a genuine bet), but the investor composition is financially-driven.

## Caveats / red flags

- Flywheel energy storage is a proven technology (Beacon Power, Vycon, Active Power have operated commercial systems), but most prior attempts at grid-scale flywheel deployment have struggled with economics at the kWh level vs. lithium
- The transition from "home energy management" (original positioning) to "modular power plants for utilities" is a significant scope change; whether the underlying technology translated cleanly is worth verifying
- Short-duration storage (flywheels' sweet spot) faces competition from lithium batteries, pumped hydro, and compressed air storage — the market is not empty
- $200M raise from Magnetar (a hedge fund with energy credit experience) may signal project-finance-style deployment rather than pure technology venture — worth understanding the capital structure
