# US Magnesium

**Tier:** C — included for completeness; honest "look elsewhere" call
**Domain:** Energy — Magnesium primary production from Great Salt Lake brine
**Also relevant to:** [Manufacturing & materials](../manufacturing-and-materials/), [Defense & security](../defense-and-security/) (strategic metal supply), [Other](../other/) (Great Salt Lake)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Established (largest magnesium producer in North America)
**HQ:** Rowley, UT (on the western shore of Great Salt Lake)
**Founded:** 1972 (as National Lead / Magcorp; current ownership since 2001)
**Website:** [usmagnesium.com](https://usmagnesium.com)

## Mission

Produce primary magnesium metal — one of the lightest structural metals and a critical material for aerospace, automotive lightweighting, and defense — from the high-salinity brine of the Great Salt Lake.

## Why it matters

Magnesium is a strategically important metal: it is ~30% lighter than aluminum, used in aerospace structures, automotive parts, smartphones and laptops, missile components, and increasingly in defense platforms where weight matters. Global primary magnesium production is ~90% concentrated in China. The US has essentially one primary magnesium producer operating at commercial scale: US Magnesium at Rowley, Utah.

That makes US Magnesium a strategic metals producer at the level of a national-security supplier. Defense programs that need domestic magnesium (ITAR-qualified, non-China-sourced) effectively have to source from this facility. The company is also a quiet but consequential piece of the Great Salt Lake story — brine extraction is one of the larger industrial activities on the lake, and the company is being pushed to consider impacts on lake level and exposed-bed dust.

Under the wiki's inclusion bar: US Magnesium is not a frontier-tech startup, but it is one of the more consequential industrial operations in Utah, and strategic-metals supply chain security is a real and growing national priority. Engineers who care about metals, materials, and industrial process work in a domestic-supply-chain context have a limited number of places to go; this is one.

## The hard problem

Primary magnesium production from brine is an extreme industrial process: solar evaporation ponds concentrate the brine, then electrolytic cells at ~700 °C split magnesium chloride into metal and chlorine gas. Running this at scale is chemically complex (chlorine handling is hazardous), energetically intensive, and environmentally exposed (both to lake level changes and to environmental regulation). Competing on price with Chinese magnesium — produced in facilities that don't carry US environmental compliance costs — is a persistent challenge.

## Mechanism of impact

Supply chain for the US aerospace and defense industrial base. Every domestic F-35 magnesium casting, every US-sourced automotive magnesium component, every missile structure that requires non-China-sourced material traces back here. Second-order: supports the argument for keeping strategic metals production domestic.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Chemical, electrochemical, and process engineers; metallurgists; industrial maintenance specialists; environmental compliance engineers. Large-industrial culture, not startup culture. Good fit for engineers who want to work on heavy industry with national-security relevance in Utah.

## Ownership

Private, owned by The Renco Group (Ira Rennert-affiliated). Not public; not venture-funded. Ownership has been stable but has had environmental and labor history worth reading on.

## Why this is not ⭐

Legacy heavy industry, not a frontier company. Environmental and labor records have significant complications (EPA enforcement actions, air-quality issues, past safety incidents). The strategic-materials mission is real but the company's operational history includes material questions about externalities. A company working on cleaner, more modern magnesium or lithium extraction from Great Salt Lake brine — with explicit lake-preservation intent — would earn S-tier; US Magnesium as currently operated does not.

## Caveats

- Environmental history includes EPA enforcement and significant emissions-related litigation
- Extraction from Great Salt Lake brine contributes to lake-level issues; relationship with preservation advocates is adversarial
- Private company; limited financial transparency
- Competing with subsidized Chinese supply on price is structurally difficult
- Workforce safety history has been contentious historically
