# Fervo Energy ⭐

**Tier:** S — category-defining; arguably the best place in the world to do this work
**Domain:** Energy — Enhanced Geothermal Systems
**Stage:** Series E (~$1.5B raised total)
**HQ:** Houston, TX *(primary project — Cape Station — is in Beaver County, UT)*
**Founded:** 2017
**Website:** [fervoenergy.com](https://fervoenergy.com)

## Mission

Deliver firm, 24/7 carbon-free electricity by unlocking geothermal energy at scale using modern drilling technology.

## Why it matters

The electricity grid needs baseload power that runs regardless of weather. Fervo's Cape Station project in Utah is on track to deliver 400 MW of continuous clean power by 2028 — the equivalent of a mid-sized natural gas plant, but with no fuel and no emissions. More importantly, Fervo is proving the template: take horizontal drilling and fiber-optic sensing from oil & gas, apply them to hot dry rock, and make enhanced geothermal systematic rather than speculative. If they succeed, the same playbook replicates across the western U.S. and eventually globally.

## The hard problem

Traditional geothermal requires naturally occurring hydrothermal reservoirs — rare, geographically limited. Enhanced geothermal (EGS) means creating a reservoir where one doesn't exist, by fracturing hot dry rock and circulating water through it. The hard problems: drilling economically at high temperatures, creating fracture networks with the right geometry, and maintaining flow rates long-term without short-circuiting or scaling up. Fervo is solving these by borrowing from the trillion-dollar oil & gas industry's toolbox.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment — they build and operate plants that go on the grid. If Cape Station succeeds at 400 MW, it becomes proof of concept for gigawatt-scale EGS across the West. The indirect impact is larger: showing the economics work pulls in capital and talent for the entire sector.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Drilling engineers, reservoir engineers, and data scientists with appetite for field work. The company hires heavily from oil & gas (90%+ of on-site labor at Cape Station comes from fossil fuel backgrounds). Unusual for a climate company — the culture is likely more roughneck pragmatism than Silicon Valley idealism, which is a feature if you want the work to actually get built.

## Ownership

VC-majority with a strategic investor (Google). At ~$1.5B raised across Series A–E, founders Tim Latimer and Jack Norbeck are almost certainly minority shareholders. Google's involvement as both investor and power purchaser creates some alignment, but the capital structure makes a near-term acquisition or IPO likely. The mission may survive a transition, but the scrappy R&D phase is probably behind them regardless.

## Caveats

- HQ is Houston; most of the interesting R&D and operations are in Utah, not locally managed day-to-day
- At Series E and 400 MW scale, this is increasingly execution rather than invention — the pioneering technical work is somewhat done
- Long-duration reservoir performance is still uncertain; no EGS project has operated at this scale long enough to fully validate economics
