## Mission

Produce sprouted-grain livestock feed in a self-contained hydroponic container ("PastureBox") that runs automatically on less than 10% of the water conventional pasture grazing requires, so ranchers can feed cattle in drought-prone regions without tapping groundwater or buying trucked-in alfalfa.

## Why it matters

The arithmetic of Utah agriculture: roughly 70% of the state's water consumption goes to alfalfa and cattle feed, and most of that water is diverted from rivers that used to flow into the Great Salt Lake. The lake's ongoing collapse is, in large part, a consequence of that agricultural water budget. Any technology that materially shifts the water-per-calorie ratio of livestock feed is directly relevant to the biggest environmental problem in the state.

Renaissance Ag's PastureBox is a fully automated, climate-controlled, 40-foot shipping-container-sized hydroponic sprouting system. It produces ~2,000 lbs/day of fresh sprouted microgreens as livestock feed from seed (usually wheat or barley), running on a fraction of the water pasture-grazing or alfalfa-irrigation require. The Utah Department of Agriculture verified the 90%+ water reduction claim. The company has dozens of commercial deployments by late 2024 with reported customer backlogs.

This is a pragmatic systems-integration play rather than a biotechnology frontier — sprouted fodder is not novel biology — but the *systems* of making it work at scale with consistent output and low operating labor is the real contribution. If the water economics of Utah livestock feed ever get serious policy attention (water rights reform, Great Salt Lake preservation pricing), Renaissance-style systems could become significantly more competitive than alfalfa irrigation.

## The hard problem

Getting consistent biomass production in a box that can tolerate temperature swings, water chemistry variance, seed quality variance, and mold contamination, with minimal operator intervention, at economics that actually compete with $200–$400/ton hay. The equipment-reliability problem (sprouting, mold, harvesting, wash-down) is the core engineering challenge. At scale, adoption also depends on ranchers and dairies being willing to switch feeding regimes — livestock acceptance and milk/beef-production consistency on sprouted fodder has to be demonstrated.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct: every customer-deployed PastureBox is a measurable reduction in irrigation water for that operation's feed base. Second-order: if regulatory or pricing signals on water intensify, this kind of system becomes a viable alternative to alfalfa irrigation across the arid West, which would be genuinely consequential for Great Salt Lake, the Colorado River basin, and water-stressed ag regions more broadly.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Mechanical, controls, and agricultural engineers; operations-focused product managers; sales and support staff who understand ranching and dairying. Small-company culture; individual leverage is high. Good fit for engineers who want to work on pragmatic ag-tech hardware in Utah rather than silicon-valley vertical-farming skyscrapers.

## Ownership

Private. Standard early-commercial ownership. Mission autonomy high at current stage.

## Why this is not ⭐

Systems-integration play rather than a technology-frontier bet. Sprouted fodder is not novel; what's novel is the packaged, automated delivery. The economics vs. cheap baled hay are borderline unless water becomes priced to reflect scarcity. Real business but not, yet, category-defining. Earns a star if water pricing shifts or adoption expands outside Utah at scale.

## Caveats

- Economics are marginal vs. $200–$400/ton hay in non-drought years
- Customer-acceptance risk (livestock switching, equipment reliability)
- Limited public financial information (private company, rural customer base)
- "Renaissance Ag" name shared by unrelated companies; verify entity before applying for work
