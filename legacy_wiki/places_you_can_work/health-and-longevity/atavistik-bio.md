## Mission

Discover drugs that target allosteric sites — the non-active-site pockets that regulate protein function — using a platform that maps the entire landscape of metabolite-protein interactions for the first time.

## Why it matters

The best drug targets are often the ones nobody knows exist yet. Allosteric drug sites (regulatory pockets distinct from the active site) are attractive because they're more specific to individual proteins and mutants, produce a modulation effect rather than full inhibition (better tolerated), and are simply unknown for most proteins. The Rutter lab's MIDAS technology — Mass spectrometry Integrated with equilibrium Dialysis for the discovery of Allostery Systematically — is the first platform to map metabolite-protein interactions at proteome scale. Published in *Science* (2023), it revealed that metabolite regulation of proteins is far more widespread than previously known.

The immediate clinical bet: AKT1 E17K, a common driver mutation in breast, uterine, and colorectal cancers. Existing pan-AKT inhibitors hit all three AKT isoforms, causing significant toxicity (hyperglycemia, immune suppression). An allosteric inhibitor specific to AKT1 E17K could be dramatically more tolerable for the millions of cancer patients carrying this mutation.

## The hard problem

Identifying allosteric sites computationally is unsolved at scale. MIDAS/AMPS gives Atavistik an experimental readout of metabolite binding, but converting those hits into drug-like molecules requires structural biology, medicinal chemistry optimization, and — critically — demonstration that the allosteric effect translates from cell lines to patients. Allosteric sites are notoriously conformationally dynamic; drug binding can be context-dependent in ways that fail in clinical translation.

## Mechanism of impact

Pipeline + platform: ATV-1601 (AKT1 E17K, solid tumors) in Phase 1; AKT1 inhibitor for hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT) entering Phase 1 in 2026; JAK2 V617F allosteric inhibitor in discovery. The platform enables systematic exploration of a class of targets that has been inaccessible — if it works, it broadens the druggable genome.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Medicinal chemists, structural biologists, biochemists interested in allostery. Computational chemists at the intersection of ML and structure-based drug design. This is a Cambridge-based company (not Utah for day-to-day work), but it's important to know as a Jared Rutter / U of U success story and as an example of the kind of deep science that can come from the Utah ecosystem.

## Ownership

Private. Co-founders: Jared Rutter (Professor, U of U Biochemistry, still on faculty) and Ralph DeBerardinis (UT Southwestern). $120M Series B led by Nextech Invest and The Column Group, with Lux Capital and Regeneron Ventures. Column Group + Lux are among the most selective biotech investors globally — their participation is a strong validation signal.

## Caveats

- HQ'd in Cambridge — day-to-day work is not in Utah; Utah connection is the founding science and Jared Rutter
- Allosteric drug development has historically had a high attrition rate; the context-dependence of allosteric effects is a recurring failure mode
- ATV-1601 is in Phase 1 (dose-escalation); clinical efficacy remains unproven
- $299M raised implies significant capital burn; typical Phase 2/3 oncology trials will require hundreds of millions more
- Competing with Relay Therapeutics, Recursion (allosteric adjacency), and major pharma discovery programs

## Why this is not ⭐

The platform is novel and the investors are elite, but no allosteric drug developed from de novo allosteric site discovery has yet received clinical approval. ATV-1601's Phase 1 data will be a major inflection point. If it shows tumor response in AKT1 E17K patients, the tier moves up.
