# Halia Therapeutics

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Health & longevity — Inflammaging / aging biology
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Series C ($56.3M raised), Phase 2a clinical data
**HQ:** Lehi, UT
**Founded:** 2017
**Website:** [haliatx.com](https://haliatx.com)

## Mission

Treat the root mechanism of aging — chronic low-grade inflammation — rather than its downstream symptoms.

## Why it matters

"Inflammaging" is the observation that aging is not just wear and tear but a sustained inflammatory state that undermines tissue homeostasis, depletes stem cells, accumulates senescent cells, and drives most of the diseases that kill people after 60: neurodegeneration, cardiovascular disease, cancer, metabolic disorders. The specific mechanism Halia targets — the NEK7/NLRP3 interaction — is a validated bottleneck in the inflammatory cascade. Block it, and you potentially slow a root cause of aging rather than managing one disease at a time.

Their lead drug, ofirnoflast (HT-6184), is the first oral allosteric NEK7 inhibitor ever developed. In a Phase 2a trial for lower-risk myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS, a blood cancer linked to inflammation), it produced a 72% hematologic response rate — a striking result for a disease that has had few good treatment options. MDS is the first indication, but the underlying mechanism is relevant across neurodegeneration, obesity-related disease, and aging broadly.

## The hard problem

NLRP3 is a well-validated target but notoriously hard to drug directly. Halia's approach — blocking NEK7's interaction with NLRP3 upstream, via an allosteric mechanism — is a more selective route that avoids the toxicity problems that killed many direct NLRP3 inhibitors in clinical trials. Getting that selectivity right, maintaining it in humans, and translating the benefit across multiple disease areas (not just MDS) are the remaining hard problems.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct therapeutic: if ofirnoflast succeeds in MDS and the mechanism validates, it becomes a platform for treating inflammation-driven aging across multiple diseases. The counterfactual is significant — most current MDS treatments are palliative; a disease-modifying oral drug would be genuinely new. The longevity angle is longer-horizon but scientifically credible.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Drug discovery scientists, medicinal chemists, clinical pharmacologists, and translational biologists who want to work on aging biology with real clinical traction. Small team for the stage of the program, meaning individual scientists have unusually high leverage. CEO Dave Bearss is an academic founder with a track record in cancer drug discovery.

## Ownership

Private, Series C. Dave Bearss founded the company and appears to remain in an executive role. At $56M raised, founders likely retain meaningful control. Investors include MedMountain Ventures and others. No evidence of strategic pharma ownership that would constrain the mission.

## Caveats

- Phase 2a in MDS is promising but a long way from approval; most drugs that look good in Phase 2a don't make it through Phase 3
- The jump from MDS to broader "aging" applications requires more clinical validation — right now the longevity claims rest on mechanism, not outcome data
- Small team and limited capital relative to the ambition of the mission
- NEK7/NLRP3 is not the only credible inflammaging target; if competing approaches (e.g., senolytics, IL-6 pathway drugs) advance faster, Halia may face commercial crowding
