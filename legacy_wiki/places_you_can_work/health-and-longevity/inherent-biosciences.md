## Mission

Use epigenetic patterns — not just genetic variants — to diagnose whether cells are actually functioning as they should.

## Why it matters

Most diagnostics ask: what genes do you have? Inherent asks a different question: are those genes being expressed correctly? Epigenetic markers (methylation patterns, chromatin state) reveal whether a cell is doing its job, not just what instructions it was born with. This is a fundamentally different signal, and for many conditions — especially ones where everyone has the "right" genes but something is still wrong — it's the signal that matters.

Their first application is male fertility. Traditional sperm analysis measures count and motility. Inherent's SpermQT test measures epigenetic function: can these sperm actually do what sperm are supposed to do? In roughly half of infertile couples, standard testing finds nothing wrong with the male partner even when the problem lies there. SpermQT is designed to close that diagnostic gap.

The platform ambition is bigger: epigenetic diagnosis of cellular dysfunction as a general method for conditions including autoimmune disease, developmental disorders, and aging-related decline.

## The hard problem

Epigenetics is noisy — the same cell type can have meaningfully different methylation patterns depending on age, environment, and measurement conditions. Building a clinically reproducible and actionable diagnostic from epigenetic data requires careful biomarker selection and validation across diverse populations. Inherent is early in that validation curve.

## Mechanism of impact

If the platform generalizes, it creates a new diagnostic modality — one that catches dysfunction before it becomes detectable by current methods. Early in development, so the mechanism of impact is currently narrow (male fertility) but the scientific bet is broad.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Molecular biologists, bioinformaticians, and clinician-scientists interested in epigenomics. Small, early team — individual contribution is very high. NIH funding suggests the science is credible to peer reviewers. Good fit for someone who wants to work on foundational biology with near-term clinical application.

## Ownership

Private, Series A. Founder-led. NIH and NSF grant funding suggests the company has scientific credibility independent of investors. Early enough that founders almost certainly retain control.

## Caveats

- Very early stage: SpermQT is the only product and it's in a niche market
- The leap from fertility diagnostics to broader epigenetic medicine is scientifically reasonable but commercially and regulatorily long
- Epigenetics-based diagnostics have a mixed history — many promising early findings haven't translated to robust clinical tools
- Worth watching but not yet proven
