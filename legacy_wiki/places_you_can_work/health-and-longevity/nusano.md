## Mission

Stabilize the supply chain for medical radioisotopes to enable next-generation cancer diagnostics and treatment.

## Why it matters

Radiopharmaceuticals — drugs that use radioactive isotopes to target and destroy cancer cells — are one of the most promising frontiers in oncology. Drugs like lutetium-177 and actinium-225 can deliver lethal radiation directly to tumors while sparing healthy tissue. But the entire field is bottlenecked by radioisotope supply: most production today depends on aging nuclear reactors in a handful of countries, with supply chains so fragile that a single reactor outage can halt clinical trials globally. Nusano's 190,000 sq ft West Valley City facility is designed to produce more than 40 different radioisotopes simultaneously, using cyclotron and accelerator technology rather than reactor-dependent production. This is critical infrastructure for a medical revolution.

## The hard problem

Radioisotopes have short half-lives — some decay within hours or days — which means production, logistics, and delivery must all be tightly coordinated. Scaling production using accelerator-based methods (rather than depending on nuclear reactors) requires solving beam power, target chemistry, and isotope separation at volumes that don't yet exist commercially. Nusano is building that capability from scratch in Utah.

## Mechanism of impact

Enabling infrastructure: if Nusano succeeds, the bottleneck for radiopharmaceutical drug development and deployment shifts away from "can we get the isotope?" toward "does the drug work?" That unblocks hundreds of clinical programs. The secondary impact is national security — reducing U.S. dependence on foreign isotope suppliers (currently Russia and elsewhere).

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Accelerator physicists, radiochemists, nuclear engineers, and manufacturing process engineers. This is hands-on, heavily regulated, technically demanding work. People who want to see a direct line between their bench work and patient outcomes.

## Ownership

Private, but ownership structure not publicly disclosed. No announced VC rounds — possible that it's founder-funded, PE-backed, or has strategic investors from the pharma industry. Worth investigating before drawing conclusions; a pharma-majority ownership would shift incentives toward supplying specific blockbuster drugs rather than the broad 40+ isotope mission.

## Caveats

- The company is at the intersection of nuclear physics and pharma regulation — two of the most complex regulatory environments in existence
- Ownership structure is opaque; the 40+ isotope breadth of the mission only holds if no single pharma buyer captures the roadmap
- Revenue depends on pharmaceutical industry demand for specific isotopes, which is tied to drug development timelines outside Nusano's control
