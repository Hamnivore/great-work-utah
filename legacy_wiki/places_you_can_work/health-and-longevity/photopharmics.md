# PhotoPharmics

**Tier:** B — compelling mechanism and FDA validation, but Phase 3 is Phase 3
**Domain:** Health & longevity — Non-invasive phototherapy for neurodegenerative disease
**Type:** Company (private, clinical-stage)
**Stage:** Series B ($16M + $6M extension); Phase 3 clinical trial underway
**HQ:** American Fork, UT
**Founded:** ~2015
**Website:** [photopharmics.com](https://photopharmics.com)

## Mission

Treat Parkinson's disease and other neurodegenerative disorders using precisely calibrated light delivered through the eye's non-visual photoreceptors.

## Why it matters

Parkinson's is the fastest-growing neurological disorder by prevalence; current standard-of-care treatments (levodopa, deep brain stimulation) address symptoms, not the underlying photoreceptor-circadian axis that PhotoPharmics is targeting. If the mechanism holds at Phase 3 scale, Celeste would be the first non-pharmacological, non-invasive disease-modifying treatment for Parkinson's — a device, not a drug, with no systemic side effects. The counterfactual: without this work, Parkinson's patients remain on symptomatic drug regimens that lose efficacy over time and carry significant side effects.

The company's founders have 30+ years in phototherapy and previously developed light solutions for circadian rhythm disorders that were acquired by Philips-Respironics. This is not a startup running a novel hypothesis for the first time.

## The hard problem

The scientific basis is intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) — non-visual photoreceptors in the retina that project directly to the suprachiasmatic nucleus (the brain's master clock) and appear to also modulate dopaminergic signaling pathways disrupted in Parkinson's. Delivering the right wavelengths at the right intensity to specifically activate ipRGCs without stimulating the visual photoreceptors that cause glare requires precise spectral engineering. The harder problem is proving at Phase 3 scale that this mechanism produces durable, disease-modifying effect — not just circadian rhythm improvement.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct therapeutic deployment: Celeste is a tablet-sized device the patient uses at home. If approved by the FDA (Breakthrough Device designation already granted, which expedites review), it reaches Parkinson's patients without a hospital visit or a surgical procedure. One million new Parkinson's diagnoses occur annually worldwide. Even a meaningful slowing of progression has a significant human and economic impact.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Neuroscientists, biomedical engineers with photonics or optics background, clinical trial specialists, and regulatory affairs experts. Small team in a relatively quiet part of Utah County — people who want to be close to the science, not inside a large pharma apparatus.

## Ownership

Private. Series B led by Kickstart Fund (Salt Lake City VC), with $6M oversubscribed extension. FDA Breakthrough Device status provides some regulatory runway advantage. No PE or late-stage investors visible in public disclosures. Relatively small capital raise for a Phase 3 therapeutic — either suggests lean operating model or near-term capital need to complete enrollment.

## Caveats / red flags

- Phase 3 is genuinely uncertain — Breakthrough Device designation reflects FDA's view that the mechanism is worth fast-tracking, but does not predict efficacy at the primary endpoint
- The trial ("Light for PD") enrolled 200+ participants out of ~300 target; results are pending — this entry should be revisited when data readout occurs
- Light therapy devices have a complicated regulatory and market history; payer coverage for a novel device is a real post-approval hurdle
- The company has been operating for ~10 years at relatively modest funding levels — could reflect deep scientific diligence or slow commercial progress
