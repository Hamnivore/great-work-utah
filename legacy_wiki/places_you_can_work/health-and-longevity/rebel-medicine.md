# Rebel Medicine

**Tier:** C — reformulation of validated drug, not a breakthrough, but real opioid reduction potential
**Domain:** Health & longevity — non-opioid post-surgical pain management
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Phase 2 clinical trial (IND filed 2025; $6M Series A, Crocker Ventures + Utah Innovation Fund)
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Website:** [rebelmedicine.com](https://www.rebelmedicine.com)

## Mission

Eliminate opioid use after surgery by providing a long-acting (72-hour) injectable local anesthetic that controls post-operative pain at the site without systemic opioids.

## Why it matters

Post-surgical opioid prescriptions are one of the primary entry points to opioid addiction. Patients are routinely sent home with opioid prescriptions after routine procedures, and many develop dependence. A local anesthetic that works for 72 hours post-surgery — long enough to cover the acute pain window — could eliminate the opioid prescription for many surgical cases. Given that 30M+ surgeries are performed annually in the US, the public health leverage is meaningful.

## The hard problem

Bupivacaine (the active ingredient in Alevatrix™) is a well-validated local anesthetic. The hard problem is the controlled-release delivery matrix: encapsulating it in a form that provides sustained, consistent drug release at the surgical site for 72 hours without burst release or systemic toxicity. The competing product (Exparel by Pacira Biosciences) already does this with liposomal bupivacaine — Rebel Medicine needs to demonstrate clinical or cost differentiation against a well-entrenched approved product.

## Ownership

Private. $6M Series A (Crocker Ventures, Central Texas Angel Network, Utah Innovation Fund, others).

## Caveats

- Exparel (liposomal bupivacaine, Pacira) is already FDA-approved for exactly this indication — Rebel Medicine needs to beat an approved product
- The formulation differentiation (injectable matrix vs. liposomal) needs clinical evidence that it's meaningfully better
- $6M is underpowered for Phase 2 + eventual Phase 3; significant additional capital will be required
- Reformulation companies are common in pharma; the "non-opioid" framing is partly marketing for an existing drug class
