# Spire Therapeutics

**Tier:** B — real clinical data, non-invasive, targeting huge unmet needs
**Domain:** Health & longevity — focused ultrasound neuromodulation for pain and depression
**Also relevant to:** [Tools for thought](../tools-for-thought/)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Phase 2 complete (chronic pain + depression); Phase 3 initiating
**Location:** Salt Lake City, UT (U of U Biomedical Engineering spinout)
**Website:** [spire.us](https://spire.us)

## Mission

Treat chronic pain, depression, and other neurological conditions without surgery or drugs, using low-intensity focused ultrasound (LIFU) to modulate overactive deep brain circuits.

## Why it matters

About one-in-three patients with chronic pain or major depression don't respond to first-line treatments (opioids, antidepressants). Surgery (deep brain stimulation) works for some, but is invasive, expensive, and risky. Spire's Diadem device delivers precise ultrasonic pulses to deep brain regions — the anterior cingulate cortex for pain, subgenual cingulate for depression — without opening the skull. If Phase 3 confirms Phase 2 results, Diadem would be the first non-invasive, drug-free treatment for both conditions that works fast (clinical studies show rapid, immediate improvement).

## The hard problem

Low-intensity focused ultrasound can reach deep brain targets non-invasively, but precise targeting through the skull's variable acoustic properties is a hard engineering problem. Skull bone creates refraction and attenuation; Diadem solves this using patient-specific acoustic models derived from imaging. The mechanism of neuromodulation (exactly why LIFU modulates neural circuits) is still incompletely understood — an open scientific question that may affect FDA approval framing.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment: a cleared medical device that clinicians can use in-office. The closest analog is transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), which is approved for depression but requires many sessions, doesn't reach deep targets well, and requires expensive capital equipment. Diadem aims to be more effective and reach targets TMS can't.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Biomedical engineers (acoustic, ultrasound, neuroimaging). Clinical researchers interested in neuromodulation. Regulatory / FDA strategy. Market opportunity is enormous (chronic pain + depression together represent some of the largest healthcare cost categories globally).

## Ownership

U of U spinout. Altitude Lab resident. Founder: Jan Kubanek (Associate Professor, BME, U of U). Funding details not publicly available beyond pitch competition wins and institutional support.

## Caveats

- Phase 3 has not reported; Phase 2 results are promising but smaller trials
- FDA regulatory path for a novel neuromodulation device is uncertain — precedent is limited
- Mechanism uncertainty (how exactly LIFU modulates neurons) could complicate FDA labeling claims
- TMS/ECT/DBS incumbents are entrenched in clinical practice; adoption requires physician re-education
- No disclosed Series A funding — clinical trial costs may require significant new capital

## Why this is not ⭐

Strong technology and a genuinely unmet need, but Phase 3 outcome is unknown, and medical device startups at this stage often run into clinical trial execution risk and regulatory timeline risk. If Phase 3 confirms efficacy and FDA clears it, the tier should move up.
