# Recursion Pharmaceuticals ⭐

**Tier:** S — category-defining; arguably the best place in the world to do this work
**Domain:** Health & longevity — AI-driven drug discovery
**Type:** Company (public, NASDAQ: RXRX)
**Stage:** Clinical stage (~$4B market cap, $785M cash as of late 2025)
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Founded:** 2013
**Website:** [recursion.com](https://www.recursion.com)

## Mission

Industrialize drug discovery by mapping the full space of human biology using AI and massive-scale automated experimentation.

## Why it matters

Drug discovery is broken. A new drug takes ~12 years and $2.6B to develop on average, with a ~90% clinical failure rate. Most of that failure comes from not understanding biology well enough at the start. Recursion's bet is that if you run millions of experiments weekly, generate 50+ petabytes of proprietary phenomic, transcriptomic, and proteomic data, and train large foundation models on it, you can compress the discovery timeline dramatically and find targets that purely human-driven science would miss. Their BioHive-2 supercomputer (504 NVIDIA H100s, 2 exaflops) is one of the most powerful in pharma. They've already moved one program from novel target biology to preclinical candidate in under 18 months — roughly twice the industry average speed.

## The hard problem

Biology is not yet a science in the way physics is — there is no periodic table of cellular behavior. Recursion's core bet is that you can learn the latent structure of biology empirically, by perturbing cells in millions of ways and observing the results, then using AI to find patterns humans couldn't see. Whether those patterns generalize from in vitro experiments to actual patients is the unresolved question that every drug discovery company faces, but Recursion is attacking it at a scale nobody else has matched.

## Mechanism of impact

Increasingly, a platform: Recursion has shifted toward selling access to the Recursion OS as a service to large pharmaceutical companies (Bayer, Roche, Sanofi are partners), rather than exclusively developing drugs internally. This is commercially rational but means their impact depends partly on how well Big Pharma uses the tools. Internally, they still have a drug pipeline — REC-1245 (oncology) is the furthest along.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

ML engineers, computational biologists, and wet lab scientists who want to work at a genuine intersection of AI and biology — not in a lab that applies GPT to literature review, but one running real experiments at machine scale. The team is large (~1000 people) and technically deep. Salt Lake City location means lower cost of living and less competition for talent than SF/Boston biotech hubs.

## Ownership

Public company (RXRX). Founder Chris Gibson is still CEO — an unusual level of founder continuity for a public biotech. However, public market pressure is real: the company has been posting significant net losses (~$160M/quarter) and shareholders have scrutinized the platform-vs-pipeline tension. The shift to platform-as-a-service may be financially necessary but risks drifting toward serving Big Pharma's priorities rather than pursuing the most impactful biology.

## Caveats

- Public market losses create pressure for near-term commercial wins over long-horizon science
- The platform-as-a-service pivot means Recursion's impact is increasingly mediated through pharma partners, not direct
- In vitro → patient translation remains unproven at scale — lots of impressive biology doesn't become drugs
- Competition from Insilico Medicine, Schrödinger, and large pharma's own AI teams is intensifying
