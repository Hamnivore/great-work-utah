# Intactis Bio

**Tier:** C — plausible; genuinely novel compute paradigm but pre-revenue, pre-validation, pre-everything
**Domain:** Machine intelligence — Biological computing / hybrid biosilicon AI
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Pre-seed / Nucleus Fund grant; Phase 1 alpha platform live (2025)
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Founded:** 2024 (Daniel Rodriguez-Granrose, founder & CEO)
**Website:** [intactis.bio](https://intactis.bio)

## Mission

Build the world's first engineered biological computers: 3D-printed living neural tissue wired to silicon processors, enabling AI inference that is dramatically more energy-efficient and adaptive than pure silicon.

## Why it matters

The energy cost of AI inference is on track to become a civilization-scale constraint. Training a frontier model consumes the equivalent of a small power plant; at global deployment scale, inference will dwarf that. Biological neural tissue — the human brain — achieves ~20W for capabilities no data center can match at any power level. If that efficiency can be harnessed in a controllable, manufacturable form, it changes the economics of AI computation at a fundamental level. Intactis is one of a handful of groups worldwide attempting to build *biological computers* rather than just study biological circuits — a distinction that matters for anyone thinking about post-silicon compute.

## The hard problem

The challenge is not just growing neurons; it's training them on a target task and getting useful outputs back out. Intactis's approach: convert datasets into electrical pulse patterns, deliver them to living 3D-printed neural tissue, and let the tissue adapt — then read inference outputs from the same tissue via a brain-machine interface. The hard parts: keeping the tissue alive and consistent for commercial use, establishing reliable input/output protocols, and demonstrating that biological inference actually beats silicon on a real benchmark (not just a toy task). None of these have been solved at commercial scale by anyone.

## Mechanism of impact

Initially: inference-as-a-service. Customers submit models trained on silicon; Intactis converts them to biological inference format and runs them on living neural biochips. Phase 2 (planned 2027) expands to full biological training. This is a slow-burn compute infrastructure play — impact is through enabling future AI workloads at lower energy cost, not through a direct consumer product.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Neuroscientists, bioelectronic engineers, and ML researchers who want to work at the intersection of living systems and computation. The team is tiny and the work is experimental — this is a founding-team-plus-a-few-researchers environment, not a place to join for career safety. High leverage for someone with the right mix of neuroscience and engineering; very high ambiguity.

## Ownership

Pre-seed. Backed by Nucleus Fund ($250K from Utah's state-backed university spinout fund) and RPV (San Francisco, $100K part of $300K commitment). Founder-led. Not VC-majority; mission autonomy is intact at current scale.

## Why this is not ⭐

No validated benchmark results. No commercial revenue. The tissue-culture-to-inference pipeline hasn't publicly demonstrated competitive performance vs. GPU inference on any real task. The concept is compelling; the execution risk is extreme. Organic Robotics (Cornell), Cortical Labs (Melbourne), and FinalSpark (Switzerland) are further along on similar paradigms.

## Caveats / red flags

- Founded 2024; no independent technical validation of biological inference performance
- The tissue viability and consistency challenge is massive — living cells are not reliable semiconductor substrates
- Competitive field: Cortical Labs (has published peer-reviewed results with DishBrain), FinalSpark (commercial neuromorphic cloud platform using living neurons), and standard silicon neuromorphic chips (Intel Loihi, IBM NorthPole) all have head starts
- Utah-based but no obvious Utah-specific advantage — tissue culture labs exist everywhere
- $350K in total capital is pre-proof-of-concept territory; will need much more funding to survive to Phase 2
