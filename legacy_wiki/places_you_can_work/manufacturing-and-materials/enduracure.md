## Mission

Build flexible electronics on polymer substrates that perform like conventional plastics during use but can be deliberately dissolved in mild chemical baths at end-of-life, allowing recovery of the valuable electronic components (chips, metals, displays) without the e-waste problem.

## Why it matters

Global e-waste is growing faster than the overall economy; recycling rates are under 20%, and the valuable materials in electronics (rare earths, gold, cobalt) are effectively being landfilled. One large reason is that circuit boards bond electronics to substrates that cannot be cleanly separated. EnduraCure's approach — photocured polymers that act like normal substrates until intentionally dissolved — potentially unlocks a whole category of recyclable electronics: medical sensors, smart packaging, wearables, and eventually consumer devices.

This is the kind of materials-science bet that either becomes foundational (imagine if every AirPod or blood-glucose sensor dissolved into recoverable parts at end of life) or remains niche (specialty medical applications only). The mechanism and chemistry are novel; the commercial question is whether the cost and performance can match conventional substrates.

## The hard problem

A polymer that performs identically to conventional flexible substrates under normal use conditions but dissolves cleanly in a mild bath is a specific and difficult design target. You need:
- Dimensional stability during device use (no slow creep)
- Thermal and humidity resistance
- Compatibility with standard flexible-electronics fabrication (roll-to-roll printing, lithography)
- Dissolution that is triggered *only* by the intended solvent, not by sweat, rain, or dishwasher cycles
- Recoverable component yields that are high enough for recycling economics to work

Chen Wang's lab is working through this chemistry; EnduraCure is the commercialization vehicle.

## Mechanism of impact

If it works at commercial scale, direct impact on e-waste recovery. Second-order: if the approach becomes a standard for medical sensors, it addresses the specific medical-device landfill problem (hospitals dispose of enormous quantities of single-use monitoring electronics). Third-order: could pressure the broader consumer electronics industry toward designed-for-recycling substrates.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Polymer chemists, materials scientists, electronics manufacturing engineers, and business development people who understand the flexible-electronics supply chain. Early-stage startup from an academic lab — the team is small, roles are broad, and individual leverage is very high. Not the right place for someone who needs structure; right place for someone who wants to build a new materials company from the ground up.

## Ownership

Private, early-stage spinout from the University of Utah. The U of U Technology Licensing Office holds some IP position via standard university licensing; founder/CEO Dennis Pruzan runs the company. NSF STTR Phase 1 non-dilutive funding. No VC round yet as far as I can find. Mission autonomy high at this stage.

## Why this is not ⭐

Too early. The technology is promising, the e-waste problem is huge, but there is no commercial deployment yet. S-tier is reserved for proven scale or unique position; EnduraCure needs to prove either before earning a star. Watch carefully — this is the kind of bet that moves from "interesting lab paper" to "category leader" very quickly if the chemistry works.

## Caveats

- Very early stage — NSF STTR Phase 1 is the earliest federal funding tier
- Chemistry might not survive the transition from lab to manufacturing
- Even if it works, flexible electronics margins are thin; capturing value is hard
- Consumer electronics adoption of recyclable substrates depends on policy as much as technology
- Small team; single-point-of-failure risk on Dennis Pruzan and Chen Wang
