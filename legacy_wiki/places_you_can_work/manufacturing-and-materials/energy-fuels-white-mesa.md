# Energy Fuels — White Mesa Mill

**Tier:** A — uniquely positioned domestic critical-minerals infrastructure
**Domain:** Manufacturing & materials — Uranium fuel + rare earth oxide production
**Also relevant to:** [Energy](../energy/) (uranium fuel supply), [Defense & security](../defense-and-security/) (domestic rare earth supply for defense magnets)
**Type:** Company (public, NYSE: UUUU)
**Stage:** Public, operational; Phase 2 rare earth expansion underway
**HQ:** Lakewood, CO (key asset: White Mesa Mill, Blanding, UT)
**Founded:** 1987 (White Mesa Mill acquired 2012)
**Website:** [energyfuels.com](https://www.energyfuels.com)

## Mission

Process uranium and rare earth elements from North American ore bodies at the only conventional uranium mill and the only heavy rare earth oxide processing facility in the United States.

## Why it matters

**On uranium:** Energy Fuels produced approximately two-thirds of all U.S. uranium between 2017 and 2023. The US has essentially no domestic uranium fuel production without White Mesa Mill. Nuclear power provides ~20% of US electricity and ~50% of US carbon-free electricity; domestic uranium fuel supply is a national security question.

**On rare earths:** Every EV motor, wind turbine generator, and high-performance military aircraft depends on permanent magnets made from neodymium-praseodymium (NdPr), dysprosium (Dy), and terbium (Tb). China controls >85% of global rare earth processing and >90% of heavy rare earth separation. As of 2025, White Mesa Mill is the only facility in the Western Hemisphere capable of commercially producing separated, on-spec heavy rare earth oxides. Energy Fuels achieved commercial NdPr production in 2024 and produced the first US dysprosium and terbium oxides in decades in 2025.

The counterfactual: without White Mesa Mill, US rare earth processing capacity is essentially zero.

## The hard problem

Rare earth elements occur together in ore and are chemically nearly identical — separating them requires long solvent extraction (SX) circuits. Building this infrastructure from scratch takes a decade and billions of dollars; the existing processing license and infrastructure at White Mesa Mill makes it the only credible near-term path to domestic US heavy rare earth supply. Getting to commercial production at the purity and consistency that permanent magnet manufacturers require — Energy Fuels' Dy oxide passed initial qualification with a major South Korean magnet manufacturer in late 2025 — is itself a multi-year technical challenge.

## Mechanism of impact

Critical materials supplier: White Mesa produces uranium fuel for nuclear reactors, NdPr for EV and wind magnets, and is scaling toward 6,000 tpa NdPr + 240 tpa Dy + 66 tpa Tb per year by 2027. The company is acquiring Australian Strategic Materials to build a "mine-to-metal & alloy" rare earth value chain. Lower-than-expected CAPEX and cost structure positions it as "among the lowest cost NdPr production in the world" per January 2026 disclosures.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Hydrometallurgical engineers, rare earth processing chemists, nuclear fuel specialists, environmental compliance experts, and mining engineers. This is a physical-infrastructure company doing hard chemistry at scale — not a software shop. White Mesa Mill is in Blanding, UT (San Juan County, rural southeastern Utah), which means this path is for people who want to be close to a genuinely critical industrial operation rather than in a tech office.

## Ownership

Public (NYSE: UUUU). Widely held; no controlling shareholder. Management team has been stable; Mark Chalmers (CEO) has led the rare earth pivot. Revenue from uranium sales + growing rare earth offtake. The Chemours monazite offtake provides near-term feedstock (500 tpa) from Florida/Georgia mineral sands; Phase 2 expansion requires broader feedstock sourcing.

## Caveats / red flags

- HQ is Lakewood, CO — the Utah connection is purely through White Mesa Mill (the asset), which is genuinely irreplaceable but is an operation within a public company, not a Utah-founded startup
- As a public company, strategy can shift based on commodity price cycles — uranium price swings have historically dominated management attention; rare earth expansion is newer and could be deprioritized
- Heavy rare earth volumes (Dy, Tb) are currently tiny (kilograms-scale, 2025); scaling to commercial production by 2027 is a real technical and permitting challenge
- The South Korean magnet manufacturer qualification is a positive signal but not a signed offtake; real commercial rare earth revenue is still ahead
- Chinese REE producers have repeatedly used pricing to undercut Western competitors — the economics of domestic REE processing depend on sustained policy support (IRA, DoD contracts) to bridge the gap
