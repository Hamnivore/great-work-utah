# Ionic Mineral Technologies (Ionic MT)

**Tier:** A — strong; Utah-specific raw material advantage addressing a real supply chain problem
**Domain:** Manufacturing & materials — Battery anode materials / critical minerals
**Also relevant to:** [Energy](../energy/) (EV battery supply chain), [Defense & security](../defense-and-security/) (domestic critical materials)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Series B ($29M, oversubscribed, July 2025)
**HQ:** Provo, UT
**Founded:** ~2020
**Website:** [ionicmt.com](https://ionicmt.com)

## Mission

Process Utah's halloysite clay into nano-silicon anode material that gives lithium-ion batteries 30–40% more energy density — from a domestic source with no complex chemistry required.

## Why it matters

The graphite anode in virtually every lithium-ion battery today is sourced predominantly from China. Silicon has roughly 8x the theoretical charge-storage capacity of graphite and would enable longer-range EVs, faster charging, and smaller/lighter batteries across every application — but silicon's extreme volume expansion during cycling has defeated most efforts to commercialize silicon anodes at scale. Halloysite is a naturally occurring clay mineral whose tubular nanostructure gives silicon the void space it needs to expand during charging, making the material more cycle-stable without expensive synthetic engineering.

The competitive fact: the largest known high-purity halloysite deposits in the United States are in Juab County, Utah. Ionic MT has leased 4,000+ acres (Silicon Ridge) and holds a wholly owned 550-acre deposit (Halloysite Hills, 2.4M tons of reserves). A competitor trying to match this material position would need to find equivalent geology.

## The hard problem

Making silicon anodes that cycle reliably in real battery systems — not just in lab-scale coin cells — while producing them at the commodity price and volume needed to compete with graphite. The halloysite route sidesteps some of the synthetic engineering required by other silicon anode approaches, but scaling production from lab to 1,000+ metric tons annually while maintaining quality is an unsolved manufacturing problem.

## Mechanism of impact

Critical materials supply chain: Ionisil™ nano-silicon is a drop-in anode material that battery manufacturers can blend with graphite (at 15% silicon loading, Ionic MT claims doubling of anode capacity). Three co-products from a single feedstock reduce production economics: Ionisil™ nano-silicon (energy), IonAL™ high-purity alumina (battery separators, ceramics), and rare earth / critical technology metals recently discovered at Silicon Ridge.

If Ionic MT succeeds at scale, it shifts a piece of the EV battery supply chain from China-dependent graphite to a US-domestic silicon source.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Process engineers and electrochemists comfortable working across mining, materials processing, and battery cell development. Also supply chain and business development people who understand automotive and defense procurement cycles. The company spans geology, chemistry, and advanced manufacturing — rare vertical integration that suits generalists who can operate across all three.

## Ownership

Private, VC-backed. Series B ($29M) closed July 2025 (oversubscribed). No disclosed investor names found in public databases. The oversubscribed round signals investor confidence in the market timing around domestic battery material supply chains.

## Caveats / red flags

- Silicon anode commercialization has been "5 years away" for a long time; many well-funded efforts have stalled at scale
- The claimed 30–40% energy density improvement at the battery-pack level is lower than the 8x theoretical capacity advantage — cell-level benefits shrink substantially as you build up to a full pack
- Battery manufacturers have qualified graphite supply chains; switching to silicon anode materials requires re-qualification, which is slow and expensive
- The rare earth discovery at Silicon Ridge is early — major rare earth plays require extensive environmental review, and "rare earths in Utah" has a fraught regulatory history
- VC majority ownership is not confirmed but likely at Series B; mission alignment with "domestic supply chain" may not survive if an Asian battery manufacturer acquires the company
