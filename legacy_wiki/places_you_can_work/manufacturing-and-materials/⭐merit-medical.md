## Mission

Design and manufacture the disposable devices used in interventional, diagnostic, and therapeutic procedures — the catheters, micro-access kits, stents, ablation tools, and embolization devices that interventional cardiologists, radiologists, and oncologists use every day.

## Why it matters

Minimally invasive medicine — going into the body through a tiny arterial puncture instead of opening up the chest — has quietly been one of the largest medical revolutions of the last 40 years. Heart attacks that used to require open-heart surgery are now handled with catheter-based procedures. Many cancers are diagnosed via image-guided biopsy rather than exploratory surgery. Strokes that used to kill people are reversed with thrombectomy. Every one of these procedures depends on a supply of precision disposable devices.

Merit Medical is one of the global suppliers that makes those devices. They do interventional cardiology, interventional radiology, endoscopy, and critical care. In October 2025 they acquired Pentax's C2 CryoBalloon for GI cryotherapy and are transferring manufacturing to South Jordan. They just launched the Micro ACE advanced micro-access system. Founder Fred Lampropoulos ran the company for 38 years until Martha Aronson took over as CEO in October 2025 — a succession event worth watching, since the founder-led culture is part of what's built Merit.

Utah is central to the story. The South Jordan HQ, the manufacturing footprint, and much of R&D are in the Salt Lake metro. Merit is one of the largest medical device employers in the Mountain West.

## The hard problem

Interventional device design is a weird intersection of mechanical engineering, materials science (biocompatible polymers, hydrophilic coatings), regulatory engineering (FDA 510(k) and PMA pathways), and surgeon workflow. The devices have to be reliable in the hand of someone navigating a catheter through arteries under X-ray, sometimes in an emergency, where a kinked wire can kill someone. Getting that right at scale — millions of units per year, across product lines covering every anatomical region — is itself a sustained hard problem that doesn't get the respect it deserves.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment in clinical care, at global scale. Every interventional procedure that uses a Merit device is impact footprint. Because the market for interventional medicine continues to grow (aging populations, more procedures moving out of open surgery), the volume grows with it.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Mechanical and biomedical engineers who want to design things that a doctor holds in their hands. Materials engineers for the catheter polymer stack. Quality and regulatory engineers (these are sometimes dismissed, but they are how medical devices actually get to market). Manufacturing engineers for the precision extrusion and assembly lines. Merit is also a good place for people who want to stay in Utah and still work on genuinely global products.

## Ownership

Public (NASDAQ: MMSI). Founder-operator culture for the first 38 years; new professional CEO as of late 2025. Institutional ownership standard for mid-cap med-tech. Mission autonomy is decent — interventional devices are a stable, growing market, no obvious activist restructuring pressure, though the CEO transition creates some strategic uncertainty.

## Caveats

- Public mid-cap with quarterly-earnings discipline
- CEO transition from founder to professional CEO is a genuine inflection point; culture and product pace could shift either direction
- Regulatory environment (FDA, EU MDR) continues to get stricter; compliance overhead is real
- Competing with very large players (Boston Scientific, Medtronic, Abbott) in most categories
- Acquisition-driven growth (C2 CryoBalloon, others) means integration risk
