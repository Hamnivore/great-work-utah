# FamilySearch ⭐

**Tier:** S — category-defining; arguably the best place in the world to do this work
**Domain:** Other — Global genealogy infrastructure
**Also relevant to:** [Scientific infrastructure](../scientific-infrastructure/), [Health & longevity](../health-and-longevity/)
**Type:** Nonprofit, religious affiliation (The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)
**Stage:** Established institution — predecessor organization (Genealogical Society of Utah) founded 1894
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Website:** [familysearch.org](https://www.familysearch.org)

## Mission

Digitize, preserve, index, and make freely accessible the genealogical records of all humanity — every person, every family, linked across generations, no account required.

## Why it matters

FamilySearch is the largest genealogy organization in the world and runs the largest genealogy library in the world. As of 2024, the collection includes 2.4+ million rolls of microfilmed records, 340,000+ books, and 20.5+ billion searchable records and images online, with 2.5 billion new records added in 2024 alone. The service is free. You don't need to be LDS to use any of it.

This is unusual enough that it's worth saying plainly: a religious denomination is operating, at its own expense, a piece of civilizational infrastructure that academics, historians, epidemiologists, and cancer researchers depend on. The **Utah Population Database (UPDB)** — which linked FamilySearch's genealogy records to Utah birth, death, and medical records — is the specific instrument that enabled Mary-Claire King and the Skolnick group at U of U to identify **BRCA1** and **BRCA2**. It has since contributed to identifying dozens of disease-associated genes. None of that happens without FamilySearch's underlying records.

The counterfactual is strong. Without the Genealogical Society of Utah's century-long, religiously-motivated project of global genealogy digitization, the intergenerational population linkages that make UPDB uniquely powerful would not exist anywhere. The closest comparable resource is Iceland's deCODE — another small population with unusually complete records — but UPDB is ~10x larger.

For a reader thinking about where to work: FamilySearch is a real software organization doing real problems at scale. They are rolling out AI for handwriting recognition on historical documents, record linkage, and search. They operate a large engineering organization in Utah that rarely gets talked about in tech circles.

## The hard problem

Indexing handwritten historical records from 200+ years and dozens of languages is one of the hardest OCR and ML problems around. Record linkage (matching "John Smith b. 1820 Ohio" to "J. Smith, farmer, b. 1820s" across different sources) is a classic unsolved problem at this scale. Privacy-preserving access — making records useful for research while protecting living people — is another active area. On top of all this: FamilySearch has to operate ethically as a religious-affiliated service that serves a massive non-LDS user base, which means hard product and policy decisions about what record metadata to expose.

## Mechanism of impact

Enabling infrastructure, at civilizational scale. FamilySearch itself doesn't "change the world" in one step — but the research it unblocks does. Historical research, population genetics, cancer genomics, and increasingly computational social science all draw on these records. Second-order: the RootsTech conference each year is the world's largest genealogy event and shapes the broader genealogy-tech industry (Ancestry.com, MyHeritage, 23andMe trees all integrate with FamilySearch).

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Software engineers and ML engineers who want to work on genuinely hard OCR, record linkage, and large-scale data infrastructure problems, with a mission that is not about ad revenue or shareholder returns. Historians, archivists, and linguists for record curation. Dispositionally: comfortable working inside a religiously-affiliated organization (employment does not require church membership, but the culture is influenced by it). Patient — this is a century-long project that will be here long after any of us.

## Ownership

Nonprofit, funded by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Mission autonomy is effectively absolute: no investor pressure, long time horizon, deep funding stability. The main mission-integrity question a thoughtful reader should weigh is the religious sponsorship itself — whether the religious motivation (genealogy as theologically meaningful work in LDS doctrine) affects which records get prioritized, how data gets used, or how the organization evolves. Publicly, FamilySearch has maintained a clear free-to-all, religiously-neutral-service posture for decades.

## Caveats

- The religious affiliation is load-bearing for the funding model and for the culture. A reader considering working here should think clearly about whether that context fits them.
- This is infrastructure work, not frontier research. High impact, but slow and diffuse.
- Privacy and data-use debates will intensify as AI makes it easier to combine records; FamilySearch will have to navigate those carefully.
- The LDS Church itself is a large, hierarchical institution; the pace of change inside its services reflects that.
