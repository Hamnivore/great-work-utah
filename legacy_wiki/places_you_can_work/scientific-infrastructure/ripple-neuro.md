# Ripple Neuro

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Scientific infrastructure — Neural recording & stimulation instrumentation
**Also relevant to:** [Tools for thought](../tools-for-thought/), [Health & longevity](../health-and-longevity/)

**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT

**Website:** [rippleneuro.com](https://rippleneuro.com)

## Mission

Build the neural recording and stimulation hardware that research neuroscientists actually need — hundreds of channels, real-time, flexible, with the custom low-level engineering most labs cannot do themselves.

## Why it matters

Almost every major result in invasive neuroscience in the last decade — BCI decoding, neural prosthetics, motor cortex studies, closed-loop neuromodulation — depended on a recording system someone built. For a significant fraction of those labs, that system is a Grapevine, a Trek, or a Summit from Ripple. These are not consumer products and not clinical products; they are the instruments that make the science possible.

The counterfactual matters: without Ripple, the BCI and neural prosthetics fields would be measurably slower. Labs would be rolling their own electronics, burning grad-student years on hardware instead of on neuroscience, or limited by lower-channel commercial systems designed for simpler experiments. Ripple's 512-channel systems with custom DARPA/DoD-funded ASICs let a single experiment record from populations of neurons at scales that used to require multiple stacked rigs.

The newer Trek system (portable, palm-sized, wireless) pushes the ceiling further: it means BCI research can move out of the stationary rig setup and into naturalistic behavior. That unlocks experimental designs that were simply not possible before.

## The hard problem

Recording low-microvolt neural signals across hundreds of channels, in real time, while simultaneously stimulating other channels without the stimulation artifact swamping the recordings — that is an unsolved analog front-end problem at scale. Custom ASIC design is the bottleneck. So is fitting all of that into a wearable form factor (Trek) or a fully implantable form factor without compromising signal quality. Ripple has been grinding on these problems for 20 years; that longevity is itself the moat.

## Mechanism of impact

Foundational tool. Every paper enabled by a Ripple system is part of the impact footprint. Second-order: the neuromod programs funded under NIH HEAL aim at chronic pain, a condition affecting 50M+ Americans and one of the primary drivers of the opioid crisis. If implantable closed-loop neuromodulation works, Ripple is part of the instrumentation lineage that gets it there.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Analog and mixed-signal hardware engineers, ASIC designers, embedded firmware engineers, and biomedical engineers with a real interest in neuroscience. Small team (order of dozens, not hundreds), so individual leverage is very high. If you've wanted to design the instrument rather than just use it, this is that kind of place.

## Ownership

Private, founder/operator-controlled — no major VC rounds in public databases. The revenue model is selling instruments to research labs plus government-funded R&D contracts. That model aligns well with the mission: customers are scientists, and building better instruments makes them more productive. No obvious conflict between revenue and mission.

## Caveats

- This is an instrument company, not a frontier consumer BCI play — don't come expecting Blackrock-or-Neuralink-style visibility
- Market size is inherently bounded by the number of neuroscience labs doing invasive work
- Regulatory path for any clinical or implanted version of their systems would be long and capital-intensive; current business is research-grade, which is simpler but smaller
- Ownership is opaque in public databases; worth confirming before assuming mission autonomy
