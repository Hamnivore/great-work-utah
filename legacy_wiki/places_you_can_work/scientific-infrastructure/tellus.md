## Mission

Democratize air quality monitoring by building low-cost, high-accuracy sensor networks that deliver real-time pollution data to communities that can't afford regulatory-grade monitoring equipment.

## Why it matters

Traditional EPA regulatory-grade air quality monitors cost $100,000–$200,000 each and are sparsely deployed — a city might have 5–10 of them. As a result, air quality data has low spatial resolution: a monitor measuring "good" AQI three miles away tells you nothing about the air at a school or in a neighborhood near a highway or refinery. Low-income and minority communities — which tend to be closer to industrial sources — are the most underserved.

TELLUS built a sensor network starting in Salt Lake City's valley air basin, which has some of the worst wintertime PM2.5 pollution in the US (inversions trap vehicle exhaust and industrial emissions). The CoreDI platform uses low-cost sensors validated against collocated regulatory-grade monitors over 20+ years; the calibration methodology is the real product, not the sensor hardware. The AirU Pro (launched 2023) is the latest hardware iteration.

This matters because: (1) granular air quality data enables better regulatory enforcement and pollution-source attribution; (2) it lets residents make real-time behavior changes (keep windows closed, don't exercise outside); (3) the same infrastructure can support epidemiological research linking localized pollution to health outcomes. The gap between what regulators measure and what people actually breathe is enormous.

## The hard problem

Consumer-grade air quality sensors are cheap but inaccurate; they drift, respond differently to humidity and temperature, and can't be trusted without calibration. Getting low-cost sensor data to the accuracy level needed for scientific credibility and regulatory use requires careful co-deployment with reference instruments, statistical correction, and ongoing drift compensation. TELLUS built this pipeline over two decades of academic work before spinning out — the calibration rigor is the moat.

## Mechanism of impact

Infrastructure layer: TELLUS deploys and maintains sensor networks that governments, schools, health departments, and researchers subscribe to for air quality data. Each deployed network is a permanent improvement in the data available to that community. The impact compounds as the network grows: more sensors in more cities means better attribution, better research, and better policy.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Environmental engineers, sensor hardware engineers, data scientists comfortable with calibration statistics, and software engineers building real-time data pipelines and public dashboards. The culture is engineering-first with a public-health mission. Small team, high per-person leverage.

## Ownership

Private. Backed by NSF (grant funding since founding). Seeking Series A as of 2024–2025. University of Utah spinout; academic founding team. Mission autonomy high — public-interest orientation rather than VC-driven growth model.

## Why this is not ⭐

The core technology — low-cost, calibrated air quality sensing — is becoming less differentiated as sensor manufacturers improve out-of-the-box accuracy and as other well-funded companies (IQAir, PurpleAir, Clarity Movement, Aeroqual) attack the same market. TELLUS's Utah focus and 20-year calibration heritage give it credibility, but it is not yet the clear global category leader. S-tier if they win government contracts at scale or become the reference platform for regulatory-adjacent monitoring nationwide.

## Caveats / red flags

- Competing against PurpleAir (widely deployed, consumer brand), Clarity Movement (VC-backed, government-focused), and others with significant head starts in commercial markets
- Revenue and funding details not publicly disclosed
- Path from Utah-centric deployment to national/global scale is not yet demonstrated
- The "low-cost sensor" advantage narrows as sensor technology commoditizes
