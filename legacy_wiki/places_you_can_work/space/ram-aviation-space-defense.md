# RAM Aviation, Space & Defense

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Space — Propulsion valves & precision components
**Also relevant to:** [Defense & security](../defense-and-security/)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Established (50 years, ~300 employees, founder-family-held)
**HQ:** St. George, UT
**Founded:** 1975 (by Ray & Melzie Ganowsky)
**Website:** [ramasd.com](https://ramasd.com)

## Mission

Design and manufacture the precision propulsion valves and flow-control components that spacecraft, missiles, and military aircraft rely on for mission-critical fluid and gas control.

## Why it matters

RAM valves are on the SpaceX Dragon capsule that ferries crew and cargo to the ISS. When the ISS is decommissioned in 2031, the thrusters that safely deorbit it will be controlled by RAM components. RAM is Sierra Space's partner for in-space propulsion on Dream Chaser. These are not marketing claims — they are integrated parts in working flight hardware that is keeping astronauts alive today and will be involved in one of the defining space events of the decade.

Valves are the kind of component that almost no one thinks about and everything depends on. A single failed valve has killed missions (Mars Polar Lander, Apollo 13's oxygen tank stir, countless RFPs you never heard about). A company that has spent 50 years machining valves that don't fail in vacuum, at cryogenic temperatures, with hypergolic propellants, is one of a small global set that the space industry can't function without. RAM is one of those. The counterfactual argument is: if RAM's valves stopped working tomorrow, several active US human spaceflight programs would immediately have a problem.

## The hard problem

Aerospace-grade flow control has tolerances measured in microns, has to work across temperature ranges from near-absolute-zero to hundreds of °C, has to not leak measurable quantities of helium, and has to survive years in orbit without maintenance. The design-validate-qualify cycle for a new propulsion valve on a human-rated spacecraft is measured in years. The moat is the combination of machining precision, materials science, and hard-won qualification history with NASA, SpaceX, and DoD.

## Mechanism of impact

Indirect but load-bearing. RAM doesn't "change the world" on their own — but every NASA crewed mission, every commercial LEO program, every new spacecraft needs parts like RAM's. They are the sort of company that makes the space age *possible* in the same way precision bearing manufacturers made the industrial revolution possible.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Mechanical engineers with a serious interest in precision machining, materials science, and fluid/gas dynamics. QA engineers willing to spend careers at the intersection of hardware testing and regulatory qualification. Machinists with the mindset of craftsmen. This is a career where you don't ship code; you ship components that go on spacecraft and last 20 years.

## Ownership

Private, founder-family owned. 50 years of continuous ownership. Strong mission autonomy by construction — no VC, no exit pressure. The main risk is succession: whether the next generation maintains the same commitment to the hard, slow, engineering-first culture.

## Why this is not ⭐ (yet)

Strong candidate, but not marked S-tier because the work is supplier-role rather than outcome-defining. RAM makes world-changing things *possible*; they are not themselves the tip of the spear. This is the same reason a carbon fiber supplier isn't in the headline of every aircraft news story. If you want to be directly adjacent to spaceflight without moving to Florida or Texas, RAM is one of a handful of places in the Mountain West where that's possible.

## Caveats

- Supplier economics: margins depend on holding qualification lock-in against primes that would prefer multiple sources
- ISS decommissioning in 2031 is a specific customer-driven milestone, not an eternal revenue line
- The succession question — founder-family businesses can lose focus across generations
- Technical leverage of a single individual is lower than at a startup; this is a mature machine-shop-plus-engineering operation
