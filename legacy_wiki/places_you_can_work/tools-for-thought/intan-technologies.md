# Intan Technologies

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Tools for thought — Neural recording and stimulation integrated circuits
**Also relevant to:** [Scientific infrastructure](../scientific-infrastructure/)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Established, widely-deployed research ICs
**HQ:** Los Angeles, CA (founder and U of U Neural Engineering alumnus Reid Harrison)
**Founded:** 2010
**Website:** [intantech.com](https://intantech.com)

## Mission

Make high-channel-count, low-noise, low-power integrated circuits for neural recording and stimulation available off-the-shelf — so any neuroscience lab can record from hundreds of neurons without building custom front-end electronics.

## Why it matters

Historically, neural recording was bottlenecked by front-end electronics. A researcher who wanted to record 100+ channels had to build custom amplifier boards, deal with noise and crosstalk, and spend years on hardware rather than science. Intan Technologies changed that: their RHD and RHS series chips give researchers 16, 32, 64, or 128 channels of simultaneous amplification, filtering, and ADC conversion in a single chip, at noise floors competitive with custom designs.

These chips are the workhorse of modern systems neuroscience. They are inside the front ends of Open Ephys (open-source recording systems), Ripple Neuro boxes, many academic custom rigs, and numerous commercial neural interface companies. Anyone who has read a neuroscience paper in the last decade with multi-channel recording has probably been reading data that came through an Intan chip.

On the wiki's inclusion lens: Intan is the kind of component-level company whose impact is felt through everyone else's work. A small company (by headcount — ~10s of employees), high-leverage, and genuinely foundational to the field.

Note: listed here despite the LA address because the founder is a U of U Neural Engineering alumnus and the company is part of the Utah Neural Engineering industry list. The Utah lens is the cluster the founder is an alum of, not current HQ. This is a judgment call on inclusion.

## The hard problem

Low-noise, low-power amplification of microvolt-scale neural signals in the presence of movement artifact and ambient electrical noise — and doing this simultaneously across dozens to hundreds of channels on a single chip without crosstalk — is a hard analog IC design problem. Stimulation adds further complication: switching between record and stimulate on the same electrode requires careful switch design and artifact suppression. Intan has iterated on these problems for 15+ years.

## Mechanism of impact

Supply chain / infrastructure. Every paper, BCI system, or neural engineering startup using an Intan chip is a downstream impact. Arguably one of the single highest-leverage component companies in systems neuroscience.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Analog and mixed-signal IC designers, firmware engineers, biomedical electronics specialists. Very small team; individual leverage is extremely high. This is niche, specialized work — the right fit for an analog chip designer who wants to work on neuroscience infrastructure.

## Ownership

Private. Small, founder-led (Reid Harrison). Mission autonomy is very high.

## Why this is not ⭐

Small, component-level company — its impact is diffused through everyone else's products. The wiki's ⭐ goes to companies that are the named category leader in a consequential area. Intan earns an honest mention as one of the most quietly important companies in neuroscience, but the visible category ownership goes to the systems companies (Blackrock, Neuropixels/IMEC, etc.) that integrate Intan-type chips.

## Caveats

- Component company; business success tied to research funding cycles
- Competition at the high end from Neuropixels (IMEC) and other integrated platforms
- Very small company; not a large hiring base
- Founder-key-person concentration
