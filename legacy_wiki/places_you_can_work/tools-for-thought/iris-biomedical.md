# Iris Biomedical

**Tier:** B — real with significant caveats; outclassed, mid-tier, or commoditizing
**Domain:** Tools for thought — Neural interface design and manufacturing services
**Also relevant to:** [Health & longevity](../health-and-longevity/) (implantable medical devices)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Established, contract design/manufacturing
**HQ:** Salt Lake City, UT
**Founded:** ~2014 (U of U Neural Engineering alumni)
**Website:** [irisbiomed.com](https://www.irisbiomed.com)

## Mission

Design, prototype, and manufacture custom neural interface hardware — implantable electrode arrays, flexible substrates, hermetic packages, and connectors — for academic and commercial neural engineering programs that don't have in-house manufacturing capability.

## Why it matters

Every BCI company and neural engineering academic lab eventually hits the same bottleneck: neural interface hardware is not a commodity. Electrode arrays need specific geometries for specific applications, flexible substrates need to be chemically compatible with neural tissue, and hermetic packaging is required for chronic implantation. Most of the world's neural interface prototypes are made in a handful of specialized shops, and Iris Biomedical is one of them. They are a key supplier to the academic BCI community and to early-stage neurotech companies that aren't yet vertically integrated.

This is supply-chain work, not headline-grabbing neurotech. But it's the kind of supply chain that makes everything else possible — if Iris didn't exist, several early-stage BCI companies and academic labs would have to build internal fabs, slowing everything down.

Utah is unusually concentrated in this kind of neural interface expertise because of the University of Utah's Center for Neural Interfaces and the Utah Slanted Electrode Array legacy. Iris, Intan, Ripple, and Blackrock together form one of the densest BCI-hardware ecosystems in the world.

## The hard problem

Implantable neural interfaces must survive years inside biological tissue without encapsulation failure, corrosion, or delamination. The materials science (platinum-iridium electrodes, polyimide or parylene substrates, biocompatible encapsulation) is specialized. Manufacturing at small batch sizes with medical-device quality control (ISO 13485 QMS) requires careful process engineering. And each customer wants different geometries, so the shop has to stay agile across dozens of one-off designs per year.

## Mechanism of impact

Supply chain. Every academic BCI paper or early-stage neural-interface startup that depended on Iris-built hardware is a line of indirect impact. Direct impact is on the customers who actually implant the hardware — clinical trial patients, research animals, and eventually commercial BCI users.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Biomedical engineers, microfabrication engineers, hermetic packaging specialists, quality engineers with medical-device experience. Small, specialized team; individual leverage is high. Good fit for someone with a biomedical engineering or MEMS background who wants to work on neural interface hardware specifically.

## Ownership

Private. Small, employee-owned or founder-owned. Mission autonomy is high.

## Why this is not ⭐

Supply chain / contract manufacturing, not a category-defining neurotech company. Important and hard to replace, but the visible impact goes to the customers (Blackrock, academic labs, BCI startups) rather than to Iris directly. S-tier here is reserved for companies on the clinical or research deployment frontier; Iris is the shop that makes the frontier possible.

## Caveats

- Contract manufacturing depends on customer demand cycles
- Competing with other neural-interface shops (Microprobes, Plexon, custom university fabs)
- Limited public information on deal size or customer mix
- Medical device QMS overhead is expensive for small companies
