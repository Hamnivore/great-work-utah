# Autonomous Solutions, Inc. (ASI)

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Transportation — Off-road vehicle autonomy / industrial fleet automation
**Also relevant to:** [Machine intelligence](../machine-intelligence/), [Food & agriculture](../food-and-agriculture/)
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Established (25 years, founded 2000, expanding fast — SoftBank-backed construction unit, new Lehi office 2025)
**HQ:** Mendon, UT (plus Lehi, UT office since June 2025)
**Founded:** 2000 (Utah State University spinout)
**Website:** [asirobots.com](https://asirobots.com)

## Mission

Automate off-road industrial vehicles — tractors, haul trucks, dozers, loaders, landscaping equipment — through an OEM-agnostic software platform (Mobius) that runs on any manufacturer's hardware.

## Why it matters

Off-road autonomy is a much bigger, much less-covered economic story than passenger-car autonomy. Mining operations, large-scale agriculture, construction sites, and freight yards all depend on human operators driving heavy equipment in dangerous, repetitive, labor-short conditions. ASI has been quietly automating these vehicles for 25 years and has now automated **100+ vehicle models** across mining, construction, agriculture, and logistics using their Mobius platform.

The architectural bet is interesting and non-obvious. John Deere, Caterpillar, Komatsu, and the other major OEMs are building their own closed autonomous systems. ASI is doing the opposite: a platform layer that retrofits onto any OEM's vehicle. If you believe end customers don't want to be locked into one vehicle vendor for their fleet, ASI's approach wins. If you believe OEMs will force bundled full-stack solutions, ASI loses. Both bets are reasonable; the market is large enough that both could be right in different segments.

The SoftBank-backed construction automation launch in 2024 is a real signal. SoftBank is a serious AV/robotics investor, and construction automation is a much newer market than agricultural or mining autonomy. The mining business was sold to Epiroc years ago — that track record of actually exiting a business unit at scale is itself a data point that ASI is a real company, not a perpetual research project.

## The hard problem

Off-road autonomy has some fundamental differences from on-road autonomy that make it easier in some ways (no pedestrians, no highway speeds, controlled environments) and harder in others (no lane markings, no HD maps, extreme weather, rough terrain, dirt and dust that degrade sensors, radically different vehicle dynamics per machine). An OEM-agnostic platform makes this harder still: you have to abstract over 100+ control system APIs, suspension geometries, actuator latencies. The Mobius software architecture reflects 25 years of that integration work.

## Mechanism of impact

Direct deployment, at industrial scale. Every mine, farm, or construction site using Mobius is an economic impact footprint. Second-order: if OEM-agnostic fleet automation becomes the standard pattern for industrial vehicles (analogous to how OS-agnostic software beat vertically-integrated machines in computing), ASI's architecture could shape the industry.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Robotics engineers (perception, control, planning), fleet-orchestration software engineers, and vehicle systems engineers who want to work on real industrial deployment rather than slideware. Strong fit for someone who wants California-AV-caliber autonomy problems without California cost of living. Testing happens at real mines, farms, and construction sites — not simulation.

## Ownership

Private. Backed in part by SoftBank (for the construction unit). Ownership concentration details are not fully public. The SoftBank involvement is worth noting for anyone thinking about mission autonomy — SoftBank is a strategic rather than a passive investor, and past SoftBank-backed autonomy plays have had mixed outcomes.

## Why this is not ⭐

ASI has been successful for 25 years but has not had the kind of breakout moment that would make it a household name — even within the AV community. They are OEM-agnostic, which is architecturally elegant but means they compete with better-funded and more vertically-integrated OEMs on every deal. 100+ vehicle models automated is a genuinely strong number, but the top-of-funnel growth rate is unclear from public sources. A reader should see them as a solid, durable, important company in their niche rather than an obvious Big Company In The Making.

## Caveats

- OEM-agnostic positioning means no single customer relationship is deep; customer concentration risk is real
- Sold their mining business unit to Epiroc in 2021, which is a perfectly reasonable exit but also a signal that scaling a vertically-integrated mining autonomy unit inside ASI wasn't working
- 25 years of operating history without a single "Utah's hometown AV story" in the national press suggests marketing/visibility challenges
- Competing with John Deere, Caterpillar, Komatsu, and Trimble — these are enormous, well-capitalized opponents
- SoftBank strategic involvement is a double-edged sword; track record of SoftBank portfolio companies is volatile
