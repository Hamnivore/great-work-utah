# Electric Power Systems (EPS)

**Tier:** A — strong; not globally category-defining but excellent
**Domain:** Transportation — Electric aviation propulsion
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Private, growth stage ($69M state incentive package)
**HQ:** North Logan, UT
**Founded:** 2016
**Website:** [epsenergy.com](https://epsenergy.com)

## Mission

Build the power systems that make electric aviation certifiable and commercially viable.

## Why it matters

Electric aviation has an energy density problem: batteries are too heavy and too limited in range for most flight applications. EPS is one of the few companies building aviation-grade electric propulsion systems — not for hobbyist drones, but for certified aircraft. Their EPiC battery systems power NASA's X-57 Maxwell (the first all-electric X-plane), Bell's Nexus, Boeing demonstrators, SkyDrive, Ampaire, and others. Without companies like EPS solving the propulsion stack, electric aviation remains permanently grounded in demonstration projects. Their EPiC 2.0 system delivers 90+ minutes of flight time and is targeting FAA certification — the hardest gate in the industry.

The 2034 Winter Olympics in Utah is a concrete near-term forcing function: the state is actively standing up eVTOL infrastructure, and EPS is a core propulsion supplier in that ecosystem.

## The hard problem

Aviation certification is not a software problem — you cannot iterate your way through it with fast releases. The hard challenge is designing a battery system that meets the FAA's rigorous thermal runaway containment, cycle life, and failure mode requirements while also being light enough to fly. EPS has passed major NASA thermal safety milestones and is on the FAA certification path, which few companies have navigated. Energy density remains the underlying physics constraint no one has fully solved yet.

## Mechanism of impact

Infrastructure layer for electric aviation: EPS's batteries and propulsion systems go into other companies' aircraft. If they succeed at certification, they unlock dozens of eVTOL and electric aircraft programs that are currently blocked waiting for certifiable power systems.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Aerospace engineers, battery electrochemists, power electronics engineers, and certification specialists. People who are comfortable with long regulatory timelines and care about the work being real — there's no shortcut through FAA certification. Utah-based, smaller team than coastal counterparts, which means individual contribution is highly visible.

## Ownership

Private. No disclosed VC rounds — growth appears to be primarily through customer contracts and state incentives ($69M package). This funding profile suggests the company is either bootstrapped or backed by strategic investors, both of which imply more mission autonomy than typical VC-backed startups. Worth verifying.

## Caveats

- Energy density improvements are incremental — 90 minutes is meaningful but long-haul electric aviation is still decades away
- FAA certification timelines are notoriously unpredictable; being on the path is not the same as completing it
- Competition from better-funded coastal players (ZeroAvia, magniX, Ampaire) is real
- Utah State University adjacent but no deep research affiliation found — may be more engineering execution than fundamental research
