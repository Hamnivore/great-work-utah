# WAVE (Wireless Advanced Vehicle Electrification)

**Tier:** B — proven, operational, fills a real logistics gap in fleet electrification
**Domain:** Transportation — Wireless inductive charging for commercial EV fleets
**Type:** Company (private)
**Stage:** Commercial, operational — transit, port, and logistics deployments
**HQ:** Logan, UT
**Founded:** 2011 (USU spinout from the Energy Dynamics Laboratory)
**Website:** [wavecharging.com](https://wavecharging.com)

## Mission

Make fleet electrification operationally seamless by replacing plug-in charging with inductive wireless charging embedded in the roadway.

## Why it matters

The main barrier to electrifying bus fleets, port vehicles, and logistics trucks is not range or battery cost — it is charging logistics. A transit bus that has to park in a specific bay, wait for a driver to plug and unplug, and sit idle during long charge cycles imposes schedule constraints that operators resist. WAVE's approach embeds 125–500kW+ wireless charging pads under the roadway at layover points, charging the vehicle automatically whenever it stops — no driver action required, no cables, no charging depot bottleneck.

The counterfactual: without seamless charging, fleet operators defer electrification or adopt it partially. Transit agencies have been slow to electrify despite climate pressure partly because of operational complexity. WAVE reduces that complexity to near-zero.

## The hard problem

Transferring 125–500kW+ of power across a 6–10 inch air gap at high efficiency, while meeting roadway durability standards, FCC emissions limits, and safety requirements for human proximity. USU's Energy Dynamics Laboratory developed the foundational power electronics. Achieving 90%+ transfer efficiency at 50kW was considered a significant technical milestone when WAVE launched the first commercial deployment (University of Utah campus bus, 2013). Commercial deployments now operate at higher power levels.

## Mechanism of impact

Infrastructure layer: WAVE's charging pads are installed in the roadway; the fleet operator deploys compatible electric buses. Every new bus compatible with WAVE's standard becomes part of a wireless-charging network rather than a plug-in dependency. The transit, port, and logistics markets represent a significant portion of commercial vehicle miles traveled.

## What kind of contributor thrives here

Power electronics engineers, embedded systems engineers, and RF/EMC engineers. Also infrastructure project managers and fleet electrification specialists. USU connection gives it a strong engineering culture; Logan, UT base means lower cost of living than coastal competitors. Individual leverage is real in a company of this size.

## Ownership

Private. USU spinout with government funding (FTA grants, DOE contracts) in early years alongside customer revenue. No disclosed VC rounds in public databases. Revenue model is infrastructure sales (charging pads + installation) and software/service — aligns with mission of deploying working systems rather than maximizing valuation.

## Caveats / red flags

- Wireless charging is inherently less efficient than wired; the 6–10% efficiency loss at high power levels has real energy cost at fleet scale
- Competing technologies (pantograph overhead charging, megawatt-class plug-in systems like the SAE MCS standard for trucks) are also improving and may close the convenience gap
- WAVE's market is transit and fleets — not personal EVs — which limits total addressable market compared to companies targeting passenger cars
- Market traction data (how many buses, how many routes) is not publicly detailed; hard to assess actual deployment scale vs. pilot deployments
- Broader EV fleet charging market is attracting well-capitalized entrants; WAVE's USU-origin engineering advantage may not persist
