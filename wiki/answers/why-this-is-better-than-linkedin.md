## Short Answer

LinkedIn is built for professional identity at scale. Great Work is built for **rare-fit discovery with evidence** in Utah's innovation ecosystem. The wiki helps a founder, researcher, or operator answer "why this person, project, or program?" — with cited context — instead of sorting through title noise.

## Why

The product problem is not missing people or missing resources. It is **findability with judgment**.

LinkedIn optimizes for:

- broad professional graphs
- recruiter volume
- title and keyword matching
- self-reported profiles with weak verification context

That is fine for many careers. It is a poor fit when:

- a founder needs three senior engineers who specifically want hard-tech work in Utah and can be evaluated without a screening call
- a PhD candidate needs to see how Utah research became real companies and which programs help first-time commercialization
- an operator wants a mission, not another inbox full of recruiter spam
- a veteran manufacturer in Ogden or a rural woman founder in Washington County needs practical local support, not a feed of SaaS thought leadership

Great Work is closer to a **living map with taste** than a job board. Entity pages describe. Guides, matches, and answers recommend — with audience and criteria stated up front.

## Caveats

- Great Work is not trying to replace LinkedIn for general career management, personal branding, or global recruiting at scale.
- The wiki is early. Some resource pages are still stubs imported from Startup State data and need provider verification before they should drive strong live recommendations.
- No public page should imply private willingness, availability, endorsement, or a relationship unless it is sourced.
- The product's hiring ambition — helping founders filter deeply enough to skip bad screening calls — depends on richer people pages and usage signal over time. The wiki is the foundation, not the finished matching engine.
