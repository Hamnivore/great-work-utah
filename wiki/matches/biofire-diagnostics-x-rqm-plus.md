## Recommendation

A Utah founder building an in vitro diagnostic product — multiplex PCR panels, syndromic testing systems, cartridge-based assays, or any cleared consumable that identifies pathogens or biomarkers — should understand the regulatory and quality journey that BioFire Diagnostics has completed at scale. RQM+ is a medtech CRO that explicitly covers that exact journey: IVD regulatory submissions, De Novo and 510(k) pathways, EU IVDR compliance, quality systems, clinical study design for diagnostics, and reimbursement strategy. The pairing is rated Strong not because BioFire itself is likely to engage RQM+ (it operates as a mature bioMérieux subsidiary with internal regulatory infrastructure), but because BioFire's history is the clearest model of what IVD success looks like from Utah, and RQM+'s stated services map almost point-for-point onto what a founder trying to replicate that model needs. The match is strongest for a founder who has a defined product concept, an intended-use statement, and a regulatory pathway hypothesis and wants expert hands-on support to turn that hypothesis into a submission-ready dossier.

## Why This Match Makes Sense

BioFire's product record illustrates the specific FDA pathways that an IVD company working in rapid multiplex diagnostics must navigate. The Global Fever Panel was cleared through the De Novo pathway (reference DEN200043), which is the correct route for a novel IVD without a predicate — a situation many emerging diagnostic startups face when their assay architecture is genuinely new. Earlier FilmArray panels were cleared via 510(k) using established predicates as the platform matured and the predicate landscape developed. EU deployment adds CE-IVD marking under the older IVDD regime and, for newer submissions, EU IVDR compliance — a substantially more demanding framework that many US-only IVD companies underestimate. Each of those regulatory tracks requires different strategy, different evidence packages, and different quality-system documentation.

RQM+ publicly identifies in vitro diagnostics as a named service category on its official site, alongside medical devices, software-enabled products, combination products, and companion diagnostics. Its solutions listing covers regulatory and quality, clinical trials, lab and material science, and reimbursement — which maps closely onto the four major work streams any IVD company must manage from assay concept to commercial launch. Its engagement models (outsourcing, consulting, staff augmentation) suggest it can serve both early-stage companies that need strategic guidance before they have a regulatory team and later-stage companies that need execution capacity to complete a submission or stand up a QMS.

The technical vocabulary alignment is strong. A founder asking RQM+ about De Novo classification, 510(k) predicate selection, CLIA categorization, clinical study design for a molecular diagnostic, ISO 13485 QMS implementation for a diagnostic consumable, or companion diagnostic regulatory strategy is asking questions that are native to RQM+'s stated scope, not edge cases that require translation.

## What BioFire Diagnostics Needs

BioFire as a fully integrated bioMérieux subsidiary almost certainly has mature internal regulatory, quality, and clinical functions. The needs assessment for this match is therefore not about BioFire's own resource gaps but about what BioFire's history reveals — and who in Utah is actually positioned to use that information.

The BioFire model required sustained regulatory investment across several distinct work streams: assay design controls from the earliest development stages, clinical study design to generate the analytical and clinical performance data FDA requires for IVD clearance, QMS compliance maintained across both R&D and manufacturing, CE-IVD technical documentation for European market access, and post-market surveillance obligations that grow with each cleared product and each territory of deployment. For a founder building a new IVD company from the ground up in Utah, replicating even a fraction of that infrastructure from scratch is the most predictable source of delay and cost overrun.

BioFire-adjacent founders — researchers who worked at BioFire and are spinning out a related assay concept, founders who want to build on the FilmArray pattern for a different disease area, or diagnostic entrepreneurs studying the De Novo pathway that BioFire used for the Global Fever Panel — face versions of those same challenges without BioFire's accumulated internal expertise. They need regulatory strategy advice from specialists who have done IVD submissions repeatedly, not once or twice, and who can apply that experience to the founder's specific product architecture, intended use, and target population.

## What RQM+ Appears To Offer

Based on its official public site, RQM+ is a full-service medtech CRO covering the concept-to-post-market lifecycle. Its regulatory and quality services appear to include FDA submission strategy and preparation (510(k), De Novo, PMA), IDE applications for investigational device studies, EU MDR and EU IVDR compliance and technical documentation, ISO 13485 quality-management-system design and audit support, and design controls implementation.

Its clinical offerings cover clinical study design and management for medical devices and diagnostics, which for an IVD includes clinical performance study design, analytical validation protocols, clinical site management, and statistical analysis plans built around FDA's IVD performance guidance documents.

Its lab and material-science capabilities — the less commonly cited part of its service menu — are relevant to IVD founders because a diagnostic consumable like a PCR pouch or a lateral-flow cartridge involves material selections, biocompatibility considerations, and physical testing that go beyond the assay chemistry. A consumable that fails a shelf-life study or a leachables assessment late in development can set a submission back by months.

Reimbursement support matters for IVD founders because clearance and payment are separate regulatory processes. A PCR panel that earns FDA clearance still needs a CPT code, payer coverage, and clinical utility evidence to generate revenue in the US healthcare system. RQM+'s public site lists reimbursement as a named service, which makes it more useful to founders thinking ahead to commercialization than a regulatory-only firm.

Its engagement model (outsourcing, consulting, staff augmentation) also means a seed-stage company does not need to commit to a full outsourcing contract; a scoped advisory engagement around a specific question — regulatory pathway selection, QMS gap analysis, or clinical study design review — should be available.

## Risks and Unknowns

The most significant risk in framing this match is audience mismatch. BioFire itself is not a startup and is not the audience for this recommendation. A reader who misreads the match as suggesting BioFire should contact RQM+ for regulatory support would be misreading both organizations. The recommendation is specifically for founders who are studying BioFire as a model, not for BioFire's own organization.

RQM+ is headquartered in Cary, North Carolina (US headquarters) and Altrincham, United Kingdom (European headquarters) based on its official site. There is no public evidence of a Utah office or an established relationship with Utah-based founders or companies. The recommendation is a geographic extrapolation — a national specialist that is relevant to Utah work — not evidence of a local engagement history.

Engagement cost and minimum deal size are not stated on RQM+'s public site. A seed-stage diagnostics company with limited runway may find a national medtech CRO's commercial terms out of range for a first regulatory engagement. Founders at very early stages may need lower-cost regulatory education (university tech-transfer support, SCORE mentorship with a regulatory background, or BioUtah programs) before they are ready for a paid CRO engagement.

It is also unknown which specific RQM+ consultants have IVD or syndromic-diagnostics experience, and whether the firm has prior work in multiplex PCR or cartridge-based diagnostic consumables specifically. The public site describes capabilities at the service-category level, not at the disease-area or technology-platform level.

## Suggested Next Step

A Utah founder building an IVD product who wants to evaluate RQM+ should begin by articulating three things in writing before any first call: (1) the intended use statement — what the device detects, in what specimen type, in what clinical setting, for what patient population; (2) a hypothesis about device classification and regulatory pathway (510(k), De Novo, or PMA, and why); and (3) the specific question they need answered — regulatory pathway validation, QMS gap analysis, clinical study design, EU IVDR applicability, or reimbursement strategy.

Arriving with that preparation turns a first call from a general scoping conversation into a substantive exchange where RQM+ can demonstrate whether its team has the relevant IVD experience. It also surfaces quickly whether the firm's engagement model fits the company's budget and stage.

If the founder is in an earlier stage — still defining the assay concept, the target population, or the intended use — a better first step may be engagement with the University of Utah's technology commercialization office, a BioUtah regulatory committee, or a regulatory mentor through a medtech accelerator program. Those interactions can produce the clarity needed to make a first CRO engagement productive rather than premature.
