## Recommendation

Use Josh James and Domo together as the clearest Utah example of a serial enterprise-SaaS founder who built a second public company in the same state — a BI and data-integration platform now pivoting into agentic AI. The match is strongest for engineers and operators deciding whether applied data infrastructure in a public-company environment suits their career goals, and for founders studying what it means to build a Utah SaaS company from founding through two NASDAQ listings.

## Why This Match Makes Sense

James co-founded Omniture in 1996 and sold it to Adobe for $1.8 billion in 2009 — the defining Utah tech exit of its era. Rather than relocating or retiring, he founded Domo in 2010 in American Fork, applied the same enterprise-software thesis to unified business data, and took Domo public on NASDAQ in 2018 (ticker: DOMO). That back-to-back founder arc, both executed in Utah Valley, is the most direct evidence the wiki has for what staying-and-building looks like across company generations.

Domo's current product direction — agentic AI layered on a unified business data platform — creates a different kind of demand than the dashboards-for-executives story. The company now needs engineers who can ground LLM queries in accurate, multi-source business data without hallucination, which is a hard engineering problem with high tolerance for deep technical work.

The person-page and venture-page reinforce each other: James's impact in the wiki is partly the Omniture exit and partly the downstream operator community it seeded; Domo is the ongoing existence proof that Utah can sustain a public cloud-software company through the agentic-AI transition.

## What Josh James Brings

- Serial enterprise-SaaS founder with two Utah IPOs — the most documented "build here, exit here, build again" arc on the Wasatch Front.
- Operator experience navigating data-product competition across HMM, pre-cloud SaaS, and post-LLM BI waves.
- A talent-network effect: Omniture's 2009 exit trained a generation of Utah SaaS operators who now seed other companies; Domo draws on that same network.
- Angel-investor presence in the local ecosystem (Xandem and others) signals continued engagement beyond leading Domo.

## What Domo Needs

Domo's venture page identifies likely needs in data engineering, ML engineering for LLM-grounding pipelines, frontend engineering for analytics UI, and product management with BI domain expertise. The agentic-AI pivot adds constrained generation, multi-source grounding, and evaluation tooling as hard requirements. For talent matching, Domo is a mid-market public-company environment with real users and real quarterly pressure — a credible fit for engineers who want applied LLM work in a shipped product with a decade of production data-infrastructure experience behind it.

## Risks and Unknowns

- Domo competes with Snowflake, Databricks, Tableau/Salesforce, Power BI, Looker, ThoughtSpot, and Sigma from adjacent angles. The extent to which the agentic-AI repositioning translates into durable customer adoption is not established in the wiki.
- Public small-cap stock volatility and quarterly pressure may limit mission autonomy for engineering teams. This is a different operating context than a pre-IPO startup.
- James's current day-to-day product and company-strategy focus is not confirmed in public wiki sources beyond general CEO leadership.
- Domo's history includes a period of significant cash burn and financial pressure post-IPO; anyone making employment or investment decisions should verify current financial position from public filings.

## Suggested Next Step

An engineer or operator weighing a Domo role should read James's profile for the founder-lineage context, then read Domo's venture page for the current product direction and open questions around the agentic-AI thesis. The relevant due diligence is whether the data-grounding technical challenges align with their specific skills and whether the public-company operating environment fits their career stage.
