## Recommendation

Use Ryan Smith and Qualtrics together as the single most prominent example of a Utah enterprise software company built to global scale while remaining rooted in the state — and the founder's deliberate choice to keep it there through an $8 billion acquisition, a NASDAQ IPO, and a $12.5 billion take-private. The match is strongest for Utah founders studying what it looks like when a Utah-built company reaches the largest exit the state has produced, and for ecosystem researchers trying to understand how the Qualtrics precedent reshaped Silicon Slopes.

## Why This Match Makes Sense

Smith co-founded Qualtrics in 2002 in Provo with his father, brother, and Stuart Orgill. The company grew into the largest software company built in Utah, was acquired by SAP for $8 billion in 2018, returned as an independent public company on NASDAQ in 2021 (ticker: XM), and was taken private again by Silver Lake and CPPIB for approximately $12.5 billion in 2023. Smith served as CEO for most of this arc.

The person-page and venture-page together tell a compound story: Qualtrics is not just a company that grew large — it is a company that stayed in Utah when relocating would have been easy, and whose founder made that choice publicly and durably. The venture page frames Qualtrics as the anchor of Silicon Slopes; Smith's profile explains why. His civic investment — owning the Utah Jazz, speaking publicly about Utah's competitive future — makes the match more than a founder-product story. It is a founder-ecosystem story.

Qualtrics also matters on the applied-AI angle. The company sits on one of the largest databases of human experience feedback in the world, and its XM/os platform applies LLMs to unstructured feedback at enterprise distribution scale. That makes the venture page live, not merely historical: there is active ML and applied-AI work happening inside a Utah-headquartered company with Fortune 500 reach.

## Risks and Unknowns

- Smith's primary role today appears to be Utah Jazz ownership and civic leadership, not day-to-day Qualtrics operations. He is not a publicly accessible mentor or advisor in the typical startup sense.
- Silver Lake private-equity ownership since 2023 means operating cadence, hiring, and product investment priorities may have shifted from the public-company era. Verify current state from public reporting before citing Qualtrics as a model for cultural or operational norms.
- Qualtrics's AI feature claims — summarization accuracy, churn prediction, cost per inference — are based primarily on official marketing materials. Independent verification is needed before repeating specific performance numbers.
- Scott Smith's current role and public visibility are less documented than Ryan Smith's; the wiki does not have a separate people page for him; treat Qualtrics as a multi-founder company rather than a single-founder story.
- The Utah headquarters decision was made under specific conditions (SAP acquisition with explicit cultural commitments); different founders should not assume the same path is available to them without comparable leverage.

## Suggested Next Step

A Utah enterprise-software founder should read Smith's profile alongside Qualtrics's venture page to understand the full arc — founding in Provo, scale-up, global exit, public market, PE take-private — and specifically the rationale for staying headquartered in Utah at each stage.
