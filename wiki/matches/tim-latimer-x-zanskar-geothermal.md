## Recommendation

Zanskar Geothermal should treat Tim Latimer as a high-value peer-learning and ecosystem reference for the commercial geothermal path, not as a generic advisor. Latimer's public work at Fervo Energy centers on turning enhanced geothermal systems into financeable field infrastructure at Cape Station in Beaver County. Zanskar is attacking the upstream exploration bottleneck: finding hidden geothermal systems with machine learning and geoscience. The two companies sit in adjacent parts of the same Utah geothermal stack, so a carefully scoped conversation could help Zanskar test assumptions about what downstream developers, project financiers, and power buyers need from exploration intelligence.

## Why This Match Makes Sense

The wiki frames Fervo as a developer repurposing oil-and-gas drilling, reservoir engineering, and distributed sensing for firm geothermal power. Tim Latimer is the person-level face of that operating thesis: a founder-CEO with energy-engineering training, prior oil-and-gas experience, and public responsibility for Fervo's Cape Station project.

Zanskar's page frames the company as the local AI/geoscience counterpart: a Salt Lake City team trying to make geothermal prospecting more repeatable by combining seismic, geophysical, geologic, and historical drilling data. Its open commercial question is whether the business should license exploration technology, co-develop projects, or operate sites. Latimer is useful precisely because Fervo sits on the other side of that question: a project developer has to decide which exploration signals are bankable enough to justify drilling, land work, interconnection, and capital formation.

Utah FORGE strengthens the connection without implying a private relationship. It is the public field-learning layer near Milford, while Fervo's Cape Station and Zanskar's exploration work are commercial expressions of the same broader question: can enhanced geothermal become repeatable infrastructure rather than a geology lottery?

## What Zanskar Needs

Based on the existing Zanskar page, likely needs include:

- Feedback from geothermal developers on which exploration outputs change drill/no-drill decisions.
- A clearer commercialization route: software licensing, project co-development, owned sites, or a hybrid.
- Project-finance and power-market intuition about what evidence investors need before underwriting geothermal assets.
- Operator perspective on field validation, permitting, land access, drilling campaigns, and utility interconnection.
- Credible Utah-cluster context when explaining why exploration AI matters to non-software climate investors.

## What Tim Latimer Appears To Offer

Based on his person page and the Fervo page, Latimer appears to offer:

- Experience translating oil-and-gas drilling and completion methods into an enhanced-geothermal company.
- A founder-CEO view of Cape Station, a large Utah EGS project with public-market and project-finance implications.
- A practical understanding of power-purchase agreements, construction sequencing, and field operations for geothermal infrastructure.
- A strong case study for explaining why Utah's geothermal cluster matters: FORGE research infrastructure, Beaver County deployment, and private venture formation.

## Risks and Unknowns

- Fervo and Zanskar may be collaborators, customers, competitors, or simply adjacent companies depending on Zanskar's business model. Any conversation should avoid proprietary exploration data, project pipeline details, pricing, customer terms, and competitively sensitive strategy.
- Latimer is Houston-based through Fervo, while Zanskar is Salt Lake City-based. The Utah overlap is meaningful but project-centered rather than a guarantee of local availability.
- This page does not claim an existing relationship, endorsement, advisory role, customer relationship, or investment interest between Latimer, Fervo, and Zanskar.
- Zanskar's strongest immediate needs may be technical geoscience validation or capital, not CEO peer advice. The match is strongest for commercialization and field-execution questions.

## Suggested Next Step

A Utah geothermal connector could arrange a narrow, non-confidential founder conversation around one question: "What would make exploration intelligence bankable for an EGS developer before the first expensive drill decision?" That framing keeps the discussion useful without requiring either side to disclose proprietary project data.
