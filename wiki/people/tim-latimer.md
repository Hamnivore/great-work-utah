## Summary

Tim Latimer is a co-founder and CEO of [Fervo Energy](../ventures/fervo-energy.md), a Houston-based company developing enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) using horizontal drilling and completion techniques borrowed from the oil and gas industry. He holds a PhD in earth sciences from Stanford and spent time at BHP Billiton before founding Fervo in 2017.

His core insight was that the unconventional-drilling revolution — the horizontal wells and hydraulic fracturing behind the U.S. shale boom — could be redirected at hot rock to produce clean, firm electricity. That thesis underpins Fervo's Cape Station project in Beaver County, Utah, a 400 MW EGS project and one of the largest of its kind attempted to date.

## Impact

Fervo Energy's Cape Station, located in southwest Utah near the federally funded Utah FORGE research site at the University of Utah, is among the highest-profile climate infrastructure projects in the state. If it reaches full production, it would add 400 MW of firm, round-the-clock carbon-free power to the western grid — a profile that solar and wind alone cannot match without large-scale storage.

More broadly, Latimer's career has helped reframe EGS as an engineering challenge tractable with existing oil-field skills rather than an exotic research program. That shift in framing has attracted capital, partnerships, and talent from the conventional energy sector toward geothermal.

Latimer has received external recognition for this work: he has been named to Forbes 30 Under 30 in Energy and to MIT Technology Review's Innovators Under 35 list.
