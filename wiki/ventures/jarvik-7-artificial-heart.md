## Summary

The Jarvik-7 was the first total artificial heart to sustain a human patient. It was developed at the University of Utah's Division of Artificial Organs by Dr. Robert Jarvik, under the direction of Dr. Willem Kolff (the Dutch-born inventor of dialysis), and first implanted on December 2, 1982 by surgeon Dr. William DeVries into Barney Clark, a Seattle dentist with terminal cardiomyopathy. Clark survived 112 days. The device was a polyurethane-and-aluminum pneumatic pump driven by an external 375-pound air compressor; descendant devices, including the SynCardia Total Artificial Heart, remain in clinical use today as bridges to transplant.

For the Great Work Utah wiki, the Jarvik-7 is the cleanest single example of Utah-led medicine attempting something nobody had done before — and getting it far enough into the clinic that the rest of the field had to follow.

## Impact

Before the Jarvik-7, total artificial heart replacement was theoretical. Clark's implant proved that a human could survive with a completely mechanical pump in place of a biological heart, even briefly, and opened the clinical, regulatory, and engineering path for everything that followed: ventricular assist devices, the CardioWest TAH (a direct Jarvik-7 descendant), and the bridge-to-transplant systems now standard of care in end-stage heart failure.

The second-order impact mattered just as much: the program established Utah as a global center of artificial-organ research, laid the institutional groundwork for the University of Utah's later medical-device culture, and produced a generation of bioengineers who went on to seed companies and labs across the country.

## What They Built

The Jarvik-7 itself was an apparatus: two ventricles of polyurethane and aluminum, sutured to the patient's atria and great vessels, driven by air pulses from an external compressor through transcutaneous tubes. It was bulky, loud, and tethered. It was also enough to keep Barney Clark alive for 112 days, William Schroeder for 620 days, and several others for clinically meaningful spans.

The deeper thing being built was the institution around it: Kolff's Division of Artificial Organs, the surgical team, the FDA path for a Class III implantable device that didn't yet have a category, and the long argument — still ongoing — about what counts as quality of life on a mechanical heart.

## What Followed

Direct descendants include the SynCardia Total Artificial Heart (still implanted today), HeartMate and HeartWare ventricular assist devices, and the AbioCor and CARMAT fully-implantable artificial hearts. The Jarvik-7 is in the Smithsonian's collection. Robert Jarvik went on to develop the Jarvik 2000 FlowMaker, an axial-flow ventricular assist device. Willem Kolff's broader artificial-organ program at the University of Utah seeded much of the medical-device cluster that still anchors the Wasatch Front.

For talent today, the Jarvik-7 story is more relevant as a model than as a current employer: it's the proof that Utah is a place where world-firsts in medicine can happen, and where regulatory-heavy implantable-device work has institutional memory.

## Utah Context

The Jarvik-7 is the foundational story of the University of Utah's medical-device ecosystem. The Division of Artificial Organs no longer exists as such, but the lineage runs through [Merit Medical](merit-medical.md), [Blackrock Neurotech](blackrock-neurotech.md), the Sorenson medical-device cluster, and the broader Salt Lake City interventional-device industry.
