## Summary

WAVE (Wireless Advanced Vehicle Electrification) is a Logan, Utah company that builds wireless inductive charging infrastructure for commercial EV fleets — primarily transit buses, port vehicles, and logistics trucks. Founded in 2011 as a spinout from Utah State University's Energy Dynamics Laboratory, WAVE embeds high-power charging pads under roadway surfaces at bus layover points, allowing electric buses to charge automatically whenever they stop without driver action, cables, or dedicated charging bays.

WAVE's first commercial deployment was on the University of Utah campus bus system in 2013. The company now appears to have operational deployments in transit, port, and logistics settings, though specific scale data is not publicly detailed.

## Impact

The main barrier to electrifying commercial bus and truck fleets is not range or battery cost — it is charging logistics. Operators resist electrification when it requires parking in specific bays, waiting for drivers to plug and unplug, and maintaining scheduling flexibility around long charge cycles. WAVE's approach reduces charging to an automatic stop behavior: the bus pulls into a layover position, and charging happens without any human action required.

If this infrastructure model scales, it removes one of the primary operational objections to fleet electrification in transit — a sector that accounts for a meaningful portion of urban transportation emissions and one where climate pressure has been building. The counterfactual: without WAVE-style solutions, fleet operators defer electrification or adopt it partially, preserving diesel operations longer.

## What They Are Building

- **Wireless charging pads** — embedded roadway infrastructure delivering 125–500kW+ of power across a 6–10 inch air gap at 90%+ transfer efficiency
- **Fleet-compatible bus charging** — systems designed for transit agency operational requirements, including roadway durability and safety standards for human proximity
- **Port and logistics solutions** — extended product line beyond transit buses
- **Software and data layer** — monitoring and management systems for charging infrastructure operators

The core technical challenge is transferring high power across an air gap with high efficiency while meeting FCC emissions standards, roadway durability requirements, and safety requirements near humans. USU's Energy Dynamics Laboratory developed the foundational power electronics; WAVE has been commercializing and scaling that work since 2011.

## What They Need Now

Likely ongoing needs include power electronics engineers, embedded systems engineers, and RF/EMC engineers, as well as infrastructure project managers and fleet electrification specialists. Logan, UT base offers lower cost of living than coastal counterparts; USU's engineering programs provide an adjacent talent pipeline.

Funding profile appears to combine government grants (FTA, DOE) from early years with customer revenue — no disclosed VC rounds in public databases. This profile suggests meaningful mission autonomy, aligning the revenue model with actual deployment rather than exit-driven growth.

## Who Could Help

Transit agencies, port operators, and logistics companies are WAVE's primary customers. Key government relationships include FTA and DOE, which provided early grant funding. USU and its Energy Dynamics Laboratory remain connected to the company. State departments of transportation (including Utah's) are natural deployment partners.

## Utah Context

WAVE is a clean example of the USU-to-commercial spinout trajectory: foundational research at the Energy Dynamics Laboratory became a commercially deployed product within a few years, and the company remained based in Logan. The University of Utah campus bus system deployment in 2013 gave WAVE one of its earliest operational proving grounds.

For contributors interested in the infrastructure layer of transportation electrification who want to stay in Utah, WAVE is one of the more established companies operating in this space from a Utah base.
